# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 14 – Tying It All Together: Don’t Let the Bedbugs Bite! + Unit Synthesis & Critical Reflection

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains task guidance, scoring criteria, and reflection supports.

## Scripture Focus

**Colossians 1:16-17**

Emphasize that synthesis honors the One in whom all things hold together. Integrating evidence carefully is an act of humility and gratitude.

Say:
> “Today you are not just listing facts. You are combining evidence into a coherent explanation. That kind of careful synthesis reflects wisdom and integrity.”

## Teacher Objectives

- Complete a performance task synthesizing bedbug resistance.
- Integrate Chapter 15 mechanisms into a coherent explanation.
- Distinguish evidence, mechanism, and broader inference.
- Reflect critically on what the case supports and what it does not support by itself.

## Background for the Teacher

The bedbug case is a strong example of **mutation plus natural selection**.

- Mutation introduces variation.
- Some mutations confer pesticide resistance.
- Pesticide use creates strong selection pressure.
- Resistant individuals survive and reproduce more successfully.
- Allele frequencies change over time.

Important framing:

Say:
> “This is a real and well-documented case of adaptation in a population. It is powerful evidence for measurable microevolutionary change. Students should avoid overclaiming beyond what the case directly shows.”

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- “Evidence synthesis builds discernment.”
- “God’s sustaining order is visible in the way populations respond predictably to pressure.”
- “Faithfulness includes truthful explanation—not exaggeration.”

## Materials & Preparation

- Printed performance task directions.
- Rubric with claim, evidence, mechanism, and reflection categories.
- Chapter 15 organizer or planner for vocabulary and concepts.
- Optional peer-feedback checklist.

## Bell Work (5 min)

**Prompt:** Name one mechanism of change besides natural selection.

Acceptable answers:
- genetic drift
- gene flow
- mutation
- sexual selection

**Scoring:** 5 pts for effort and relevance.

## Lesson Delivery (70 min)

### 1. Task Launch (10 min)

Review the directions and rubric.

Say:
> “Your goal is to explain bedbug resistance clearly. Your claim should match your evidence, and your mechanism should explain how the change happened.”

Required elements:
- a clear claim
- at least two pieces of evidence
- explanation of mechanism
- short critical reflection on what the case can and cannot show

### 2. Independent Work (35 min)

Students complete the task independently.

**Strong claim model:**
> “Bedbug populations became more resistant to pesticides because resistant individuals were more likely to survive treatment and reproduce, increasing the frequency of resistance-related alleles over time.”

**Evidence examples:**
- population resistance increased after repeated pesticide exposure
- susceptible bedbugs died at higher rates
- resistant lineages became more common over generations

**Mechanism explanation model:**
> “Mutation produced variation, including resistance traits. Pesticide exposure then selected for resistant individuals. Because those individuals survived and reproduced more often, the resistance allele increased in frequency.”

### 3. Peer Feedback (15 min)

Students exchange work and check:
- Does the claim match the evidence?
- Is the mechanism scientifically accurate?
- Does the reflection distinguish observation from larger inference?

### 4. Unit Reflection (10 min)

Prompt:
> “What does the bedbug case strongly support, and what broader claim would still require additional evidence?”

**Model answer:**
> “The bedbug case strongly supports observable adaptation and allele frequency change in response to selection pressure. Broader claims about large-scale evolutionary history require additional evidence and cannot be established by this single case alone.”

## Homework Grading

- **Final task submission** — 15 pts
- **Critical reflection paragraph** — 5 pts

**Rubric:**
- Evidence integration: 50%
- Mechanism accuracy: 30%
- Reflection depth: 20%

## Common Confusions and Fixes

- **“This one case proves all macroevolutionary claims.”**  
  Fix: “No. It is a strong microevolution example. Larger historical claims require more evidence.”
- **“Resistance happens because bedbugs try to adapt.”**  
  Fix: “No. Mutation generates variation; selection changes frequency.”
- **“Mutation and selection are the same thing.”**  
  Fix: “Mutation introduces variation. Selection acts on variation.”

## Differentiation

- **Struggling learners:** provide sentence starters for claim, evidence, and mechanism.
- **Advanced learners:** ask them to compare the bedbug case to the deer mouse case from Day 13.
- **ELL support:** offer a vocabulary bank with resistance, mutation, frequency, selection, survive, reproduce.

## Prayer Close

> “Lord, guide our synthesis toward truth. Help us explain clearly, think humbly, and honor You in the way we handle evidence.”
