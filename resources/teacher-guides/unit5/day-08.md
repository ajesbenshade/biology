# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 8 – Chapter 15 Opener: Don't Let the Bedbugs Bite

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Psalm 104:24**
> "How many are your works, Lord! In wisdom you made them all; the earth is full of your creatures."

Read aloud at the start. Tie in:

Say:
> "Today we begin Chapter 15 by looking at one of the clearest, most directly observed examples of evolutionary change in modern biology: pesticide resistance in bedbugs. This is observable, measurable, and real — and it's also a perfect case study in the distinction we've practiced all unit. What we observe directly, and what we infer beyond it."

Psalm 104:24 frames the bedbug alongside every other creature as evidence of God's creative wisdom. Even a bedbug — irritating as it is — is an organism designed with remarkable biochemical complexity. Understanding how populations change under selection pressure is part of understanding the sustaining order God built into creation.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Students can explain the mechanism by which a beneficial mutation increases in frequency under selection pressure, using specific steps.
- Students can distinguish the observation (allele frequency change in a population over generations) from interpretation (the claim about mechanism, timescale, and extrapolation).
- Students can recognize pesticide resistance as a textbook example of microevolution — observed, measured, and documentable in real time.
- Students can articulate why pesticide resistance is *not* evidence that populations can produce radically new body plans given enough time.

**Background for you:**

**The bedbug case (Cimex lectularius):** Bedbugs were nearly eliminated in the U.S. by DDT in the mid-20th century. They rebounded dramatically after DDT was banned (1972) — not because DDT-resistant bedbugs evolved after the ban, but because DDT-resistant variants *already existed* in the population at low frequency. When DDT was applied, the non-resistant bedbugs died and the resistant variants survived and reproduced, rapidly increasing in frequency. By the time DDT was banned, resistant variants dominated the population. The resistance mechanism: mutations in the gene encoding the sodium channel protein in neuronal membranes (the kdr mutation — knockdown resistance). DDT works by keeping sodium channels open, causing uncontrolled nerve firing. The kdr mutation changes the channel protein shape so DDT cannot bind effectively. This is measurable — researchers can sequence the sodium channel gene across resistant and non-resistant populations.

**Why this is microevolution:** Bedbugs with kdr mutation are still bedbugs. The gene that changed is one allele of one gene — a modification to an existing protein. No new body plan, no new metabolic pathway, no new organ system. The frequency of an existing variant increased under selection. This is what microevolution means: observable allele frequency change within a population. It does not demonstrate that the same mechanism, given enough time, produces new phyla or body plans. That extrapolation is the macroevolutionary claim — inference, not direct observation.

**The simulation logic:** The population simulation models this mathematically. Start with a population in which 2% of individuals carry the kdr allele. Apply "pesticide pressure" (remove all individuals without the kdr allele each generation — they die). Track frequency across generations. Within 5–10 generations (bedbugs reproduce rapidly; ~3–4 generations per year), the kdr allele dominates the population. Students graph this and observe: the change is rapid, measurable, and mechanically explained by differential survival.

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Pesticide resistance is a real, observed example of God's design at work — populations carry built-in genetic variation, and that variation allows them to respond to environmental pressure. This is not random; it's the expression of pre-existing genetic diversity."
- "Notice: the resistant bedbugs were already in the population before DDT was applied. Selection did not *create* the resistance — it *revealed* pre-existing variation. That is consistent with a creation framework in which kinds were designed with built-in variation."
- "Rapid adaptation shows God's sustaining design: life is not static but responsive — within the boundaries of the kind. Bedbugs adapting to survive pesticides are still bedbugs."
- "We can observe this change directly. That's the power and the limit of the case study — we can firmly conclude 'allele frequency changed under selection pressure.' What we cannot conclude from this case alone is 'given enough time, the same process produces new body plans.'"

## Materials & Preparation

- **Bedbug image/projector:** A close-up microscope or scanning electron microscope image of *Cimex lectularius*. This gets students engaged immediately — bedbugs are genuinely striking under magnification.
- **Population simulation sheets (one per student):** Two-column table — Generation | Number of Resistant Individuals (out of 100). Parameters: Start = 2 resistant; each generation, all non-resistant die; resistant individuals produce offspring at the same rate as the original population. Students run 10 generations and graph the result.
- **Simplified variant:** Use M&Ms or colored counters. Start with 2 "resistant" (red) and 98 "non-resistant" (another color). Each round, remove all non-resistant. Each resistant individual "reproduces" to restore population to 100 (doubling as needed). Concrete and memorable.
- **Case narrative handout:** One-page summary of the bedbug DDT resistance case. Include: (1) timeline of DDT use and banning, (2) bedbug population crash and rebound data, (3) brief description of kdr mutation and sodium channel mechanism, (4) a CER template at the bottom.

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Question / Prompt:** "Predict: Why might a pesticide that worked perfectly the first time you used it gradually stop working?"

| Level | Answer |
|-------|--------|
| Basic | "The bugs get used to it over time." |
| Good | "Some bugs were already resistant and survived. Their offspring inherited resistance, so resistant bugs became more common." |
| Strong | "If a population naturally contains some genetic variation, and if one variant (a resistance allele) allows survival when pesticide is applied, those resistant individuals will survive and reproduce while non-resistant individuals die. Over generations, the resistance allele increases in frequency — not because the pesticide caused the mutation, but because it eliminated the individuals that lacked it, leaving resistant variants to dominate." |

**Important biology to watch for:** Students often say "the bugs adapted" as if individual bugs changed during their lifetime. Correct this immediately — evolution acts on populations over generations, not on individuals during their lifetimes. The bug either has the resistance allele or doesn't. Selection determines which bugs reproduce.

**Grading:** 1 pt. Award the point for a population-level explanation that includes allele frequency shift and differential survival or reproduction.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Phenomenon Launch (10 min)

Project the bedbug image. Ask:

> "What do you notice about this organism?" (Structure, appearance, segmentation, mouthparts.)

> "Bedbugs were nearly eliminated in the United States in the 1950s and 1960s. Then DDT was banned in 1972. What happened next?"

If students know: "They came back — massively."

Say:
> "Correct. Bedbugs came roaring back after DDT stopped being used. But here's what's interesting: the bedbugs that came back were almost entirely resistant to DDT — even though DDT hadn't been used for years. Why would that be?"

Let students puzzle over this for 2 min. Then:

> "The answer involves understanding something fundamental about how selection works on populations. And by the end of class, you'll be able to explain it in biological terms — not just 'they got used to it.'"

### 2. Case Narrative Read + CER Annotation (20 min)

Distribute the case narrative. Students read independently (8 min), then annotate:
- Circle every **directly measured observation** (biting rates, allele frequencies, population counts).
- Underline every **mechanistic claim** (what caused the change).
- Put a question mark next to any **extrapolation beyond what is directly measured** in this case.

After annotation (5 min debrief): What did you mark? Students share one of each.

**CER — Class models together on board:**

- **Claim:** "Bedbug populations developed resistance to DDT through natural selection acting on pre-existing genetic variation."
- **Evidence:** "Genetic sequencing of resistant vs. non-resistant bedbug populations shows that the kdr mutation (a specific change in the sodium channel gene) is present at high frequency in resistant populations and low or absent frequency in non-resistant populations. DDT works by binding to the sodium channel — kdr changes the channel shape so DDT cannot bind. Directly measured: kdr allele frequency in field populations."
- **Reasoning:** "Bedbugs with the kdr allele survived DDT application; those without it died. Survivors reproduced, passing the kdr allele to offspring. Over generations, kdr frequency increased — a direct consequence of differential reproduction under selection pressure. This is measurable, mechanically explained, and repeatable."

Say after writing this on board:

> "Now mark this CER: which sentences are directly measured? Which require mechanistic inference? Which require any assumptions beyond this specific case?"

- Directly measured: kdr frequency in populations before/after selection, DDT-binding mechanism.
- Mechanistic inference: the causal chain (kdr → survival → reproduction → frequency shift) — highly supported by the direct measurements, but the causal link is inferred from the correlation.
- Extrapolation: any claim that "this shows new species arise by natural selection" — that goes beyond this case entirely.

### 3. Population Simulation (25 min)

Distribute simulation sheets (or M&Ms).

**Setup:**
- Population = 100 individuals
- Generation 0: 2 resistant (kdr/kdr or kdr/+), 98 non-resistant (+/+)
- Rule: After pesticide application, all non-resistant die. Resistant individuals reproduce to bring population back to 100.

**Students work through 10 generations, filling in the table and graphing.**

**Expected results (simplified — actual would involve Hardy-Weinberg):**

| Generation | Resistant | Non-Resistant |
|-----------|----------|--------------|
| 0 | 2 | 98 |
| 1 | 4 | 96 |
| 2 | 8 | 92 |
| 3 | 16 | 84 |
| 4 | 32 | 68 |
| 5 | 64 | 36 |
| 6 | 100 | 0 |

*Note: This is a simplified model (100% selection for one allele). Real populations show more gradual S-curve allele frequency shifts due to heterozygotes and population structure. The simplified model illustrates the directional principle clearly.*

Say mid-simulation:
> "Notice how fast this happens. Bedbugs reproduce 3–4 times per year. Within 2 years of DDT application, resistant variants could dominate a population. This is not a slow, imperceptible process — it's measurable in real time."

After graphs are completed:

Say:
> "Now here is the key interpretive question. You just showed, mathematically, that a resistance allele can go from 2% to 100% of a population in six simulated generations. What did this model assume that isn't necessarily true in real populations?"

Expected student answers:
- 100% of non-resistant individuals die (real selection is rarely absolute).
- No new non-resistant individuals immigrate from other populations.
- Resistant individuals have exactly the same reproduction rate as non-resistant ones before selection.

Say:
> "Right — and those are real limitations. The model simplifies. Now here is the bigger question: Does this simulation show that natural selection can produce new body plans given enough time?"

Expected answer: No — because the simulation only shows *allele frequency change within a population*, not the creation of new alleles, new genes, or new body plans. The resistant individuals are still bedbugs with the same anatomy, the same organ systems, the same life cycle. One allele of one gene changed in frequency.

Say:
> "Microevolution — allele frequency change within populations — is what this case demonstrates. That it happens is not in scientific dispute. Whether the same mechanism, scaled up over deep time, can produce all the body plan diversity we observe is a separate claim, and it requires different evidence."

### 4. Debrief (10 min)

Class discussion:

- "Observation: What exactly did we observe today? State it in one sentence." (Allele frequency changed over generations under selection pressure; resistant individuals survived and reproduced.)
- "Mechanism: What causal mechanism did we propose? How well does the evidence support it?" (Differential survival → differential reproduction → allele frequency shift. Highly supported by direct measurements of kdr allele in populations.)
- "Extrapolation: What would we need to claim to go from 'pesticide resistance arose by natural selection' to 'all life arose from a common ancestor by natural selection'?" (Different kinds of evidence — transitional fossil sequences, demonstrated mechanisms for producing new information/body plans, time — none of which are demonstrated by the bedbug case alone.)

Close:
> "The bedbug case is powerful evidence for microevolution — observable, measurable, mechanically explained. That's exactly what good science looks like. The question of whether the same mechanism explains large-scale historical change across all life is a bigger claim that requires bigger evidence. We don't have to resolve that today. We do have to be honest about what this case shows and what it doesn't."

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** State one directly observed fact from today's case and one interpretation or extrapolation that goes beyond what the bedbug evidence alone can show.

**Model answer:**
- "Directly observed: The kdr allele (a specific mutation in the sodium channel gene) is found at high frequency in DDT-resistant bedbug populations and at low or absent frequency in non-resistant populations. This is directly measured by genetic sequencing."
- "Beyond the evidence: 'Natural selection acting on random mutations can produce new body plans, new organ systems, and new phyla given sufficient time.' The bedbug case shows allele frequency change in one gene; whether that mechanism scales to produce major body plan innovation is an extrapolation that this case alone does not demonstrate."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for: one specific directly measured observation + one specific extrapolation beyond the case with explanation of why it goes beyond. Deduct 2 pts if "observation" is actually the interpretation (e.g., "natural selection happened" — that's the mechanism inferred from the data, not the raw observation).

## Homework

Homework is practice only. Check for completion and give feedback, but do not record points.

| Assignment | Suggested completion look-fors |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Chapter 15 opening questions (textbook) | Accurate biological mechanisms; correct use of allele frequency terminology; no individual-adaptation errors |
| Reflection short responses (observation vs. assumption) | Each response correctly identifies the observed component and the assumed/inferred component of the claim; no conflation |

**Strong reflection response example:**

*Question:* "Is pesticide resistance evidence that populations evolve?"
> "Yes — with precision. 'Evolve' means 'change in allele frequencies over time.' In that precise sense, pesticide resistance is direct evidence: the kdr allele frequency measurably increased over documented generations under DDT selection. This is observed. What requires additional assumption is the further claim that the same mechanism, given enough time, produces radically different body plans. The bedbug case supports the first claim directly; the second requires additional evidence about mechanisms that produce new genetic information and new developmental pathways — evidence not present in this case."

**Suggested feedback focus:**
- Observation specificity: Names specific measured data (allele frequency, kdr mutation, sequencing evidence)
- Assumption identification: Names what is assumed beyond the observation and explains why it requires additional evidence

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Individual bugs adapted to DDT" | "No individual organism evolves during its lifetime. The individual either has the kdr allele or doesn't — that was set at birth. What changed was the *population's* allele frequency — because resistant individuals survived and non-resistant individuals died. Evolution is a population-level phenomenon." |
| "Resistance = new trait created by DDT" | "DDT did not create the resistance mutation. The kdr mutation already existed at low frequency in the population before DDT was applied. DDT revealed and selected for it — it eliminated the non-resistant individuals, leaving the resistant ones to dominate. Selection acts on existing variation; it doesn't generate mutations." |
| "This proves all organisms share a common ancestor" | "Pesticide resistance demonstrates microevolution — allele frequency change within a population of one species. Demonstrating macroevolution — that the same mechanism, over deep time, produces all life's diversity — requires evidence of a different kind: mechanisms for producing new genetic information, transitional forms across major body plans, etc." |
| "If evolution is true in bedbugs, Christianity is false" | "Observable allele frequency change in bedbug populations is scientific data. Whether God works through natural processes to accomplish His purposes — like variation within created kinds — is a theological question that many thoughtful Christians answer differently. The bedbug data doesn't determine that larger question. What it shows is specifically and only that allele frequencies change under selection." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Research Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium. Under what conditions does allele frequency *not* change? How does the bedbug scenario violate Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium? What factors drive the departure? Write a one-page analysis connecting Hardy-Weinberg conditions to the bedbug simulation results.
- **Struggling learners:** Provide a pre-made graph template with labeled axes (Generation | Percent Resistant) and the first two data points plotted. Students complete the graph for Generations 2–10 using their simulation table, then describe the trend in one sentence.
- **ELL support:** Vocabulary card: allele, frequency, mutation, natural selection, resistance, kdr allele, sodium channel. Illustrated diagram of the selection process: Generation 0 (mixed population) → pesticide applied → resistant survive → Generation 1 (more resistant). Visual timeline.
