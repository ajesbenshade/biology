# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 2 – Microevolution, Macroevolution, and Cladistics

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Colossians 1:16-17 / Genesis 1:21**
> "All things were created through him and for him… in him all things hold together." (Col. 1:16-17)
> "So God created the great creatures of the sea and every living thing with which the water teems… according to their kinds." (Gen. 1:21)

Genesis 1 describes God creating distinct kinds with built-in diversity. Colossians 1 frames all of creation as sustained by Christ. The goal today is not to pit these verses against cladistics—it is to show that observing shared traits and building branching diagrams is legitimate scientific work. Where worldview matters is in the interpretation of *what those branches mean about history*.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Define microevolution (observable, measured allele/trait frequency changes within populations) and macroevolution (historical narrative about large-scale ancestry across major groups).
- Sort the five lines of evolutionary evidence into their correct categories.
- Build and interpret a simple cladogram from a trait matrix, correctly labeling what is data versus what is inference.

**Background for you:** These two terms are often conflated. Microevolution is well-documented and uncontroversial: bacteria develop antibiotic resistance; owl populations in polluted forests show color-frequency shifts; dog breeding produces rapid morphological change. All of these are observed. Macroevolution—the claim that fish-to-amphibian-to-reptile-to-mammal transitions occurred over deep time—is a historical interpretation built on the same mechanisms extrapolated. Students need to know the difference.

A cladogram is a branching diagram that groups organisms by shared derived traits. It is a data visualization tool—the branches are determined by the trait matrix. Whether those branches represent real ancestral relationships is an interpretation built on the assumption of common ancestry. The diagram itself is legitimate either way: it organizes data. What you claim the diagram *proves about history* is where the worldview difference appears.

---

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Observable changes in populations—microevolution—show God's wisdom in designing life with adaptable built-in variation. A dog breed changing body size is real, measured, and not in dispute."
- "Macroevolutionary claims extrapolate mechanisms beyond what is directly observed. That extrapolation is a historical interpretation—not a laboratory measurement."
- "Cladistics is a legitimate tool for organizing comparative data. What we claim the diagram *means about history* is where interpretation enters."

## Materials & Preparation

- 20 printed evidence cards (sorted in advance by teacher into five categories for the answer key below; shuffled for students). Each card should describe one piece of evidence with a brief description.
- Six-organism trait matrix handout (see template below).
- Blank cladogram template (branching tree with unlabeled nodes).
- Colossians 1:16-17 and Genesis 1:21 printed or displayed.

## Five Evidence Categories + Sample Card Sort Answer Key

**Fossil evidence:** Physical remains or impressions of past organisms in rock layers.
- Examples: Trilobite found in Cambrian strata; transitional whale ancestor (Pakicetus) with limbs; series of horse fossils with size changes.

**Anatomical (structural) evidence:** Shared physical structures across species.
- Examples: Pentadactyl limb (5-digit structure in human hand, bat wing, whale flipper, horse leg); vestigial structures like the human coccyx.

**Embryological evidence:** Similarities in early embryonic development stages.
- Examples: Pharyngeal pouches in fish, bird, and human embryos during development; similar early-stage body plans across vertebrates.

**Molecular evidence:** Similarities in DNA, RNA, or protein sequences.
- Examples: Cytochrome c protein sequence similarity percentages; mitochondrial DNA comparison across primates; universal genetic code.

**Biogeographical evidence:** Distribution of species and fossils across geographic regions.
- Examples: Marsupials concentrated in Australia and South America (separated by ocean); island species more similar to nearest mainland species than to species on other islands with similar climates.

**Answer key for a 20-card sort (adjust to your printed cards):**
- Fossil: 4 cards
- Anatomical: 4 cards
- Embryological: 4 cards
- Molecular: 4 cards
- Biogeographical: 4 cards

## Cladogram Build — Template and Answer Key (30 min)

**Six-organism trait matrix:**

| Organism | Has backbone | Has hair/fur | Produces milk | Has amniotic egg | Has feathers |
|---------|-------------|-------------|--------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Lamprey (jawless fish) | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Salmon | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Frog | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |
| Turtle | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | ✗ |
| Eagle | ✓ | ✗ | ✗ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Dog | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ |

**Reading the matrix to build the cladogram:**
1. All organisms share "has backbone" → this is the outgroup trait shared by all; place all on one trunk.
2. Amniotic egg separates turtle/eagle/dog from lamprey/salmon/frog → first branch point.
3. Feathers separate eagle from turtle and dog → second branch point within amniotes.
4. Hair/fur + milk separate dog from turtle → third branch point.

**Resulting cladogram structure (text representation):**
```
Root
├── Lamprey
├── Salmon
├── Frog
└── [Amniote ancestor node]
    ├── Turtle
    ├── Eagle (+ feathers)
    └── Dog (+ hair, milk)
```

**Student annotation task:** For each branch point, label:
- (O) = "Observed trait present in specimens" (e.g., feathers measured on eagle specimens)
- (I) = "Inferred relationship" (e.g., "eagle and turtle share a common amniote ancestor")

**Expected labeling:** Trait presence at each node = (O); the node itself (implying a historical ancestor) = (I).

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Prompt:** Show one evidence card. Ask students to identify which of the five categories it belongs to and explain why.

**Grading:** 1 pt for correct category + one-sentence justification. Award the point for any well-reasoned categorization with justification.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Definitions + Card Sort (15 min)

Write on board:
- **Microevolution:** Measured change in allele or trait frequencies within a population over time. *Directly observable.*
- **Macroevolution:** Large-scale historical pattern of ancestry across major groups. *Inferred from multiple evidence types.*

Say:
> "Neither definition is a value judgment. Microevolution is simply what we can document with data. You can watch it happen in a lab with bacteria or in pepper moth populations. Macroevolution is the larger story that evolutionary biology tells using that data plus additional assumptions. Our job this unit is to stay clear about which claims belong to which category."

Groups sort evidence cards. After 10 min, share answers. Correct any misplacements—common errors:
- Putting protein sequence similarity in "anatomical" → it belongs in "molecular."
- Confusing embryological with anatomical → embryological = *developmental stages*, not adult structures.

### 2. Cladogram Build (30 min)

Distribute the trait matrix. Walk through filling it out together for the first two organisms, then groups complete the rest.

Say:
> "A cladogram is a tool for organizing comparative data. You're grouping organisms by which derived traits they share. The branch points represent 'these organisms have this trait in common.' That's the data part."

After groups build their cladograms:

Say:
> "Now here's the interpretive question: This branch point here—this node—represents what, exactly? In evolutionary biology, it represents a hypothetical common ancestor. Is that observation or inference?"

Expected response: Inference—we infer the ancestor existed; we didn't observe it.

Say:
> "Right. The trait data is observed. The ancestor implied by the node is inferred. That's the key distinction—and it holds whether you accept the evolutionary framework or not. The cladogram organizes data. What you say the data *means about history* is interpretation."

### 3. Interpretation Check (15 min)

Students annotate their cladograms using (O) and (I) labels. Circulate and prompt: "Is this something someone measured? Or something the model assumes?"

Whole-class debrief: Which conclusions are most direct? Which are most assumptive?

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Write one data-based conclusion and one inference from today's cladogram.

**Model answer:** 
- Data-based: "The eagle has feathers—this is a directly observed trait used to place it in its position on the cladogram."
- Inference: "Eagles and turtles share a common amniote ancestor—this is inferred from shared derived traits (amniotic egg), not from observing the ancestor directly."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for one accurate (O) and one accurate (I), each labeled correctly.

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Cladogram proves evolution" | "The cladogram organizes shared-trait data. The ancestor implied by each node is inferred—it requires the assumption of common ancestry. The diagram is valid either way; what you say it *proves about history* is interpretation." |
| "Microevolution eventually becomes macroevolution given enough time" | "That's the extrapolation claim. It may be true—but it's not the same as observing it happen. We can test the mechanism at the micro level; the deep-time extrapolation is not directly testable in the same way." |
| "Biblical kinds means no change ever" | "No—Genesis says God created according to kinds. Variation within those kinds (size, color, behavior) is built-in and observable. The biblical question is whether new fundamental body plans arise from unguided processes, not whether populations change." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Add a row to the cladogram for a new organism of their choice, find its traits, place it on the diagram, and argue which branch point placement is most justified by trait data. Label assumptions required.
- **Struggling learners:** Pre-filled trait matrix with three organisms already placed; students complete the remaining three. Provide a completed sample cladogram as a model for their annotation task.
- **ELL support:** Evidence category cards with images and bilingual labels; cladogram with labeled sample (O) and (I) annotations as a model.
