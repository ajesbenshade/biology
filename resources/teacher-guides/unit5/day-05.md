# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 5 – Embryological, Anatomical, and Genetic Evidence

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

**Psalm 111:2 / Romans 1:20**
> "Great are the works of the Lord; they are pondered by all who delight in them." (Psalm 111:2)
> "For since the creation of the world God's invisible qualities—his eternal power and divine nature—have been clearly seen, being understood from what has been made." (Romans 1:20)

Read both aloud at the start. Tie in:

Say:
> "Today we examine three lines of evidence that biologists cite when building the case for evolutionary relationships: the shared developmental stages of embryos, the shared structural plans of anatomical features, and the similarity percentages we find when we compare DNA or protein sequences. Our job is what it always is — to look carefully at what is actually measured and what is assumed, and to be honest about both."

These patterns are genuinely striking and worth studying seriously. They do reveal God's orderly wisdom in design. Whether they require a common-ancestry explanation or are equally consistent with a common-design explanation is an interpretive question — and student thinking is sharpened by engaging it honestly.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Students can compare vertebrate embryo stages, correctly identifying which features are shared early and which diverge later.
- Students can classify biological structures as homologous, analogous, or vestigial using specific trait evidence.
- Students can read a molecular similarity table, calculate percentages, and identify what the data shows and what it does not prove on its own.

**Background for you:**

**Embryology:** All vertebrate embryos pass through a stage when pharyngeal (throat) arches are present. In fish these develop into gill structures; in humans they develop into structures of the ear, jaw, and neck. The fact that such similar structures arise in radically different adult organisms has historically been cited as strong evidence for common ancestry. Common-design biologists note that this could equally reflect a shared developmental toolkit — God using efficient developmental pathways across different body plans. The data (shared early stages, divergent later stages) is real; the interpretation differs.

**Homologous vs. analogous:** Homologous structures share the same underlying skeletal or biochemical framework but may differ dramatically in function (human arm, bat wing, whale flipper, horse foreleg — all built on the same humerus-radius-ulna-carpals-phalanges plan). Analogous structures share a similar function but are built differently (bird wing and insect wing are both flight structures, but their internal frameworks are completely different). The pentadactyl limb as a homologous structure is one of the strongest anatomical evidence lines for vertebrate relatedness — or, from a design perspective, for a common blueprint.

**Vestigial structures:** This is the most contested of the three. Classic examples like the human appendix and the coccyx are often presented as "useless leftovers from ancestors." However, the appendix has known immune functions (it maintains gut flora populations), and the coccyx is an attachment point for important pelvic floor muscles. "Vestigial" should be defined carefully as *reduced in the original function compared to a proposed ancestral version* — not necessarily as *functionless*. Students who hear the appendix called "useless" and know it has a function will dismiss the entire argument — so accuracy here is important for credibility.

**Molecular evidence:** DNA and protein sequence comparisons produce measurable similarity percentages. Cytochrome c (a protein involved in cellular respiration) is remarkably similar across organisms — humans and chimpanzees share ~99% similarity; humans and yeast share ~60–70%. Molecular biologists interpret this as evidence of common descent with selective conservation of a crucial functional protein. Common-design biologists note that a functionally constrained protein (cytochrome c performs the same cellular task in all aerobic organisms) would be expected to have similar sequences regardless of ancestry — the function dictates the sequence, not the history.

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Repeated structural patterns — the pentadactyl limb appearing in bats, whales, humans, and horses — reveal the Creator's elegant consistency. Similar design blueprints produce similar structural solutions."
- When classifying structures: "We classify based on what we can observe and measure. Whether that classification also tells us about ancestry or about design is an interpretive step — and honest science keeps those two steps separate."
- On vestigial structures: "Calling something 'vestigial' or 'useless' is a claim that requires evidence beyond 'I don't know what it does.' Appendices and coccyges have known functions; intellectual honesty requires updating our claims when we discover function."
- On molecular similarity: "Functional constraint is a real explanation for why crucial proteins are highly conserved. Both 'same ancestor' and 'same Creator using the same molecular tool' predict high similarity in essential proteins."

## Materials & Preparation

- **Structure cards (12):** Print and cut one set per group. Suggested pairs:
  1. Human arm / whale flipper (homologous)
  2. Human arm / bat wing (homologous)
  3. Bird wing / insect wing (analogous)
  4. Dolphin fin / shark fin (analogous — common function, different internal structure)
  5. Human coccyx (vestigial — with note that it has muscle attachment function)
  6. Python pelvis remnants (vestigial — reduced hind-limb bones visible in X-rays)
  7. Whale flipper / bat wing (homologous)
  8. Horse foreleg / human arm (homologous)
  9. Cactus spines / rose thorns (analogous — both defensive, different tissue origin)
  10. Human appendix (vestigial — reduced cecal function, but immune role documented)
  11. Mole foreleg / human arm (homologous — deep burrowing mole has same pentadactyl plan)
  12. Butterfly wing / bird wing (analogous)
- **Embryo diagram handout:** Four stages of development for fish, frog, chicken, and human embryos. Stage 1 should show the strong pharyngeal arch similarity; later stages should show clear divergence. Use textbook figures or public-domain images. Pre-label axes: "Early Stage" and "Later Stage."
- **Molecular similarity table:** A short dataset (5–6 organisms, 2 proteins: cytochrome c and hemoglobin). Percentage similarity relative to humans. Example values:
  - Chimpanzee: cytochrome c 99%, hemoglobin 99%
  - Rhesus monkey: cytochrome c 91%, hemoglobin 93%
  - Dog: cytochrome c 88%, hemoglobin 87%
  - Chicken: cytochrome c 73%, hemoglobin 45%
  - Yeast: cytochrome c 65%, hemoglobin N/A
- **CER template:** Claim / Evidence / Reasoning (three-column format with space for two CERs per student).

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Question:** "What does 'homologous' mean? Give one example."

| Level | Answer |
|-------|--------|
| Basic | "Similar structures from a common origin — like a human arm and a whale flipper." |
| Good | "Structures that share the same underlying framework even if they serve different functions — e.g., the pentadactyl limb in vertebrates." |
| Strong | "Homologous structures share the same skeletal or biochemical plan — wing of a bat, arm of a human, flipper of a whale — all built on the same humerus-radius-ulna bones. The evidence is the observable structural similarity; whether that similarity reflects common ancestry or common design is an interpretive step." |

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for definition + one accurate example. Deduct 2 pts for examples that are actually analogous (e.g., bird wing and butterfly wing — these share function, not structure).

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Direct Instruction: Key Terms and Definitions (15 min)

Write on board:

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Homologous** | Same structural plan, potentially different function | Human arm / bat wing / whale flipper |
| **Analogous** | Same function, different structural origin | Bird wing / insect wing |
| **Vestigial** | Reduced in the original function compared to proposed ancestral version | Python pelvic remnants |

Say:
> "When we classify a structure, we're using measurable evidence — what bones are present, what tissue type is involved, what developmental pathway produced it. That's observation. When we go further and say 'this similarity is caused by common ancestry,' we've made an interpretive step. Both steps are legitimate. Our job is to know which one we're on at any given moment."

Work through one example per category explicitly before the sort:
- **Homologous:** Human arm and bat wing. "Open a vertebrate anatomy textbook. You will find: humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, metacarpals, phalanges — in the same positional sequence in both. That relationship is directly observed. The interpretation is that this reflects either shared ancestry or shared design blueprint."
- **Analogous:** Bird wing and insect wing. "Both are used for flight. But a bird wing is supported by modified bones; an insect wing is an outgrowth of the exoskeleton with no internal bone. Same function — completely different structure. The similarity is functional, not structural."
- **Vestigial:** Python pelvic remnants. "Pythons have small remnant bones in the pelvic region visible in X-rays, positioned where hind limbs would be in lizards. These bones are reduced compared to similar bones in lizards. Whether they represent 'leftovers from a legged ancestor' or 'a design element with minor retained function' is interpretation."

### 2. Structure Sort Activity (20 min)

Groups of 3–4 sort the 12 structure cards into three categories: Homologous / Analogous / Vestigial. For each card, they write one justification sentence.

**Answer key:**

| Card | Category | Justification |
|------|---------|--------------|
| Human arm / whale flipper | Homologous | Same pentadactyl bone sequence in both |
| Human arm / bat wing | Homologous | Same humerus-radius-ulna-phalanges plan |
| Bird wing / insect wing | Analogous | Both for flight; completely different internal structures |
| Dolphin fin / shark fin | Analogous | Similar hydrodynamic shape; cartilage vs. bone internal structure |
| Human coccyx | Vestigial | Reduced from proposed tail-bearing ancestor; retains pelvic muscle attachment |
| Python pelvic remnants | Vestigial | Small remnant bones where hind limbs occur in related lizards |
| Whale flipper / bat wing | Homologous | Same pentadactyl framework |
| Horse foreleg / human arm | Homologous | Same bone sequence; elongated metacarpals in horse |
| Cactus spines / rose thorns | Analogous | Both defensive; spines are modified leaves; thorns are modified stem tissue |
| Human appendix | Vestigial (with caution) | Reduced cecal function; immune role remains; avoid "completely useless" claim |
| Mole foreleg / human arm | Homologous | Same pentadactyl plan; mole's humerus is widened for digging |
| Butterfly wing / bird wing | Analogous | Both flight structures; completely different structural origin |

**Tricky ones to watch:** 
- Cactus spines / rose thorns — students often say "homologous" because both are plants. They are analogous — same defensive function, different tissue of origin.
- Human appendix — accept "vestigial" but prompt: "Is it completely functionless? What do we know about the immune role?" Required correction if students write "useless."

**Debrief (5 min):** Share answers to at least 3 disputed cards. Say:
> "Whenever you classified a card, you used observable, measurable structure. That's solid. Whenever you wrote 'because it evolved from…' in your justification — pause. The classification is observation; the ancestry claim is interpretation. Let's keep those clearly separate."

### 3. Embryology Diagram Annotation (20 min)

Distribute embryo diagram handout.

**Annotation task:** Students label:
- **(S) = Shared feature** at early stages (pharyngeal arches visible in all four organisms at Stage 1)
- **(D) = Diverging feature** at later stages (fish develop gill slits; human/chicken develop ear structures and neck anatomy)
- **(O) = Directly observed** (the arch structures themselves, measurable in embryos)
- **(I) = Inferred** (the claim that shared early stages reflect a shared ancestor)

Say:
> "Look at Stage 1 across the four columns. What do you notice? Yes — all four embryos show remarkably similar pharyngeal arches at this stage. That is directly observed — researchers have measured and photographed these. Now look at Stage 4. The fish has developed gill structures; the human embryo has developed into a very different form. Divergence is also observed."

Say:
> "The interesting interpretive question is: Why do all four embryos share these early stages? Evolutionary biology says: shared ancestry — vertebrates all descended from an aquatic ancestor, and that early developmental pathway was conserved. A common-design interpretation says: God used an efficient shared developmental toolkit early in development; separate kinds express that toolkit differently as development proceeds. Both explanations are consistent with the observed data. The data itself does not select between them."

Prompt if students finish early: "Which of your labels required you to measure something directly? Which required you to know something about history?"

### 4. Molecular Similarity Table Analysis (10 min)

Distribute or display the table. Students:
1. Calculate the average similarity across both proteins for each organism relative to humans.
2. Rank the organisms by average similarity.
3. Answer: Which pieces of data are directly measured? Which conclusions require interpretation?

**Answer key:**

| Organism | Cytochrome c | Hemoglobin | Average |
|---------|-------------|----------|---------|
| Chimpanzee | 99% | 99% | 99.0% |
| Rhesus monkey | 91% | 93% | 92.0% |
| Dog | 88% | 87% | 87.5% |
| Chicken | 73% | 45% | 59.0% |
| Yeast | 65% | N/A | 65% |

**Direct measurement:** The similarity percentages themselves — these are laboratory results from sequencing.

**Interpretation required:** "This similarity reflects shared ancestry" vs. "Cytochrome c performs the same cellular function in all aerobic organisms — functional constraint predicts sequence conservation regardless of ancestry. A protein that must fit the same molecular binding site in all aerobic organisms will converge on similar sequences because only certain sequences work."

Say:
> "The data — 99% similarity between human and chimp cytochrome c — is directly measured. It's real. The question is what explains it. Common ancestry is one explanation. Functional constraint is another — and it is a mechanically plausible alternative that scientists discuss. Good science requires naming both options, not just the first one that comes to mind."

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Choose one evidence type from today (structure sort, embryology, or molecular). Name one observation, one inference, and one unresolved question.

**Model answers:**

*Structural:*
- Observation: "The bat wing and human arm share the same bone sequence: humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, phalanges — observed in skeletal specimens."
- Inference: "This shared framework reflects either common ancestry or a common design blueprint."
- Unresolved: "Can we determine from bone sequence data alone which explanation is correct — or does that require additional evidence about developmental pathways, genetics, or fossil transitions?"

*Embryological:*
- Observation: "Pharyngeal arches appear at the same developmental stage in fish, frog, chicken, and human embryos — directly imaged and documented."
- Inference: "This shared feature reflects a conserved ancestral developmental pathway."
- Unresolved: "Does the shared developmental toolkit require a common ancestor, or could a common designer use the same efficient pathway in unrelated created kinds?"

*Molecular:*
- Observation: "Human and chimpanzee cytochrome c proteins share 99% sequence identity — measured directly by sequencing."
- Inference: "This high similarity reflects common ancestry."
- Unresolved: "How much of the similarity is explained by functional constraint (only similar sequences work in this binding site) versus inheritance from a common ancestor? How do we separate these two contributions?"

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for one specific observation (something measurable), one specific inference (an interpretive claim), and one genuine unresolved question. Deduct 2 pts if observation and inference are not distinguished.

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Structure practice worksheet (12 items) | 10 pts | Accurate H/A/V classification + one justification sentence per item |
| CER paragraph (embryology or molecular) | 5 pts | Clear claim; specifically cited evidence from today's data; logical reasoning step that names an assumption |
| Vestigial structures short response | 5 pts | Evidence-based interpretation; does not claim "useless" without data; mentions known function where applicable |

**Strong CER paragraph example (molecular):**
> "Claim: High molecular similarity between human and chimpanzee cytochrome c proteins is consistent with close evolutionary relationship. Evidence: Sequencing of the cytochrome c protein shows 99% amino acid identity between humans and chimpanzees, compared to 88% with dogs and 73% with chickens. Reasoning: If organisms share a recent common ancestor, proteins essential to basic cellular function would be expected to show high sequence conservation because mutations in functionally critical proteins are typically lethal. However, this reasoning requires the assumption that sequence similarity primarily reflects ancestry rather than functional constraint — and a common-design interpretation predicts equally high conservation if the same Creator used the same functional protein in closely related organisms. The data alone does not distinguish these two explanations; additional evidence about the mechanism of similarity would be required."

**Strong vestigial response example:**
> "The human appendix is often cited as a vestigial organ because it appears reduced in size and cecal function compared to the large cecum seen in herbivores like rabbits. However, research has identified a role for the human appendix in maintaining gut flora populations — acting as a reservoir that can repopulate the gut after illness. Calling it 'useless' overstates what the data shows. A more accurate claim is: 'The appendix appears to have reduced cecal function compared to related structures in herbivores, though it retains an immune-related role.' This is an important distinction because scientific integrity requires that our claims match our evidence."

**Vestigial rubric:**
- Evidence citation (50%): Specific documented function named (immune role, muscle attachment, etc.)
- Reasoning (30%): Clear connection between evidence and conclusion
- Intellectual precision (20%): Avoids "useless" overclaim; states what data shows and what it doesn't

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Vestigial means useless" | "Vestigial means *reduced in the original function compared to a proposed ancestral version* — not necessarily functionless. The appendix has an immune role; the coccyx has muscle attachment function. Accurate biology requires accurate language." |
| "Molecular similarity proves common ancestry" | "It is consistent with common ancestry — and also consistent with functional constraint (the same functional protein will have similar sequences because only similar sequences work). Distinguishing those two explanations requires more than similarity percentages alone." |
| "Homologous structures can't be explained by design" | "Common design is a coherent alternative — a Creator using the same efficient structural blueprint across different organisms would produce exactly the same pentadactyl pattern. The structural data is consistent with both explanations." |
| "Pharyngeal arches in human embryos means we had fish ancestors" | "The arches are present and real — that is observed. The claim that this means human ancestors were fish is an interpretation. An equally coherent interpretation is that God used similar efficient developmental pathways in vertebrate embryos across different kinds." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Research the concept of "functional constraint" in molecular evolution. Find one publication that discusses how the binding requirements of a protein predict its sequence conservation. Does this affect how you interpret cytochrome c similarity data? Write a paragraph evaluating both explanations.
- **Struggling learners:** Pre-sorted structure card deck with six cards already placed in the correct category as anchors. Students add six remaining cards. Embryo diagram with Stage 1 shared features pre-labeled (S) so students can focus on identifying divergent features in later stages.
- **ELL support:** Vocabulary card with definitions and images: homologous / analogous / vestigial / pharyngeal arch / cytochrome c / molecular similarity. Include a labeled diagram of the pentadactyl limb with bones named.
