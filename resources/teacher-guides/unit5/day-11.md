# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 11 – 15.3 Other Mechanisms of Evolution

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

> "We know that the whole creation has been groaning as in the pains of childbirth right up to the present time." (Rom. 8:22)

Read both aloud. Tie in:

Say:
> "Today we add more mechanisms to our model of evolutionary change — beyond natural selection. Some of these are random (genetic drift), some involve movement of individuals between populations (gene flow), and some involve traits that are about reproductive success rather than survival (sexual selection). These mechanisms show us the full complexity of how populations change. Romans 8:22 is a reminder that creation is not in its original perfect state — some of what we observe, including the random loss of variation in small populations, may reflect that groaning. ## Teacher Objectives

- Students can define and distinguish gene flow, genetic drift, and sexual selection from natural selection.
- Students can compare bottleneck effect and founder effect as specific types of genetic drift, identifying what distinguishes them.
- Students can build a semantic map connecting all mechanisms to the central concept of allele frequency change.

**Background for you:**

**Natural selection (review):** Differential survival and reproduction based on heritable traits. Directional, stabilizing, or disruptive. Tied to fitness.

**Gene flow:** The movement of alleles between populations when individuals migrate and reproduce in a new location. Effect: increases similarity between populations (homogenizes allele frequencies); can introduce new alleles to a population; can prevent populations from diverging into separate species if flow is constant.

**Genetic drift:** Random changes in allele frequency due to chance events — not selection. Most powerful in small populations (where chance has bigger effects). Two important types:
- **Bottleneck effect:** A catastrophic event (disease, disaster, hunting) drastically reduces a population. Survivors carry only a subset of the original genetic variation. The resulting small population has a limited gene pool that may not represent the original — by chance, some alleles are lost entirely. Example: Northern elephant seal — hunted to ~20 individuals in the 19th century; a genetic analysis shows extremely low variation today despite having recovered to ~170,000 individuals.
- **Founder effect:** A small number of individuals leave a larger population and establish a new population elsewhere. They carry only the alleles they personally have — a limited, potentially unrepresentative sample. The new population may have very different allele frequencies from the source population, purely by chance. Example: Amish communities — due to the small founder population, certain rare genetic conditions (like Ellis-van Creveld syndrome) are much more common than in the general population.

**Sexual selection:** A subset of natural selection where traits are selected because they increase mating success, even if they reduce survival. Two mechanisms:
- **Intrasexual selection:** Competition among members of the same sex (usually males) for access to mates. Example: elk antlers — large antlers help males compete for females; the "winner" mates more.
- **Intersexual selection:** Members of one sex (usually females) choose among members of the other sex. Choosy females select for male traits that signal genetic quality. Example: peacock tail — large, colorful tail is energetically costly and reduces predator escape, but females preferentially mate with males who have them. The trait is maintained because mating success outweighs survival cost.

**Why genetic drift matters theologically:** Drift can fix harmful alleles in small populations (they become universal by chance). This is one mechanism by which creation might "groan" — populations losing variation, harmful alleles spreading not by design but by chance. This is a sobering observation that doesn't require an evolutionary framework to acknowledge — small isolated populations in any model are subject to reduced variation and possibly increased genetic load.

- **CER template:** Standard three-column format.

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Question:** "Name one mechanism of evolution besides natural selection."

| Level | Answer |
|-------|--------|
| Basic | "Genetic drift." |
| Good | "Gene flow — when individuals move between populations and bring new alleles with them." |
| Strong | "Genetic drift — random changes in allele frequency that are most pronounced in small populations. Unlike natural selection, drift is not tied to fitness — an allele can become common or disappear purely by chance." |

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for naming a mechanism with a brief accurate description. Deduct 2 pts for naming "mutation" alone (mutation introduces variation but is not itself a mechanism of frequency change in the way the others are — though it can be accepted if student explains it adds new alleles).

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Semantic Map Build (15 min)

Distribute semantic map templates. Build together on the board; students fill in their templates as you go.

**Central concept:** Allele frequency change — any process that changes how common an allele is in a population.

Fill in each branch:

**Natural Selection:**
- Mechanism: Differential survival/reproduction based on heritable traits.
- Direction: Directed by environment — not random.
- Population size dependence: Works in large and small populations.
- Example: Antibiotic resistance; finch beak size.

**Gene Flow:**
- Mechanism: Individuals migrate; bring alleles from one population to another.
- Direction: Depends on which alleles migrate — not systematically directional.
- Population size dependence: More impactful in small populations (one migrant can significantly shift frequencies in a small pop).
- Effect on divergence: Reduces divergence between populations; keeps them similar.
- Example: Birds colonizing new islands from mainland; human migration carrying new alleles.

**Genetic Drift (overall):**
- Mechanism: Random sampling error — like flipping a coin, small sample sizes deviate from expected frequencies by chance.
- Direction: Random — can increase or decrease any allele.
- Population size dependence: Most powerful in small populations. In large populations, drift effects average out.
- Example: Any small population event.

**Bottleneck Effect (type of drift):**
- Distinguished by: Catastrophic event that suddenly reduces population size.
- Example: Northern elephant seal hunted to ~20 individuals → loss of genetic variation → today's large population still shows low genetic diversity.

**Founder Effect (type of drift):**
- Distinguished by: Small group leaves larger population to found a new one elsewhere.
- Example: Amish — small founding group carried higher frequency of Ellis-van Creveld syndrome allele; condition is now much more common in Amish communities than general population.

**Sexual Selection:**
- Mechanism: Traits that increase mating success are selected, even if they reduce survival.
- Intrasexual: Competition within one sex (usually male-male competition). Example: elk antlers, bighorn sheep horns.
- Intersexual: Choice by one sex (usually females choosing males). Example: peacock tail — females prefer longer, more colorful tails.
- Direction: Directed by mate preference — can produce traits that conflict with survival (costly signals).

Say after completing the map:
> "Notice that some of these are directed — natural selection and sexual selection respond to environmental fitness or mate preference — and some are random — genetic drift doesn't 'care' about fitness. This mix of directed and random is one of the things that makes population genetics complex. In a designed creation, we'd expect directed mechanisms to be primary; drift may reflect the current imperfect state of creation."

### 2. Scenario Classification (25 min)

Groups classify scenario cards. Each group records: mechanism name, justification (one sentence), and whether the mechanism is directed or random.

**Answer key:**

| Scenario | Mechanism | Directed or Random | Justification |
|---------|----------|-------------------|--------------|
| 1. A volcano kills 95% of a bird population on an island. The survivors happen to all carry a rare color allele that becomes universal in the rebuilt population. | Bottleneck effect (genetic drift) | Random | The color allele spread not because it was adaptive but because it happened to be in the survivors — chance, not selection. |
| 2. A peahen only agrees to mate with the peacock who has the longest, most colorful tail. | Sexual selection (intersexual) | Directed (by mate preference) | The female's preference directs which alleles are passed on — not random, not based on survival. |
| 3. Eight rabbits from a mainland population float to an island on storm debris. They found a new population. Their allele frequencies differ from the mainland because they were a small sample. | Founder effect (genetic drift) | Random | The allele frequencies in the founding group were a random sample from the mainland; their limited variation is chance, not selection. |
| 4. Some wolves from one pack join a nearby pack due to territory pressure, introducing alleles not previously present in that pack. | Gene flow | Neither directed nor random — depends on which individuals move | Migration moves alleles between populations; tends to homogenize frequencies. |
| 5. Male elephant seals with the largest bodies win fights for access to beach females; smaller males rarely mate. | Sexual selection (intrasexual) | Directed (by competition outcome) | Male-male competition determines who mates — size is selected by reproductive success, not survival alone. |
| 6. A disease kills 80% of a cheetah population. The few survivors all happen to share a similar set of immune alleles, leaving cheetahs as one of the least genetically diverse large mammals. | Bottleneck effect (genetic drift) | Random | The immune allele similarity is because survivors all happened to have it — chance sampling, not adaptive selection of the specific alleles that remained. |
| 7. A student colony of lab mice accidentally loses one cage, and now only one allele for a coat color gene exists in the remaining population. | Genetic drift (small population) | Random | In a tiny population, one allele can disappear purely by chance with no selective disadvantage. |
| 8. Female birds in a rainforest population consistently avoid mating with males who have parasites, selecting for males with bright plumage (which signals parasite-free health). | Sexual selection (intersexual) | Directed (by female preference signaling genetic quality) | Female choice is directed — plumage signals health; the preference is not random. |

**Debrief (5 min):** Work through #1 vs. #6 (both bottleneck, but different catastrophe type) and #2 vs. #5 (both sexual selection, intrasexual vs. intersexual).

Say:
> "Bottleneck and founder are both drift — random — but they have different triggers. Bottleneck = population crashes; founder = population starts new. Both result in reduced variation compared to the source. Sexual selection has two forms: male-male competition (intrasexual) and female choice (intersexual). Both result in reproductive success determining which traits spread — but by different pathways."

### 3. CER Practice (20 min)

Students write one CER applying one mechanism from today to an original claim.

**Model CER 1 (Bottleneck):**
- **Claim:** "The Northern elephant seal population today shows very low genetic diversity despite being large, because of a historical bottleneck."
- **Evidence:** "Elephant seals were hunted to approximately 20 individuals in the 19th century. Genetic analysis of the current population (~170,000 individuals) shows genetic variation far below what would be expected for a population of this size — consistent with a severe founder/bottleneck event."
- **Reasoning:** "When a population is reduced to 20 individuals, only the alleles those 20 individuals carry survive. Many alleles that existed in the larger original population are lost forever by chance — not because they were maladaptive, but because the individuals who carried them happened to die. The current large population is genetically descended from those 20 survivors. No matter how large it grows, it cannot recover the lost variation without mutation or gene flow from other populations. This is genetic drift — random frequency change due to small population size — not natural selection."

**Model CER 2 (Founder Effect):**
- **Claim:** "The elevated frequency of Ellis-van Creveld syndrome in Amish communities reflects the founder effect."
- **Evidence:** "Ellis-van Creveld syndrome is a rare condition in the general population (~1 in 60,000–200,000). In some Amish communities, the rate is approximately 1 in 200. Genetic analysis traces the syndrome allele to a small number of founding ancestors in the 18th century."
- **Reasoning:** "The Amish founding population in North America was a small group of emigrants. By chance, the founders carried the Ellis-van Creveld allele at a higher frequency than the general European population. Because the Amish community has historically practiced limited intermarriage with outside populations (low gene flow), the founding allele frequency was preserved and amplified through subsequent generations. The elevated rate is not because the allele is adaptive in the Amish environment — it is a random sampling artifact of the small founding group. This is the founder effect: genetic drift caused by a small founding population."

**Grading:** Students are not expected to know these specific examples — let them write CERs about scenarios from the card sort or from class. The model above is for you to evaluate quality.

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Compare bottleneck and founder effects in two sentences — what do they share and what distinguishes them?

**Model answer:** "Both bottleneck and founder effects are types of genetic drift in which a small population carries reduced genetic variation compared to a larger source population — and allele frequencies may change by chance rather than by selection. They differ in cause: a bottleneck occurs when a large population is drastically reduced by a catastrophic event; a founder effect occurs when a small subset of a population leaves to establish a new population in a new location."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for: both named as drift (or equivalent) + shared consequence (reduced variation, chance-based frequency change) + distinguishing cause. Deduct 2 pts if student describes only one.

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| 15.3 practice set | 10 pts | Mechanism classification accurate; bottleneck vs. founder distinguished; sexual selection intrasexual vs. intersexual distinguished; gene flow direction effects explained |
| Reflection comparing drift and natural selection | 5 pts | Names the key difference (random vs. directed by fitness); names population size as a factor in drift strength; does not conflate the two |

**Strong reflection example:**
> "Genetic drift and natural selection are both mechanisms of allele frequency change, but they differ fundamentally in cause and direction. Natural selection is directed — the environment consistently favors certain heritable traits, so allele frequencies change in a predictable direction tied to fitness. Genetic drift is random — allele frequencies change by chance, with no connection to whether the allele is adaptive. In a large population, drift's effects are small because random deviations average out. In a small population, drift can be powerful — a single random event can fix or eliminate an allele entirely. This is why the two mechanisms can conflict: an adaptive allele can be lost by drift in a small population, and a neutral or even harmful allele can become universal by drift in a bottleneck. Selection and drift are not alternatives — they operate simultaneously, with drift dominating in small populations and selection dominating in large ones."

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Genetic drift = random evolution" | "Drift is random frequency change — but it doesn't mean 'unguided overall.' Drift operates within a creation where other mechanisms (selection, gene flow) are also active. Drift explains patterns like loss of variation in small populations and elevated rare allele frequencies in founder populations — real, measurable things." |
| "Sexual selection and natural selection are the same thing" | "Sexual selection is a subset of natural selection — both involve differential reproduction. The distinction is mechanism: natural selection is driven by survival differential; sexual selection is driven by mating success differential. A peacock's tail reduces survival but increases mating success. Both affect allele frequencies, but by different pathways." |
| "Gene flow always helps populations" | "Gene flow increases diversity (helpful) but can also prevent local adaptation (unhelpful) if populations are continually 'refreshed' from a source population before local selection can act. Very high gene flow prevents divergence — which can be beneficial or not depending on context." |
| "Bottleneck = founder" | "Both result in reduced variation. The distinction: bottleneck = a large population shrinks catastrophically due to a disaster. Founder = a small group leaves to start a new population. Same outcome (small gene pool), different trigger." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Research the cheetah genetic bottleneck. How low is cheetah genetic diversity compared to other large cats? What are the proposed explanations (end-of-Pleistocene bottleneck vs. more recent bottleneck)? What are the conservation implications? Write a two-paragraph analysis connecting your findings to today's content.
- **Struggling learners:** Semantic map template with all six branch labels pre-printed and "Mechanism" and "Example" fields labeled — students fill in a one-sentence definition and one example per branch. Scenario cards pre-sorted into three color groups (drift mechanisms = blue; selection = red; gene flow = green) so students classify within categories rather than across all six.
