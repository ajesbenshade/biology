# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 13 – Looking at the Data: Tracking Evolution (Deer Mice) + Chapter 15 Review

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, delivery notes, and grading supports.

## Scripture Focus

**Colossians 1:16-17 + Romans 1:20**

> “All things were created through him and for him… His invisible qualities… clearly seen from what has been made.”

Read aloud at the start. Tie in:

Say:
> “Quantitative data analysis is stewardship. When we measure allele frequencies and compare expected and observed patterns, we are studying the order God built into living things. Our task is to observe carefully, calculate honestly, and avoid claiming more than the data can support.”

## Teacher Objectives

- Calculate allele and genotype frequencies from deer mouse coat-color data.
- Apply the Hardy-Weinberg principle to compare expected vs. observed frequencies.
- Interpret deviations from equilibrium using likely mechanisms such as selection, drift, or migration.
- Review Chapter 15 concepts through stations and error correction.

## Background for the Teacher

**Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium** is a null model. It describes what happens when allele frequencies remain stable across generations because no major evolutionary forces are acting. The classic conditions are:

- no natural selection
- no migration (gene flow)
- no mutation
- very large population size (so drift is minimal)
- random mating

If those conditions hold, then:

$$p + q = 1$$

and genotype frequencies are predicted by:

$$p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$$

Where:
- $p$ = frequency of one allele
- $q$ = frequency of the other allele
- $p^2$ = homozygous dominant expected frequency
- $2pq$ = heterozygous expected frequency
- $q^2$ = homozygous recessive expected frequency

Important teaching point:

Say:
> “Deviation from Hardy-Weinberg does not automatically mean natural selection. It means one or more equilibrium conditions were not met. Then we ask: which force best explains the pattern?”

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- “Data tables show real, measurable change. That glorifies God’s design for adaptability and reminds us to steward truth by separating observation from assumption.”
- “Observed frequency shifts are strong evidence for micro-change within populations.”
- “Broader historical claims require interpretation, and honest interpretation matters.”
- “Correction builds integrity. Proverbs 12:22 reminds us that truthful speech matters in science as well as in daily life.”

## Materials & Preparation

- Printed deer mouse data tables showing light and dark coat-color frequencies across generations.
- Hardy-Weinberg reference sheet.
- Calculators.
- Review station cards for Chapter 15.
- One or two flawed sample student responses for correction practice.

## Bell Work (10 min)

**Prompt:** Define allele frequency.

**Model answer:**
> “The proportion of a specific allele in the population gene pool.”

**Scoring:** 5 pts for effort and reasonable accuracy.

- 5 pts: proportion-based definition is clear
- 3–4 pts: “how common an allele is” but vague
- 1–2 pts: incomplete or confused with genotype

## Lesson Delivery (70 min)

### 1. Scripture + Intro (5 min)

Read Colossians 1:16-17 and Romans 1:20.

Say:
> “Today we do what scientists must do well: work carefully with numbers. Creation reflects God’s order. Careful data analysis is one way we honor that order.”

### 2. Deer Mice Data Analysis (25 min)

Walk students through one sample before releasing them to work.

**Teacher model steps:**
1. Identify the number of each genotype or phenotype provided.
2. Convert counts into allele frequencies.
3. Solve for $p$ and $q$.
4. Use Hardy-Weinberg to calculate expected genotype frequencies.
5. Compare expected to observed data.
6. Propose a likely explanation.

**Suggested script:**
> “If the dark-fur allele increases across generations in a lava-field environment, that pattern is consistent with directional selection favoring camouflage. That is a measurable, population-level change.”

Emphasize:
> “This is direct evidence of microevolutionary change in a population. It is not, by itself, proof of every broader historical claim people may attach to evolution.”

### 3. Hardy-Weinberg Check (20 min)

Groups compare observed and expected frequencies.

Discussion prompts:
- Are observed frequencies close to equilibrium?
- If not, what force may be acting?
- Would small population size matter here?
- Could migration explain the pattern?

**Teacher point:**
> “Hardy-Weinberg is the null model. When data depart from it, that tells us something is happening. Then we use evidence to identify the likely cause.”

### 4. Review Stations + Error Correction (20 min)

Set up 3–4 stations:
- Station 1: selection types
- Station 2: evidence vs. inference
- Station 3: gene flow / drift / sexual selection
- Station 4: error correction and vocabulary precision

Give students one weak sample answer to revise.

**Example weak response:**
> “The mouse data proves all life came from one ancestor.”

**Revised stronger response:**
> “The mouse data shows a measurable shift in allele frequency within a population, likely due to selection. It supports microevolutionary change, but broader ancestry claims require additional lines of evidence and interpretation.”

## Exit Ticket

**Prompt:** Identify one Chapter 15 statement supported by strong direct evidence and one statement that depends heavily on broader assumptions.

**Model key:**
- Strong-evidence example: “Observed frequency shifts in deer mice populations.”
- Assumption-heavy example: “This single data set proves universal ancestry.”

**Scoring:** 5 pts
- 5 pts: clear distinction between direct evidence and broader inference
- 3–4 pts: one example solid, one vague
- 1–2 pts: confuses evidence and interpretation

## Homework Grading

- **Chapter 15 review set** — 10 pts
- **Reflection: measured change vs. broader claims** — 5 pts

**Rubric:**
- Math/data accuracy: 50%
- Reasoning and clarity: 50%

## Common Confusions and Fixes

- **“Deviation always means selection.”**  
  Fix: “Not always. Drift, migration, mutation, or non-random mating may also cause deviation.”
- **“Hardy-Weinberg describes what always happens.”**  
  Fix: “No. It is the null case used for comparison.”
- **“Micro-change automatically proves macro-history.”**  
  Fix: “Observable population change is strong evidence for microevolution. Larger historical conclusions require more evidence and careful interpretation.”

## Differentiation

- **Struggling learners:** provide a step-by-step Hardy-Weinberg calculation sheet.
- **Advanced learners:** add a small-population drift comparison or optional simulation.
- **ELL support:** pre-teach frequency, allele, genotype, expected, observed, equilibrium.
