# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 10 – 15.2 Evolution in Populations and Hawks & Mice Minilab

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Colossians 1:16-17 (continued)**
> "In him all things hold together."

Say:
> "Yesterday we saw how humans figured out the mechanism of selection. Today we zoom in to the population level — where we can actually measure allele frequency change in data. This is where evolutionary biology becomes most scientific: observable, quantifiable, and testable in real time. And it's where the evidence for selection as an active mechanism is strongest."

Colossians 1:16-17 frames the sustaining order God built into creation. Population-level allele frequency change under selection is that sustaining order in action — life responsive to its environment within the parameters God designed.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Students can define allele frequency accurately and calculate a simple frequency from a population data set.
- Students can distinguish stabilizing, directional, and disruptive selection and correctly match each to a graph shape, scenario, and example.
- Students can design and execute the Hawks and Mice simulation, accurately recording data across generations, graphing results, and identifying which selection type the data demonstrates.

**Background for you:**

**Allele frequency:** The proportion of a specific allele among all copies of that gene in a population. If a population of 100 individuals has 40 copies of allele A out of 200 total alleles (diploid — each individual has 2 copies), the allele frequency of A = 40/200 = 0.20 (20%). Frequency always expressed as a decimal between 0 and 1, or as a percentage.

**Selection types:**
- **Stabilizing:** The most common (intermediate) phenotype is favored; extremes are selected against. Population mean stays the same; variance decreases. Example: human birth weight — very small and very large newborns have lower survival rates; intermediate weights are favored.
- **Directional:** One extreme phenotype is favored; the other extreme is selected against. Population mean shifts in one direction over generations. Example: antibiotic resistance (resistance allele increases); finch beak size shifts during drought toward larger beaks that crack tough seeds.
- **Disruptive:** Both extremes are favored; the intermediate phenotype is selected against. Population can split into two sub-distributions. Example: African seedcracker finches that have either very small or very large beaks — both are efficient on different seed sizes; medium-beaked birds are inefficient at both.

**Hawks and Mice simulation:** Students use colored beads or beans representing "mice" on a contrasting background paper. "Hawks" (students using tongs or their dominant hand to quickly pick up mice) preferentially remove the easiest-to-see mice (those that contrast most with the background). After each "generation," surviving mice reproduce (add one copy of each survivor back into the population). Students track the frequency of each mouse color over multiple generations. The expected result: the frequency of the well-camouflaged color increases over generations (directional or stabilizing selection depending on setup).

**Simulation variants:**
- One background color → directional selection toward the camouflaged phenotype.
- Two background patches, each favoring a different color → can simulate disruptive selection across patches.
- Pre-set population with most mice at intermediate color + backgrounds that favor extremes → disruptive selection.

Choose your variant based on materials and what selection type you want to demonstrate. The simplest version (one background, one well-camouflaged color) shows directional selection cleanly.

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Population data reveals God's built-in mechanisms for survival — measurable, quantifiable, reproducible. The frequency shift we see in the simulation is a real phenomenon that happens in wild populations."
- "Stabilizing selection preserving a well-designed intermediate is consistent with a world where God's design has already optimized many traits — the intermediate IS the best solution for that environment."
- "Observe humbly, interpret carefully. The simulation shows *which phenotypes survive under this specific selection pressure.* What it does not show is that this mechanism, over deep time, produces novel body plans — that remains the inference step."

## Materials & Preparation

**Per group (3–4 students):**
- 50 colored beads or dried beans: 20 color A (matches background), 20 color B (contrasts background), 10 color C (intermediate). Adjust based on available materials.
- 1 sheet of colored background paper (e.g., green or brown, matching color A).
- Tongs or forceps (the "hawk") — one per group.
- Timer (15-second "predation" rounds).
- Data table handout (columns: Generation | Color A count | Color B count | Color C count | Total).
- Graph template (line graph: Generation on x-axis, Frequency on y-axis, one line per color).

**Teacher prep:**
- Pre-count each color into labeled bags for quick distribution.
- Test the simulation yourself first — 15-second predation rounds with tongs reliably removes the conspicuous color each round.
- Decide in advance which selection type your setup will demonstrate and have that ready to reveal after students identify it from their data.

**Frayer square template (one per student):** Four-quadrant card — upper left: Definition; upper right: Characteristics; lower left: Examples; lower right: Non-examples.

## Bell Work Answer Key (10 min)

**Question:** "Define allele frequency."

| Level | Answer |
|-------|--------|
| Basic | "How common an allele is in a population." |
| Good | "The proportion of a specific allele among all alleles of that gene in a population — expressed as a decimal from 0 to 1." |
| Strong | "The proportion of a specific allele among all copies of that gene locus in a population. Calculated as: (number of copies of the allele) ÷ (total number of alleles of that gene in the population). For a diploid population of N individuals, the denominator is 2N." |

**Practice calculation (optional, 3 min):** "A population of 50 rabbits has a fur color gene with two alleles: B (brown) and b (white). 30 rabbits are BB, 15 are Bb, and 5 are bb. What is the frequency of the b allele?"

- Total alleles = 100 (50 rabbits × 2 alleles each).
- b alleles: 5 × 2 (from bb) + 15 × 1 (from Bb) = 10 + 15 = 25.
- Frequency of b = 25/100 = **0.25 (25%).**

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for proportion-based definition (not just "how common"). Calculation: +2 bonus points if offered.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Frayer Squares — Three Selection Types (10 min)

Students complete four-quadrant cards for three terms: stabilizing selection, directional selection, disruptive selection.

**Completed Frayer squares:**

**Stabilizing Selection:**
- Definition: Natural selection that favors intermediate phenotypes; extremes are selected against.
- Characteristics: Population mean stays the same; variation decreases over time; graph shows narrowing bell curve around the mean.
- Examples: Human birth weight; spider web silk thickness (must be strong enough to hold prey, light enough for the spider).
- Non-examples: Antibiotic resistance (that's directional); finch beak split (that's disruptive).

**Directional Selection:**
- Definition: Natural selection that favors one extreme phenotype; the other extreme is selected against; population mean shifts.
- Characteristics: Population mean moves in one direction over generations; graph shows bell curve shifting left or right.
- Examples: Antibiotic resistance (frequency of resistance allele increases); bedbug DDT resistance; finch beak size during drought; DDT resistance in mosquito populations.
- Non-examples: Human birth weight (that's stabilizing); African seedcracker beak split (that's disruptive).

**Disruptive Selection:**
- Definition: Natural selection that favors both extreme phenotypes and selects against the intermediate; can produce a bimodal (two-peaked) distribution.
- Characteristics: Population variance increases; intermediate phenotypes decrease; may lead to two sub-populations.
- Examples: African seedcracker finches (very small or very large beaks efficient; medium beaks inefficient at both seed sizes); oyster body size in some populations; coat color in some shellfish.
- Non-examples: Human birth weight, antibiotic resistance.

### 2. Selection Pattern Matching Drill (15 min)

Students match 8 scenarios to the correct selection type. Work individually (5 min), then compare with a partner.

**Answer key:**

| Scenario | Selection Type |
|---------|--------------|
| 1. A population of moths experiences pressure from owls that see medium-gray moths most easily. Black and white moths survive at higher rates. | Disruptive |
| 2. Baby cheetahs that are either very fast or very slow are less likely to survive — medium speed is optimal for the hunt-escape balance at this age. | Stabilizing |
| 3. A drought kills small-seeded plants. Only large, hard seeds remain. Birds with large beaks can crack them; small-beaked birds starve. | Directional (toward larger beaks) |
| 4. Salmon in a river evolve toward an average body length because very large individuals are caught in shallow rapids and very small individuals cannot compete for spawning sites. | Stabilizing |
| 5. In a hospital setting, bacteria that are more resistant to an antibiotic reproduce more; susceptible strains die. | Directional (toward resistance) |
| 6. A population of beetles on a two-toned leaf surface: green and brown beetles blend in; medium-tan beetles are visible against both surfaces. | Disruptive |
| 7. Human height is under moderate stabilizing selection (very short and very tall individuals face slight disadvantages in ancestral environments). | Stabilizing |
| 8. A fish population in warming water: fish with heat-tolerance alleles reproduce more successfully; cold-adapted variants die earlier. | Directional (toward heat tolerance) |

**Debrief (5 min):** Go over #1 and #6 (disruptive — most frequently confused). Say:
> "Disruptive is the least intuitive because it works against the middle. Think of it as: if the environment has two distinct niches and the intermediate phenotype fits neither, disruptive selection can pull a population toward two different peaks."

### 3. Hawks and Mice Minilab (30 min)

**Setup (3 min):** Distribute materials. Each group spreads their 50 beads randomly on the background paper. They record the starting count of each color in Generation 0.

**Predation round (each generation = 4 min):**
1. "Hawk" uses tongs to remove as many beads as possible in 15 seconds — picking whatever is easiest to grab (most conspicuous).
2. Count remaining survivors. Record.
3. Add one bead of each surviving color (prey reproduce — each survivor has one offspring added back to the population). This keeps total population from collapsing.
4. Repeat for 5 generations.

**Expected data (approximate, one background, color A matches):**

| Generation | Color A (camouflaged) | Color B (conspicuous) | Color C (intermediate) |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| 0 | 20 | 20 | 10 |
| 1 | 22 | 14 | 9 |
| 2 | 25 | 10 | 8 |
| 3 | 28 | 7 | 7 |
| 4 | 31 | 5 | 6 |
| 5 | 34 | 3 | 5 |

*Note: Actual results will vary by group. The trend should be clear: Color A increases; Color B decreases.*

**Mid-lab prompt (say after Generation 2):**
> "Don't just record the numbers — watch what is happening. Which color are you actually picking up most easily? That's the selection pressure. The trend in your data should reflect what you're experiencing as the hawk."

**Analysis questions (students answer after data collection):**
1. Which color's frequency increased most over the five generations? Why?
2. What type of selection does your graph show — stabilizing, directional, or disruptive? How do you know?
3. What real-world scenario does this simulate? Name one actual species where this type of selection has been documented.
4. What does the simulation **not** model that would happen in a real population? (Expected: genetic recombination, mutation, prey reproducing with mates, multiple predator types, immigration from other populations.)

**Answer key for analysis:**
1. Color A (camouflaged) increased because it was least visible against the background and therefore least frequently "predated" — those individuals survived and reproduced.
2. Directional selection — the frequency of one extreme phenotype (camouflaged) increased systematically over generations; the other extreme (conspicuous) decreased.
3. Real-world: peppered moth color frequency shifts during industrial revolution in England (Biston betularia) — one of the best-documented examples of directional selection in wild populations.
4. Simulation limitations: No mutation (new colors can't arise); reproduction is asexual (no recombination); all predation by one type of predator; no immigration; no environmental change mid-simulation; "reproduction" adds exactly one offspring per survivor (no variation in reproductive fitness).

### 4. Data Analysis and Discussion (15 min)

Students graph their results (line graph, one line per color over 5 generations) and answer the analysis questions. Share out question 4:

Say:
> "Every model is a simplification. What's important is knowing what the model does well — shows directional selection clearly — and what it omits — mutation, recombination, immigration. When a model works, we can be confident about what it demonstrates. When it simplifies, we have to be careful about what we conclude beyond the simulation."

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Name the selection type your simulation showed, describe the graph shape it produces, and give one real-world example.

**Model answer:** "Our Hawks and Mice graph showed directional selection — the frequency of the camouflaged color (Color A) increased each generation while the conspicuous color (Color B) decreased. The graph shows one line trending upward and one trending downward over generations — the bell curve of phenotype frequencies would shift toward the camouflaged extreme. A real-world example: finch beak size during the 1977 Galápagos drought. When small seeds were exhausted, only large hard seeds remained. Birds with large beaks survived preferentially; the population's mean beak size increased measurably over two breeding seasons — directly observed and published by Peter and Rosemary Grant."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for: selection type named + graph shape/trend described + one specific real-world example. Deduct 2 pts for naming a type without describing the graph pattern.

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Minilab graph + analysis questions | 10 pts | Graph accurate (labeled axes, correct trends, legend); all 4 analysis questions answered; question 4 names at least 2 simulation limitations |
| 15.2 review + reflection on model limits | 5 pts | Reflection identifies at least one thing the simulation cannot model and explains why that limits what we can conclude from simulation results alone |

**Strong reflection example:**
> "The Hawks and Mice simulation clearly shows directional selection — the camouflaged color increases frequency under predation pressure. One significant limitation is that the model has no mutation. In real populations, new alleles can arise and add to the variation available for selection to act on. The simulation only shows selection acting on pre-existing variation — it cannot model how new variation arises. This matters because one of the open questions in evolutionary biology is whether natural selection acting on new mutations (plus other mechanisms) can produce genuinely novel structures — not just shift frequencies of existing alleles. The simulation does not address that question, which is why conclusions from it should be limited to 'selection changes allele frequencies' rather than 'selection can produce unlimited morphological change.'"

**Minilab graph rubric:**
- Both axes labeled with units (10%): generation number, color frequency or count.
- Correct trends shown (40%): Color A increasing, Color B decreasing, Color C stable or slightly decreasing.
- Legend present (10%): three lines distinguished.
- Analysis questions answered fully (40%): selection type named; one real-world example; two limitations.

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Directional always increases size or fitness" | "Directional selection favors *one extreme* — that could be smaller, larger, more resistant, less resistant, darker, or lighter. The direction depends on which extreme the environment currently favors. During a drought favoring large seeds, beaks get larger. In a low-food environment favoring energy efficiency, smaller bodies might be selected." |
| "Stabilizing selection means nothing is changing" | "Stabilizing selection is still selection — it's just maintaining the current optimal phenotype by removing extremes. Variation decreases (fewer extreme individuals), but the mechanism of differential survival is actively maintaining the average." |
| "The simulation proves selection can produce new species" | "The simulation shows allele frequency change under selection over 5 generations. It does not show speciation — that requires populations to become reproductively isolated and diverge sufficiently. Our simulation has no reproductive isolation, no mutation, and only 5 generations. Stay within what the data shows." |
| "Hardy-Weinberg means allele frequencies never change" | "Hardy-Weinberg describes the null case — frequencies stay constant only if NO selection, NO mutation, NO drift, NO gene flow, and random mating all hold simultaneously. In real populations, at least one of those is usually violated. Hardy-Weinberg is useful as a baseline to detect when something IS changing frequencies." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Calculate the expected allele frequency change under Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium for two alleles, then compare to the Hawks and Mice simulation results. Why does your simulation depart from Hardy-Weinberg? Which conditions are violated? Write a paragraph connecting the simulation to H-W assumptions.
- **Struggling learners:** Graph template with axes pre-labeled and scale pre-set — students plot points and connect lines. Analysis questions with sentence starters: "The selection type shown is ___ because ___." "One real-world example is ___."
- **ELL support:** Vocabulary card: allele frequency, stabilizing, directional, disruptive, predation, frequency. Hawks and Mice procedure with visual step-by-step diagrams (no text-heavy instructions).
