# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 15 – Chapter 16 Opener + Case Study: Tracking Tuskless Elephants

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains teacher background, discussion supports, and grading guidance.

## Scripture Focus

**Genesis 2:15 + Colossians 1:16-17**

Tie in:
> “Humans are called to steward creation. When human actions harm populations, science helps us understand consequences and make wiser choices.”

## Teacher Objectives

- Interpret data showing the increase of tusklessness in Gorongosa elephants.
- Distinguish direct observations from inferences.
- Connect human selection pressure to measurable population change.
- Discuss the ethical dimension of human impact on creation.

## Background for the Teacher

The Gorongosa elephant case is an example of **human-caused selection pressure**.

During periods of intense poaching, elephants with tusks were disproportionately killed for ivory. Tuskless females, who were less likely to be targeted, survived and reproduced at higher rates. Over time, tusklessness became more common in the population.

Important distinctions:
- This is a change in **trait frequency** within an elephant population.
- It is **not** evidence that a new species formed.
- It is a powerful example of how strong selection pressure can rapidly shift a population.

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- “Human-caused pressure highlights stewardship responsibility.”
- “Observable adaptation within elephants shows resilience built into creation.”
- “Science can expose the consequences of sinful exploitation and support wiser care.”

## Materials & Preparation

- Printed tuskless elephant case study.
- Trait-frequency data.
- Map of Gorongosa / regional poaching context.
- CER template.

## Bell Work (8 min)

**Prompt:** Predict how heavy poaching for ivory might affect the frequency of tusks in an elephant population.

**Strong model response:**
> “If elephants with tusks are killed more often, then tuskless elephants will survive and reproduce more, so tusklessness may become more common over time.”

**Scoring:** 5 pts for effort and relevance.

## Lesson Delivery (70 min)

### 1. Phenomenon Launch (10 min)

Show image or graph of tusked vs. tuskless elephants.

Say:
> “Today’s question is simple but important: what happens to a population when humans consistently remove individuals with one particular trait?”

Have students annotate one image or graph for pattern, question, and possible cause.

### 2. Case Read + Summary (20 min)

Students read the case study in groups and summarize:
- what changed
- what likely caused the change
- what evidence supports the explanation

**Teacher point:**
> “The strongest observation here is a change in trait frequency before and after intense poaching pressure.”

### 3. Map Interpretation (15 min)

Connect location and poaching history to the trait shift.

Discussion prompts:
- Where did poaching pressure occur?
- Why would tusklessness become more common in that setting?
- What does this say about human influence on selection pressures?

### 4. CER Writing (20 min)

**Prompt:** Explain why tusklessness increased in the population.

**Model CER:**
- **Claim:** Tusklessness increased because poaching selected against tusked elephants.
- **Evidence:** Tusked elephants were more likely to be killed for ivory; the percentage of tuskless females increased after intense poaching.
- **Reasoning:** When humans remove one trait more often than another, the surviving population reproduces disproportionately. Over time, the less-targeted trait becomes more common.

### 5. Brief Reflection (5 min)

Ask:
> “What does this case teach us about stewardship and unintended consequences?”

## Homework Grading

- **Case questions** — 10 pts
- **Reflection on evidence and ethics** — 5 pts

**Rubric:**
- Data use: 60%
- Ethical tie: 40%

## Common Confusions and Fixes

- **“Tuskless elephants are a new species.”**  
  Fix: “No. This is a trait-frequency change within the same species.”
- **“Elephants chose to lose tusks.”**  
  Fix: “No. Existing variation plus differential survival changed the population.”
- **“Human selection pressure is different from natural selection in mechanism.”**  
  Fix: “The selecting agent is different, but the population process is still differential survival and reproduction.”

## Differentiation

- **Struggling learners:** provide CER sentence stems.
- **Advanced learners:** compare the elephant case to artificial selection and to natural selection examples from Chapter 15.
- **ELL support:** pre-teach poaching, trait frequency, selection pressure, ivory, population.

## Prayer Close

> “Lord, help us steward creation wisely and learn from the consequences of human actions.”
