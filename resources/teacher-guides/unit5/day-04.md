# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 4 – Radiometric Dating and Forensic Isotope Data

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Proverbs 18:13 / 1 Thessalonians 5:21**
> "To answer before listening—that is folly and shame." (Prov. 18:13)
> "Test everything. Hold to the good." (1 Thess. 5:21)

These verses set the standard for today: careful listening to what the data actually shows before drawing conclusions, and testing claims rather than accepting them uncritically. Radiometric dating is a real, rigorous scientific technique—and it has real, discussable assumptions and limitations. Both should be engaged honestly.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Explain the basic logic of radioactive decay and why it produces predictable ratio changes over time.
- Interpret a ¹⁴C/¹²C graph, identify the nuclear-test-era anomaly, and explain why calibration is required.
- Calculate half-life problems accurately.
- Identify method-specific limitations (e.g., why ¹⁴C cannot date dinosaur bones).

**Background for you:** Radioactive decay is the process by which unstable isotopes lose particles over time and transform into stable "daughter" isotopes. The *half-life* is the time for exactly half the parent isotope to decay—this is a constant for each isotope measured in laboratory settings. Carbon-14 (¹⁴C) is produced in the upper atmosphere from cosmic ray bombardment of nitrogen-14, and living organisms continuously exchange carbon (including ¹⁴C) with their environment. When an organism dies, exchange stops, and ¹⁴C begins to decay without replenishment. By comparing ¹⁴C to stable ¹²C, we can estimate how long ago the organism died.

**Limitations that are legitimate to discuss:**
1. **Range:** ¹⁴C has a half-life of ~5,730 years. After ~10 half-lives (~57,000 years), the remaining ¹⁴C is too small to measure reliably. It cannot date material older than ~50,000–60,000 years.
2. **Calibration:** Atmospheric ¹⁴C concentrations have not been constant—nuclear weapons testing in the 1950s–1960s spiked ¹⁴C levels significantly; variations in solar activity and ocean-atmosphere exchange also shift levels. Calibration curves (using tree rings, coral, varves) are required for accurate estimates.
3. **Initial condition assumption:** The calculation assumes we know the initial ratio of ¹⁴C/¹²C at the time of death. If initial concentrations varied significantly, the estimated date shifts. For the forensic application (post-1950 material), the nuclear test spike actually *helps*—it provides a known perturbation that can pinpoint death year quite precisely.
4. **Contamination:** Carbon exchange after death (from groundwater, nearby material) can alter measured ratios and produce anomalous dates.

**For older materials (dinosaurs, deep geological time):** Other isotope systems are used—uranium-lead (U-Pb, half-life billions of years), potassium-argon (K-Ar, half-life ~1.25 billion years), rubidium-strontium. These share the basic decay logic but apply to igneous rocks that crystallize at known temperatures, not organic material.

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Question:** Define half-life in your own words.

**Model answer:** "A half-life is the amount of time it takes for exactly half of a radioactive parent isotope to decay into the stable daughter isotope. After one half-life, 50% remains. After two half-lives, 25% remains. After three, 12.5%, and so on."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for: time-based definition + the 50%-per-period concept. Deduct 2 pts if student confuses half-life with full decay time.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Graph Annotation (15 min)

Distribute the two graphs.

**Graph 1 — Idealized ¹⁴C decay curve:**
- X-axis: time since death (years); Y-axis: ¹⁴C/¹²C ratio.
- Students annotate: mark the ratio at 0 years (organism just died), at 5,730 years (one half-life: half remains), at 11,460 years (two half-lives: quarter remains), and the approximate limit of reliable detection (~50,000 years).

Say:
> "This is the theoretical curve—what we'd see if atmospheric ¹⁴C had been perfectly constant throughout history. Notice how the curve flattens out near the bottom. Once the remaining ¹⁴C is too small to measure accurately, you can't get a reliable date. That's why ¹⁴C tops out around 50,000 years."

**Graph 2 — Post-1950 nuclear test spike:**
- Students annotate: mark the spike peak (approximately 1963–1964; about double the pre-test atmospheric ¹⁴C level).
- Note the decline after the Limited Test Ban Treaty (1963) stopped atmospheric testing.

Say:
> "Nuclear weapons testing essentially doubled atmospheric ¹⁴C in the early 1960s. Any living thing that was alive during that period has elevated ¹⁴C in its tissues compared to the pre-industrial baseline. This means if you try to date a piece of wood from 1965 using the original calibration curve, you'll get a nonsensical answer—it looks 'younger than today.' Calibration curves correct for this."

**Forensic application:** ¹⁴C from the bomb pulse is now used in forensic science to determine the death year of unidentified individuals. If elevated ¹⁴C is present in bone tissue, the individual was alive post-1950. The exact ratio can narrow the death year to within ~1–2 years.

### 2. Decay Simulation with Manipulatives (20 min)

**Coin method (pairs):**
1. Start with 64 coins, all heads-up (parent isotope).
2. Shake and spread. Remove all tails (decayed to daughter isotope). Record how many heads remain.
3. Repeat for 6 rounds (= 6 half-lives).
4. Graph: rounds (x-axis) vs. remaining heads (y-axis).

**Expected approximate results:**
| Half-life | Expected | Typical observed range |
|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| 0 | 64 | 64 |
| 1 | 32 | 28–36 |
| 2 | 16 | 12–20 |
| 3 | 8 | 5–12 |
| 4 | 4 | 2–7 |
| 5 | 2 | 1–4 |
| 6 | 1 | 0–3 |

Say:
> "Notice the variation in your results versus the expected curve. Real isotope decay follows the theoretical curve very precisely—far more precisely than our coin simulation—because we're dealing with trillions of atoms, not 64. The law of large numbers makes decay rates extremely reliable at scale."

Say:
> "This is also why small samples are problematic. A very old sample with very few remaining ¹⁴C atoms has more statistical uncertainty. Scientists report this as error bars, not as a single precise date."

### 3. Forensic Data Questions (25 min)

Students use their annotated graphs to answer questions.

**Sample questions and answer key:**

**Q1:** A wood sample from a medieval building gives a ¹⁴C/¹²C ratio of approximately 1/8 the modern baseline. About how old is it?
- Answer: Three half-lives (½ × ½ × ½ = ⅛). 3 × 5,730 = ~17,190 years old.

**Q2:** A forensic bone sample has elevated ¹⁴C well above the pre-1950 baseline. What does this tell you?
- Answer: The individual was alive during or after the nuclear-test era (post-1950). The specific ¹⁴C ratio can be matched to the bomb-pulse calibration curve to narrow the death year.

**Q3:** A paleontologist wants to date a dinosaur bone using ¹⁴C. What's the problem?
- Answer: Dinosaurs are estimated to be 65+ million years old. After ~10 half-lives (~57,000 years), measurable ¹⁴C is essentially gone. There would be no ¹⁴C remaining in a 65-million-year-old sample to measure. The method cannot date material that old. Other isotope systems (U-Pb, K-Ar) would be used instead.

**Q4:** A researcher gets a ¹⁴C date of 3,500 years ± 200 years. What does the ± 200 mean?
- Answer: It's an error bar reflecting the range of uncertainty in the measurement. The actual age is most likely between 3,300 and 3,700 years. It doesn't mean the method failed—it means scientists are honest about the precision limit of the measurement.

**Q5:** What assumption is built into every ¹⁴C date?
- Answer: That the initial ¹⁴C/¹²C ratio when the organism died was the same as (or predictably related to) the ratio used in the calibration curve. If past atmospheric ¹⁴C differed significantly from the calibrated values, the date estimate would be off.

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** State one strength and one limitation of carbon-14 dating in one concise paragraph.

**Model answer:**
> "Carbon-14 dating is a powerful tool for dating once-living material from the past ~50,000 years. Its strength is that radioactive decay rates are consistent and measurable under controlled conditions, making dates reproducible and reliable when proper calibration is applied. Its limitation is range: after approximately 10 half-lives (~57,000 years), remaining ¹⁴C is too small to measure accurately, making it useless for ancient geological samples. Additionally, the method assumes that initial atmospheric ¹⁴C concentrations are known—variations in the past (from solar activity, ocean-atmosphere exchange, or nuclear testing) require calibration corrections."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for one specific strength (decay rate consistency, reproducibility, calibration-corrected accuracy) + one specific limitation (range, initial condition assumption, contamination, or calibration requirement). Deduct 2 pts for vague "it's accurate" or "it might be wrong."

**Half-life problem answer key:**

**Problem 1:** An isotope has a half-life of 10,000 years. A sample has 25% of the original parent isotope remaining. How old is it?
- 100% → 50% (1 half-life) → 25% (2 half-lives). 2 × 10,000 = **20,000 years old.**

**Problem 2:** A ¹⁴C sample started with 1,200 ¹⁴C atoms. After three half-lives, how many remain?
- 1,200 → 600 → 300 → 150. **150 atoms remain.**

**Problem 3:** A sample gives a ¹⁴C/¹²C ratio of 1/16 the modern baseline. How many half-lives have passed?
- ½ = 1; ¼ = 2; ⅛ = 3; 1/16 = **4 half-lives.** Age = 4 × 5,730 = **22,920 years.**

**Problem 4:** Why can't ¹⁴C be used to date a rock layer containing dinosaur fossils?
- Dinosaur fossils are ~65+ million years old. ¹⁴C has a half-life of 5,730 years. After ~10 half-lives (~57,000 years), ¹⁴C is undetectable. No ¹⁴C would remain in a sample that old. **The correct isotope system would be uranium-lead or potassium-argon**, which have half-lives in the millions to billions of years.

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Carbon dating proves the earth is billions of years old" | "Carbon-14 can only date material up to ~50,000 years old. The billions-of-years age estimates use completely different isotope systems (uranium-lead, potassium-argon) applied to igneous rocks. They're related techniques but different methods with different applicable ranges." |
| "The bomb pulse means carbon dating is broken" | "The opposite—scientists identified the bomb pulse, incorporated it into calibration curves, and now use the known perturbation as a forensic tool. Identifying and correcting for a known anomaly is what good science looks like." |
| "Error bars mean the date might be completely wrong" | "Error bars represent the range of statistical uncertainty in the measurement—not the possibility of total failure. A date of 3,500 ± 200 years means we're confident the answer is between 3,300 and 3,700 years, not that we have no idea." |
| "All isotope dating uses carbon" | "No. Carbon-14 is for organic material up to ~50,000 years. Geologists use other isotopes (U-Pb, K-Ar, Rb-Sr) for rocks and minerals over millions to billions of years. Method selection depends on what is being dated and its approximate age range." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Research uranium-lead dating. How does it differ from ¹⁴C dating? What assumptions does it require? Why is it used for zircon crystals specifically? Write a comparison paragraph.
- **Struggling learners:** Provide a half-life reference card with: the formula (remaining = initial × (½)^n, where n = number of half-lives), a completed worked example, and a simple decay table they can use as a reference during calculations.
- **ELL support:** Vocabulary card: isotope, radioactive decay, half-life, calibration, parent isotope, daughter isotope—with pronunciation guide and one-sentence definitions.
