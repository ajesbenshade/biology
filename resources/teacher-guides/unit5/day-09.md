# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 9 – 15.1 Developing the Theory of Evolution

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Colossians 1:16-17**
> "For in him all things were created: things in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible… all things have been created through him and for him. He is before all things, and in him all things hold together."

Read aloud at the start. Tie in:

Say:
> "Today we look at how human beings have tried to explain the patterns they observed in living things — how traits change, how populations differ, how variation arises. These are legitimate scientific questions, and the history of answering them is fascinating. But as we trace these ideas from Lamarck through Darwin and Wallace, anchor yourself to this: the patterns they were trying to explain are real patterns God built into creation. The question of *mechanism* — how those patterns arose — is where interpretation enters. We'll practice keeping those two things separate."

## Teacher Objectives

- Students can compare artificial and natural selection, identifying agent, timescale, directionality, and examples for each.
- Students can place Lamarck, Darwin, and Wallace correctly in the historical development of evolutionary ideas, naming the distinctive contribution of each.
- Students can sort a set of statements into "directly observed" vs. "interpretive inference" categories.

**Background for you:**

**Lamarck (1809):** Proposed that organisms change during their lifetime in response to use or disuse, and that these acquired changes are then passed to offspring (inheritance of acquired characteristics). Example: a giraffe stretching its neck reaches higher leaves → its neck gets longer → offspring inherit the longer neck. This is now known to be wrong as a mechanism — somatic changes to muscles and bones are not heritable at the genetic level. However, Lamarck deserves credit for recognizing that species are not fixed and that change over time occurs — a major conceptual shift.

**Darwin and Wallace (1858-1859):** Independently proposed natural selection as the mechanism for change. Key insight: populations produce far more offspring than can survive (Malthus's influence on Darwin); individuals vary; some variations are heritable; individuals with favorable variations are more likely to survive and reproduce. Over time this shifts the population. Darwin had over 20 years of evidence when he published *On the Origin of Species* (1859). Wallace reached the same conclusion independently while doing fieldwork in the Malay Archipelago and wrote Darwin a letter — this prompted Darwin to finally publish.

**What is a scientific theory?** Students often confuse "theory" with "guess." A scientific theory is a well-supported explanatory framework that organizes a large body of evidence. It is more than a hypothesis. The theory of evolution by natural selection is well-supported at the level of observable microevolution. Whether the same framework fully explains all of life's diversity over deep time — including the origin of novel body plans — remains an area of active research and legitimate scientific discussion.

**Artificial selection as an analogy:** Darwin himself used pigeon breeding extensively as evidence that selection can produce dramatic morphological change. He bred pigeons himself. The analogy: if *directed human selection* can produce all the variety among domestic pigeons from a common ancestor in a few hundred years, perhaps *undirected environmental selection* could produce even greater diversity over millions of years. The analogy is persuasive but not proof — it shows selection can change populations rapidly; it does not demonstrate that undirected selection can produce novel body plans.

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Artificial selection is a real, observed process that shows traits can change dramatically under directed selection. Dogs from wolves, corn from teosinte — these are documented. This is data."
- "The analogy from artificial to natural selection is Darwin's argument. It is persuasive. But an analogy is not proof — an analogy says 'if this, then perhaps that.' It doesn't demonstrate that undirected natural selection over deep time produces all of life's diversity."
- "Lamarck was wrong about the mechanism (inheritance of acquired traits) but right that species change over time. Good science corrects its mechanisms while retaining what was right."
- "We delight in pondering God's works (Psalm 111:2). Studying the history of how humans figured out selection — with wrong turns, corrections, and gradual improvement — is part of delighting in a world orderly enough to be figured out."

## Bell Work Answer Key (10 min)

**Prompt:** "How might humans have influenced wolf traits over time?" (Textbook Predict prompt from 15.1 opener.)

| Level | Answer |
|-------|--------|
| Basic | "Breeding the calmer ones to get dogs." |
| Good | "By choosing to breed wolves with calmer, more submissive behavior, humans selected for docility — and over generations, those traits became dominant, producing domesticated dogs." |
| Strong | "Artificial selection: humans chose which individuals to breed based on desired traits (temperament, size, coat). Only selected individuals reproduced, so their alleles increased in frequency. The analogy to natural selection is: the environment acts as the selecting agent instead of humans — but the mechanism (differential reproduction based on heritable variation) is the same." |

**Grading:** 1 pt. Award the point for connecting artificial selection to selection plus at least one specific trait.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Scripture + Introduction (5 min)

Read Colossians 1:16-17 aloud. Say:
> "All things hold together in Christ. The orderly patterns — variation, inheritance, differential survival — are patterns God built into creation. Today we look at how humans figured out those patterns. We'll be honest about what the evidence shows and where the interpretation begins."

### 2. Artificial vs. Natural Selection Compare Chart (20 min)

Build on whiteboard together. Call on students for contributions.

**Completed chart:**

| | Artificial Selection | Natural Selection |
|-|---------------------|------------------|
| **Agent** | Human breeder (intentional choice) | Environment (differential survival/reproduction) |
| **Timescale** | Fast — visible in years to decades | Slower — typically generations to centuries at minimum (though bedbug case is fast) |
| **Direction** | Directed toward a human goal | No goal — whatever survives reproduces |
| **Source of variation** | Pre-existing variation + mutation | Pre-existing variation + mutation |
| **Example** | Dog breeds from wolf ancestor; pigeon breed variety; corn from teosinte | Antibiotic resistance in bacteria; DDT resistance in bedbugs; finch beak size shifts during drought |

Say after completing the chart:
> "Notice what both have in common: they require variation to already exist, they require that variation to be heritable, and they require differential reproduction based on that variation. Darwin's key insight was that these three conditions — variation, heritability, differential reproduction — are sufficient to produce change in populations over time."

Say:
> "Now the analogy question: Darwin argued that if *directed* human selection can produce all the variety among pigeons in a few hundred years, then *undirected* natural selection over millions of years could produce even greater diversity. Is that a proof? Or an analogy?"

Expected response: Analogy — it makes the mechanism *plausible*, but doesn't demonstrate that unguided selection can produce novel body plans.

> "Right. That distinction — between 'plausible analogy' and 'demonstrated evidence' — is exactly what we've been practicing."

### 3. Historical Ideas Timeline (15 min)

Write three names on the board: **Lamarck / Darwin / Wallace**

**Quick profile for each (fill in together):**

| Thinker | Year | Key Idea | What was right | What was wrong (or incomplete) |
|---------|------|---------|----------------|-------------------------------|
| Lamarck | 1809 | Use/disuse; inheritance of acquired characteristics | Species change over time; organisms respond to environment | Mechanism wrong — somatic changes not heritable |
| Darwin | 1859 | Natural selection; variation + heritability + differential survival | Mechanism correct; well-evidenced for observable change | Deep-time extrapolation; origin of novel genetic information still debated |
| Wallace | 1858 | Same mechanism as Darwin, reached independently | Showed the idea was discoverable from field data alone | Same scope limitations as Darwin |

Say:
> "What's historically significant about Wallace? He wasn't in Darwin's intellectual circle — he was doing fieldwork in Malaysia. He reached the same conclusion independently from different observations. When two different scientists, using different data sets, converge on the same mechanism, that is evidence the mechanism is real."

> "What's worth noting about Lamarck? He was wrong about the mechanism. But he was right that species aren't fixed. Science corrects its mechanisms while retaining what was right. That's how it's supposed to work — and it's a reason to trust the process even when individual claims are revised."

### 4. Evidence Sort Activity (20 min)

Groups of 3 sort 12 statement cards into two piles: **Directly Observed** (O) or **Interpretive Inference** (I).

**Answer key:**

| Statement | Category | Reason |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| "Beak sizes vary across Galápagos finch populations" | O | Directly measured in wild populations |
| "Dog breeds were produced from wolf ancestors by human selection" | O | Documented historical record; genetic evidence |
| "All birds share a common ancestor" | I | Inferred from shared traits and molecular similarity; not directly observed |
| "Lamarck observed that organisms appeared adapted to their environments" | O | Observational — he looked at organisms |
| "Natural selection over millions of years produced all vertebrate diversity" | I | Deep-time historical claim; not directly observed |
| "Some finch beaks were better suited to available seeds during drought" | O | Measured by Grant et al. in field studies |
| "Finches diversified from a single ancestral population that reached the Galápagos" | I | Inferred from geographic distribution and genetics |
| "Corn can be bred from teosinte by selective breeding" | O | Directly demonstrated in agriculture |
| "Darwin bred pigeons and observed trait changes over generations" | O | Historical record — Darwin kept pigeons |
| "All tetrapods descended from a common fish-like ancestor" | I | Fossil + molecular inference; historical claim |
| "Antibiotic resistance increases in bacterial populations under antibiotic exposure" | O | Directly measured in laboratory and clinical settings |
| "Natural selection acting on variation over deep time can produce new phyla" | I | Extrapolation from observed micro-level mechanism |

**Debrief (5 min):** Share any disputed cards. Key discussion: Cards 5 and 12 often generate the most disagreement — they sound like they follow logically from the observed evidence, but they involve historical and timescale claims not directly measured.

Say:
> "Strong science separates data from worldview framing. A statement can sound scientific — use the right vocabulary — and still be an interpretive inference rather than a direct observation. Your job as a critical thinker is to notice which column each claim belongs in."

### 5. Word Wall Build (10 min)

Students write definitions + examples for all six terms on index cards. Post completed cards.

**Definitions:**
- **Theory:** A well-supported explanatory framework that organizes a large body of evidence. More than a hypothesis.
- **Variation:** Differences in traits among individuals in a population.
- **Inheritance:** The passing of traits from parents to offspring through heritable genetic material.
- **Selection:** Differential survival or reproduction based on heritable traits.
- **Artificial selection:** Selection directed by human choice toward a desired trait.
- **Natural selection:** Selection directed by environmental factors — individuals with favorable traits are more likely to survive and reproduce.

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Write one paragraph identifying one piece of evidence from 15.1 and one assumption built into a 15.1 claim.

**Model answer:**
> "Evidence: Darwin bred pigeons and documented that selective breeding can produce dramatic morphological differences — large fancy tails, unusual plumage, different body sizes — from a common ancestor in a relatively short time. This is directly observed. Assumption: Darwin's argument extends this to claim that natural selection, without a directing agent, over millions of years, can produce all of life's diversity. That extension requires assuming that (1) the rates of change observed under intense artificial selection apply to slower natural selection, (2) natural selection can generate novel genetic information, and (3) the same mechanism that shifts beak size can produce fundamentally new body plans. These are interpretive extrapolations, not measurements from the pigeon experiments themselves."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for: one specific piece of evidence named + one specific assumption that goes beyond the evidence + the word "assumption" or equivalent. Deduct 2 pts for vague "some evidence shows this."

## Homework

Homework is practice only. Check for completion and give feedback, but do not record points.

| Assignment | Suggested completion look-fors |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| 15.1 guided notes + vocabulary review | All six terms defined accurately; guided note blanks complete; at least one example per term |
| Reflection: one strength and one limitation of the analogy evidence | Strength: shows selection can change populations rapidly; Limitation: directed analogy ≠ demonstration that undirected process produces same results; names the specific gap |

**Strong reflection example:**
> "Strength: The artificial selection analogy is genuinely powerful evidence that selection can produce dramatic variation from a common ancestral population in a short time — dog breeds from wolves, corn from teosinte, pigeons with elaborate traits. This shows the mechanism of selection is real and effective when applied with intensity. Limitation: The analogy involves a *directing agent* — a human making intentional choices. Natural selection has no director. Whether an undirected process achieves the same outcomes over longer timescales is an extrapolation, not a demonstration. The analogy shows it's *plausible*; it does not show it *happened*. That distinction matters for evaluating the strength of the evidence."

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Selection proves evolution is true" | "Selection demonstrates a mechanism for change within populations — measurably true for microevolution. Whether that mechanism, over deep time, produced all of life's diversity (macroevolution) is the extrapolation claim. Don't skip from the evidence to the full claim without naming the interpretive step." |
| "Darwin invented evolution" | "Darwin proposed *natural selection* as the mechanism. Lamarck, among others, had already argued species change over time. Darwin's distinctive contribution was the mechanism — plus two decades of detailed evidence supporting it." |
| "Theory = guess" | "A scientific theory is a well-tested, well-supported explanatory framework that organizes a large body of evidence. 'Theory of gravity' doesn't mean we're guessing about gravity. 'Theory of evolution' means this is the best-supported framework we have for explaining biological change. It can be revised when evidence demands it — that's a feature, not a weakness." |
| "Lamarck was just wrong and irrelevant" | "Lamarck was wrong about the mechanism, but right that species change over time. Honoring the history of science means crediting what was right and explaining why the mechanism was revised — not dismissing the whole contribution." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Research "epigenetics" — the field that studies heritable changes not encoded in DNA sequence (e.g., methylation patterns that can be passed to offspring). Is this a modern vindication of Lamarck's intuition? Is it the same mechanism Lamarck proposed? Write a one-page evaluation.
- **Struggling learners:** Pre-filled compare chart with all row headers and three examples already placed. Students add one more example per column. Evidence sort cards color-coded: blue for observation-type statements, red for inference-type — students confirm the sort rather than building it from scratch.
- **ELL support:** Vocabulary card with: artificial selection, natural selection, variation, inheritance, theory, heritability — with pronunciation guide, one-sentence definitions, and one concrete image per term.
