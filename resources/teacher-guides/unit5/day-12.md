# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 12 – National Geographic Explorer: Christopher C. Austin and Green-Blooded Skinks

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

Read aloud at the start. Tie in:

Say:
> "Today we look at one of the most remarkable biochemical discoveries in recent vertebrate biology: lizards in New Guinea with blood so green it colors their bones, muscles, and tissues. ## Teacher Objectives

- Students can summarize Christopher Austin's findings on green-blooded skinks: what is observed, how it was measured, and what is currently hypothesized.
- Students can populate a hypothesis matrix for at least two competing explanations, identifying the evidence that supports each and what additional evidence would be needed to decide between them.
- Students can distinguish a supported claim from an open question in a case where evidence is incomplete.

**Background for you:**

**The discovery:** Green-blooded lizards in the genus *Prasinohaema* (and related genera) in New Guinea have blood, muscle, tongue, and bone tissue colored bright green. The green pigment is biliverdin — a bile pigment produced as a breakdown product of hemoglobin. In mammals, high biliverdin levels cause jaundice and are toxic. These lizards have biliverdin concentrations approximately 40 times higher than the threshold at which biliverdin becomes toxic in humans — yet they are healthy.

**Christopher Austin's work:** Austin (LSU) sequenced the genomes of multiple green-blooded and non-green-blooded skink species across New Guinea and produced a phylogenetic analysis showing that the green blood trait evolved independently at least four to six times in separate skink lineages — it was not inherited once from a shared ancestor. This is a striking case of convergent evolution (or, from a common-design perspective, repeated creative use of the same solution).

**Current hypotheses for the function of high biliverdin:**
1. **Malaria/parasite protection:** Biliverdin has demonstrated antiparasitic properties in laboratory settings. The geographic distribution of green-blooded skinks overlaps substantially with regions of high *Plasmodium* (malaria-causing parasite) prevalence. This is the leading hypothesis. Evidence: geographic overlap + in vitro biliverdin inhibition of *Plasmodium* growth.
2. **Protection against other blood parasites:** Similar logic — biliverdin may suppress other parasites beyond malaria.
3. **Camouflage:** Green tissues might reduce visibility when lizards are injured and bleeding. This hypothesis has limited support and is considered less plausible — other camouflage mechanisms are far more efficient.
4. **Incidental byproduct / no current function:** High biliverdin might be a metabolic byproduct of another process that is currently adaptive. Less parsimonious given its independent evolution.

**What remains open:** No in vivo study has yet confirmed that green-blooded skinks have measurably lower parasite loads than non-green-blooded relatives in the same environment. The in vitro data is promising; the field data is not yet published. Austin himself acknowledges the hypothesis is supported but not yet proven.

**Note for teachers:** This topic appeared in Day 6 as an introduction (zebra/skink comparison). Today's lesson expands the case using Austin's specific findings and research approach. If students remember the Day 6 discussion, that's excellent background — build on it explicitly.

Source from the textbook's National Geographic Explorer feature; supplement with class-generated notes from Day 6 if available.

- **Evidence quality scale (on board or printed):** Same 1–5 scale used in Day 6.
- **Optional:** Display one or two color images of green-blooded skinks — the vivid green of the blood, tongue, and bone tissue is striking and motivating. Austin's published photographs are striking.

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Prompt:** "What might green blood do for a lizard?"

This is an open prediction — accept any scientifically plausible idea. The goal is activating prior knowledge and generating hypotheses before the reading.

**Possible student responses (all acceptable if reasoned):**
- Camouflage when injured
- Signals to predators (warning coloration)
- Parasite resistance
- Thermoregulation
- Response to specific diet component

**Grading:** 5 pts for any prediction with one sentence of reasoning. Full credit does not require the "correct" hypothesis — the habit of predicting before reading is the goal.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Read-Through and Annotation (15 min)

Distribute case handout. Students read independently (10 min), then annotate (5 min):
- **Square** every claim that is directly measured (biliverdin concentration levels, geographic overlap, in vitro inhibition results, number of independent lineages).
- **Circle** every claim that is inferred or hypothesized (biliverdin protects against malaria, the trait evolved for parasite resistance).
- **Question mark** next to anything that is not yet tested or where "we don't know yet" applies.

**Key annotation targets:**

| Statement | Category | Reason |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| "Biliverdin concentration in these skinks is ~40× the mammalian toxic threshold" | Direct measurement (square) | Measurable via blood chemistry analysis |
| "The trait evolved independently in at least 4–6 lineages" | Direct measurement (square) | Result of phylogenetic analysis of sequenced genomes |
| "Green-blooded skinks inhabit high-malaria regions" | Direct measurement (square) | Geographic distribution mapped against malaria data |
| "Biliverdin inhibits *Plasmodium* growth in laboratory conditions" | Direct measurement (square) | In vitro assay — measurable |
| "High biliverdin evolved to protect against malaria" | Hypothesis (circle) | No in vivo confirmation yet; in vitro ≠ in vivo |
| "Green-blooded skinks have fewer malaria infections than non-green relatives" | ? (question mark) | Not yet demonstrated in published field data |

Say after annotation:
> "Austin's team has excellent in vitro data and strong geographic correlation. What they don't yet have — what would decisively support the malaria hypothesis — is field data showing that green-blooded skinks actually have lower parasite loads than non-green relatives living in the same high-malaria habitat. That's the experiment still needed. Good science names what it knows and names what it still needs."

### 2. Hypothesis Matrix (20 min)

Students fill in the matrix for at least two hypotheses (malaria protection is required; students choose at least one more).

**Answer key:**

**Hypothesis 1 — Malaria/parasite protection:**

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Hypothesis | High biliverdin levels protect green-blooded skinks from malaria (*Plasmodium*) infection. |
| Supporting evidence | Geographic overlap with high-malaria regions (correlation); in vitro demonstration that biliverdin inhibits *Plasmodium* growth (mechanistic). |
| Evidence quality | 4 — strong correlation + preliminary mechanistic data; needs in vivo confirmation. |
| Evidence still needed | Field study comparing parasite loads in green-blooded vs. non-green-blooded skinks in the same habitat; dose-response data confirming biliverdin concentration in blood is sufficient to inhibit in vivo infection. |

**Hypothesis 2 — Camouflage/warning coloration:**

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Hypothesis | Green tissues function as camouflage or warning coloration to deter predators. |
| Supporting evidence | Green tissues are extensive — bone, muscle, tongue; green could blend in forest vegetation. |
| Evidence quality | 1–2 — observational; no experimental test; less parsimonious than parasite hypothesis given that dedicated camouflage mechanisms (skin color) are more efficient and already present. |
| Evidence still needed | Predator avoidance experiments comparing attack rates on green-blooded vs. non-green-blooded skinks; demonstration that internal tissue color is visible to relevant predators. |

**Hypothesis 3 — Metabolic byproduct / no current adaptive function:**

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Hypothesis | High biliverdin is a byproduct of another adaptive metabolic process and has no specific adaptive function of its own. |
| Supporting evidence | None directly; this is the null hypothesis. |
| Evidence quality | 1 — no direct support. Less parsimonious given independent evolution in 4–6 lineages: if it were a neutral byproduct, why maintain it across unrelated groups? |
| Evidence still needed | Identification of the primary metabolic process that produces the biliverdin as a byproduct; demonstration that removing biliverdin has no effect on fitness. |

Say after students complete the matrix:
> "Notice how the quality scores differ. Hypothesis 1 scores a 4 because it has two types of converging evidence — geographic correlation and mechanistic in vitro data. Hypothesis 2 scores a 1–2 because it's an observation without an experimental test. Hypothesis 3 scores a 1 because it has no direct support and is made less plausible by the pattern of independent evolution. Better-supported hypotheses have more evidence types converging on the same conclusion."

### 3. Evidence Quality Discussion (20 min)

Class discussion:

- "What is the single strongest piece of evidence in this case? Why?"
- "What is the one test that would most clearly confirm or eliminate the malaria hypothesis?"
- "Why does independent evolution in 4–6 lineages matter for evaluating the hypotheses?"

**Expected discussion points:**

*Strongest evidence:* The in vitro biliverdin inhibition of *Plasmodium* growth — because it goes beyond correlation to a mechanistic test. The geographic overlap alone could be coincidental (confounded by other environmental variables). The in vitro test shows *how* biliverdin could work.

*Most crucial next test:* Field survey comparing parasite loads (measured by blood smear or PCR assay for *Plasmodium* DNA) in green-blooded vs. non-green-blooded skinks caught in the same high-malaria habitat. This would be a direct test of the prediction: if high biliverdin protects against malaria, green-blooded skinks should have lower infection rates.

*Why independent evolution matters:* If the trait arose once and was inherited, it could be a neutral byproduct of ancestral physiology. But if it arose independently 4–6 times in separate lineages, under similar environmental pressures (high malaria regions), natural selection is a much more plausible explanation than chance — something about having high biliverdin must be providing a selective advantage. The convergence strengthens the adaptive hypothesis.

Say:
> "This is one of the most exciting things about the green-blooded skink story: Austin's work isn't finished. He has strong evidence pointing toward the malaria hypothesis, but the field confirmation hasn't been published as of our class. This is science in progress — not a textbook fact, but an active investigation. That's what real science looks like: good hypotheses, strong preliminary evidence, and more tests still to be done."

### 4. Synthesis Write (10 min)

Students write a short response (one paragraph): one claim that is well-supported by Austin's evidence + one open question that remains unresolved.

**Model synthesis response:**
> "Austin's research provides well-supported evidence that the high biliverdin concentrations in green-blooded skinks are likely adaptive rather than incidental. Specifically: biliverdin inhibits *Plasmodium* growth in laboratory conditions (direct mechanistic evidence), the trait has evolved independently in at least four to six separate lineages (suggesting repeated positive selection), and these lineages inhabit regions with high malaria pressure (geographic correlation). Together, these three converging lines of evidence make the parasite-protection hypothesis the most parsimonious explanation. However, the open question is whether green-blooded skinks actually have lower malaria infection rates than non-green relatives living in the same habitats — this field test has not yet been published. A hypothesis supported by in vitro data and geographic correlation is promising, but in vivo confirmation is still required before the case can be considered settled."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for: one supported claim with at least two specific evidence types cited + one open question with an explanation of what kind of evidence would resolve it.

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Name one claim Austin's research strongly supports and one question that remains open. One sentence each.

**Model:** 
- Supported claim: "The green blood trait evolved independently multiple times in separate skink lineages — this is supported by Austin's phylogenetic analysis of sequenced genomes."
- Open question: "Whether green-blooded skinks actually have lower malaria infection rates than non-green relatives in the same habitat remains unconfirmed by field data."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for one specific claim with evidence type named + one specific open question. Deduct 2 pts for vague "more research is needed" without specifying what research and what it would show.

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Guided questions on Austin's case study | 10 pts | Answers accurately cite data from case handout; observation/inference distinction maintained; at least one "still needed" test named |
| Paragraph on supported vs. unsupported claims | 5 pts | One specific supported claim with 2+ evidence types; one specific unsupported/open claim with explanation of type of evidence missing |

**Guided question answer expectations:**

*Q: Why does independent evolution in multiple lineages strengthen the adaptive hypothesis?*
> "If the trait were a neutral byproduct with no function, we'd expect it to arise rarely and be maintained inconsistently. Independent evolution in 4–6 lineages under similar environmental pressures (malaria-endemic habitats) strongly suggests the trait is being maintained by selection — something about having high biliverdin is providing a consistent advantage across different skink lineages."

*Q: What is the specific evidence quality weakness in the current case for malaria protection?*
> "The mechanistic evidence (in vitro inhibition) is laboratory-based, not field-based. In vitro conditions differ from in vivo — biliverdin concentration in the blood may behave differently against parasites in a living organism. Additionally, geographic overlap is correlation — other variables (diet, habitat type, co-occurring species) also correlate with both green-bloodedness and malaria prevalence. Without a controlled field study comparing infection rates in green vs. non-green skinks in the same environment, the causal claim (green blood protects against malaria) remains an inference."

**Rubric for paragraph:**
- Evidence specificity (70%): Names specific evidence types (phylogenetic analysis, in vitro data, geographic correlation) rather than vague "Austin found things."
- Intellectual caution (30%): Avoids "proves"; uses "supports," "is consistent with," "suggests"; names open question explicitly.

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Convergent evolution proves evolution" | "Convergence — the same trait arising independently in multiple lineages — is consistent with both evolutionary selection and common design. For evolutionary biology, it's actually somewhat surprising that identical solutions arise independently rather than being inherited. For common design, it's expected that a Creator would use the same efficient solution in multiple contexts. The convergence pattern is data; which explanation is correct is interpretation." |
| "In vitro = proven" | "In vitro means 'in glass' — in a laboratory test tube or dish. It is real experimental data. But in vivo (in a living organism) often behaves differently because of metabolic interactions, blood chemistry, immune responses, and other factors. In vitro is promising; in vivo is confirmation." |
| "Austin's hypothesis is proven" | "Austin has strong evidence for the malaria hypothesis — geographic correlation and in vitro mechanistic support. He does not yet have field confirmation (in vivo parasite load comparison). Good science distinguishes 'strongly supported hypothesis' from 'confirmed conclusion.' Austin himself makes that distinction." |
| "Green blood is just weird — it doesn't do anything" | "The pattern of independent evolution (4–6 times in similar environments) is exactly the kind of evidence that makes 'random byproduct' an implausible explanation. When the same expensive trait evolves independently in multiple lineages under similar pressures, selection is the most parsimonious mechanism. 'It doesn't do anything' would require explaining why selection didn't eliminate the costly biliverdin accumulation." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Find one published paper by Christopher Austin or his collaborators on green-blooded skinks. Read the abstract and methods section. What specific measurements did they take? What controls did they use? What did they conclude? What did they acknowledge as limitations? Write a one-page lab report evaluation.
- **Struggling learners:** Case handout with key sentences pre-highlighted in three colors: yellow = direct observation, blue = hypothesis, green = open question. Hypothesis matrix pre-filled with Hypothesis 1 (malaria protection) fully completed as an example; students complete Hypothesis 2 independently.
- **ELL support:** Vocabulary card: biliverdin, in vitro, in vivo, convergent evolution, phylogenetic analysis, hypothesis, parasite. Case summary available as a simplified one-page version with shorter sentences and bolded key terms.
