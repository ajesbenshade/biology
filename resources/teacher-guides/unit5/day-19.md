# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 19 – Sustainability and Future Outlook

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains teacher background, scenario-analysis supports, and grading notes.

## Scripture Focus

**Revelation 11:18 + Genesis 2:15**

Tie in:
> “Stewardship includes long-term obedience. Sustainable living is not worship of the earth; it is wise care for what belongs to God.”

## Teacher Objectives

- Define sustainability.
- Evaluate sustainable practices using evidence.
- Analyze future biodiversity scenarios.
- Connect long-term planning to biblical wisdom and stewardship.

## Background for the Teacher

A common definition of **sustainability** is meeting present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs.

Students may hear the “triple bottom line” language of people, planet, and profit. A biblical framing can keep that language properly ordered:
- people matter because they bear God’s image
- creation matters because it belongs to God
- wise provision matters because stewardship requires prudence

Examples of sustainable practices:
- renewable energy use
- sustainable agriculture
- reduced waste
- habitat corridors
- careful fisheries and forestry management

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- “Long-term thinking reflects wisdom.”
- “Sustainability can be a form of neighbor love and stewardship.”
- “We do not worship the earth; we care for it as God’s possession.”

## Materials & Preparation

- Sustainability scenario handout.
- Resource-use practice questions.
- Optional chart showing optimistic vs. pessimistic biodiversity projections.

## Bell Work (8 min)

**Prompt:** What does sustainability mean?

**Model answer:**
> “Using resources in ways that meet needs now without damaging the ability of future generations to meet their needs.”

**Scoring:** 5 pts for effort.

## Lesson Delivery (70 min)

### 1. Definition and Examples (15 min)

Teach the basic definition, then give 2–3 examples.

Suggested script:
> “Sustainability asks whether a pattern of living can continue without careless depletion or long-term damage.”

### 2. Scenario Analysis (25 min)

Groups evaluate scenarios such as:
- high conservation cooperation
- moderate policy response
- continued overuse and weak protection

Ask:
- What evidence suggests a better outcome?
- What human choices most affect the projection?
- What assumptions are built into the scenario?

### 3. Sustainable Practice Brainstorm (20 min)

Class list may include:
- reducing waste
- restoring habitat
- using land more carefully
- protecting breeding grounds
- designing corridors between fragmented habitats

Teacher point:
> “Sustainability is not abstract. It becomes visible in concrete practices.”

### 4. Wrap-Up (10 min)

Ask:
> “How can present choices honor or dishonor the Creator?”

## Homework Grading

- **Scenario questions** — 10 pts
- **One sustainable action plan** — 5 pts

**Rubric:**
- Evidence-based reasoning: 50%
- Biblical connection: 50%

## Common Confusions and Fixes

- **“Sustainability means worshiping the earth.”**  
  Fix: “No. Christians worship God and steward His world.”
- **“Sustainable choices make no real difference.”**  
  Fix: “At multiple scales, long-term choices influence ecosystems and human communities.”
- **“Only governments matter.”**  
  Fix: “Policy matters, but personal, local, and institutional practices matter too.”

## Differentiation

- **Struggling learners:** use a guided organizer for each scenario.
- **Advanced learners:** assign a written critique of one scenario’s assumptions.
- **ELL support:** vocabulary bank for sustainability, renewable, corridor, projection, resource.

## Prayer Close

> “Lord, help us live wisely and sustainably for Your glory, thinking beyond ourselves and acting as faithful stewards.”
