# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 6 – Explorer Case Studies: Zebra Stripes and Green-Blooded Skinks

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

**Job 12:7-10 / Psalm 19:1**
> "But ask the animals, and they will teach you, or the birds in the sky, and they will tell you… In his hand is the life of every living creature." (Job 12:7-10)
> "The heavens declare the glory of God; the skies proclaim the work of his hands." (Psalm 19:1)

Read both aloud at the start. Tie in:

Say:
> "Today we look at two animals that researchers have studied carefully in the field: zebras and green-blooded skinks. Both raise the same core scientific question we've been practicing all unit — what do the measurements actually show, and what are we inferring beyond the data? Good field science means observing carefully, testing hypotheses with controls, and being honest about what remains open even after the data is in."

Job 12:7 is an invitation to serious study of living things as a way of understanding creation's wisdom. These case studies model what that looks like when done with rigor.

## Teacher Objectives

- Students can fill out a hypothesis matrix (Claim | Test Design | Evidence Outcome | Evidence Quality) for two real biological case studies.
- Students can evaluate and rank evidence quality on a defined scale.
- Students can identify convergent traits and discuss whether convergent evolution or common design is a better fit for the evidence.

**Background for you:**

**Zebra stripes:** Multiple hypotheses have been proposed over the last 150 years — camouflage, heat regulation, social signaling, predator avoidance, and fly repulsion. The Larison et al. (2015, *Royal Society Open Science*) study found the strongest correlation between stripe density and biting fly density across zebra species ranges — denser stripes in regions with more horseflies and tsetse flies. Tim Caro's experimental work (2019) had domesticated horses in flyproof coats with striped or plain panels — flies landed and bit far more often on plain panels. The fly-repulsion hypothesis currently has the strongest experimental support. Stripe-thermoregulation (stripes create micro air currents) and stripe-social-signaling hypotheses have weaker or mixed evidence.

**Green-blooded skinks (Prasinohaema):** These lizards in New Guinea have green blood due to extremely high concentrations of biliverdin — a bile pigment that is typically toxic at these levels in vertebrates. The trait appears to have evolved independently at least four times in different skink lineages (verified by phylogenetic analysis). Current hypotheses: protection against malaria (in regions with high Plasmodium parasite pressure), protection against other blood parasites, or unknown function. This is one of the most striking examples of convergent traits in vertebrates.

**Convergence:** Evolutionary biology explains the independent appearance of similar traits in unrelated lineages as convergent evolution — similar selection pressures favoring the same solution. Common-design advocates point to convergence as evidence against a pure ancestry model: if two completely unrelated lineages independently develop the same complex trait, why couldn't a Designer produce the same trait in unrelated groups? Both explanations are worth surfacing.

## Materials & Preparation

- **Hypothesis matrix template (one per student):** Four columns — Claim | Test Design | Evidence Outcome | Evidence Quality (1–5 scale). Two rows for zebra case; two rows for skink case.
- **Zebra case data summary (printed or displayed):** Include Larison et al. correlation data (stripe density vs. biting fly density across geographic range) and Caro et al. experimental data (fly landing/biting rates on striped vs. plain horse coat panels).
- **Skink case data summary:** Include biliverdin concentration levels (skinks: 40× higher than typical toxic threshold in mammals), geographic distribution overlapping with malaria-endemic regions, and phylogenetic branching showing independent trait acquisition.
- **Evidence quality scale (printed or on board):**
  - 5 = Controlled experiment; multiple replications; directly measures the claim.
  - 4 = Strong correlation; multiple lines of evidence converging.
  - 3 = Consistent correlation; one study; confounds possible.
  - 2 = Observational match; no experimental control.
  - 1 = Plausible but no direct test.
- **Optional:** Short video clip notes sheet if you show a National Geographic segment on zebra stripe research. Timed clips (2–3 min) work well for the phenomenon launch.

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Question:** "What makes a hypothesis strong?"

| Level | Answer |
|-------|--------|
| Basic | "It can be tested." |
| Good | "It is testable and can be proven wrong (falsifiable). It makes specific predictions." |
| Strong | "A strong hypothesis is testable with a controlled experiment, falsifiable (specifies what would disprove it), makes specific measurable predictions, and is grounded in prior evidence. A correlation study begins testing it; a controlled experiment with isolated variables provides stronger evidence." |

**Grading:** 1 pt. Award the point for identifying that a strong hypothesis is testable, falsifiable, and grounded in evidence.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Case Launch: Zebra Stripes (5 min)

Display or distribute an image of a zebra herd. Ask for 2–3 student predictions:
- "Why might zebras have stripes? What problem are stripes solving?"

Record predictions on board without evaluating them. Students will return to these at the end.

Say:
> "Evolutionary biologists have proposed at least five hypotheses for zebra stripes over the last 150 years. Today we're going to evaluate two of them with actual data — and we're going to rate the quality of that evidence, not just whether the hypothesis sounds reasonable."

### 2. Zebra Hypothesis Matrix (20 min)

Students fill the matrix for two hypotheses: fly repulsion and thermoregulation.

**Matrix answer key — Zebra:**

**Hypothesis 1 — Fly Repulsion:**

| Claim | Test Design | Evidence Outcome | Quality |
|-------|------------|-----------------|---------|
| Stripes deter / confuse biting flies | (Larison) Compare stripe density across zebra populations vs. biting fly density across same geographic range | Strong positive correlation: highest stripe density in regions with highest biting fly pressure | 3 — strong correlation; observational; confounds possible (other variables also correlate with latitude) |
| Stripes deter / confuse biting flies | (Caro) Dress domesticated horses in striped vs. plain coats; count fly landings and bites | Significantly fewer landings and bites on striped panels | 5 — controlled experiment; directly measured; isolated stripe variable |

**Hypothesis 2 — Thermoregulation:**

| Claim | Test Design | Evidence Outcome | Quality |
|-------|------------|-----------------|---------|
| Black/white stripes create micro air currents that cool the animal | Measure body surface temperature in striped vs. non-striped areas; test for air current generation | Mixed/weak — some studies detect small temperature differentials; no clearly functional air currents documented | 2 — observational inconsistencies; no controlled experiment showing functional cooling |

Say:
> "Notice the difference between those two quality scores — a 5 for the Caro experiment and a 2 for thermoregulation. What makes the difference? Controls. The Caro study isolated *exactly one variable*. The thermoregulation studies measured a correlate — temperature difference — but didn't test whether that difference actually reduces metabolic heat load. Evidence quality depends on how directly the test addresses the claim."

Ask: "Does the strong fly-repulsion evidence mean that's the *only* reason for stripes? Could stripes serve multiple functions?" Expected answer: Yes — traits can be multifunctional. Strong evidence for one function does not rule out others.

### 3. Data Walk and Evidence Ranking Debrief (10 min)

Post the four matrix rows around the room (or display sequentially). Students circulate or discuss:

- Which evidence item has the highest quality score? Why?
- What would you need to add to move the thermoregulation hypothesis from a 2 to a 4?
- What assumption is built into the geographic correlation study that the experimental study doesn't require?

Expected answers:
- Caro experiment scores highest (5) — controlled, direct measurement.
- To improve thermoregulation: need a controlled experiment where horses in striped/plain equivalent coats are measured for core body temperature in controlled heat conditions.
- Geographic correlation assumes that the same variable (fly pressure) drives stripe density — confounded by latitude, temperature, and other variables that correlate with both.

Say:
> "Strong data supports function — but causation requires controls. A correlation tells you 'these things go together.' An experiment tests 'does one cause the other.'"

### 4. Green-Blooded Skinks: Convergence Discussion (25 min)

Distribute the skink data summary. Students read (8 min) and then work in pairs to fill the matrix for two hypotheses.

**Matrix answer key — Skinks:**

**Hypothesis 1 — Malaria/parasite protection:**

| Claim | Test Design | Evidence Outcome | Quality |
|-------|------------|-----------------|---------|
| High biliverdin levels inhibit malaria parasites in blood | Geographic correlation: do green-blooded skinks live in high-malaria regions? | Yes — geographic overlap is strong | 3 — correlation; confounds possible |
| High biliverdin levels inhibit malaria parasites in blood | Laboratory assay: does biliverdin inhibit *Plasmodium* growth in vitro? | Preliminary positive data; ongoing research | 4 — direct mechanistic test; needs replication |

**Hypothesis 2 — Unknown function / byproduct:**

| Claim | Test Design | Evidence Outcome | Quality |
|-------|------------|-----------------|---------|
| High biliverdin is a metabolic byproduct with no current adaptive function | Compare metabolic byproduct pathways in green vs. non-green skinks | Not yet directly tested | 1 — plausible; no direct test available |

**Convergence discussion (12 min):**

Say:
> "Here is one of the most striking facts about this case: phylogenetic analysis shows the green blood trait arose independently in at least four separate skink lineages — it was not inherited once from a common ancestor and passed down. Each lineage developed it separately."

Write on board:
- **Evolutionary interpretation:** Convergent evolution — the same selection pressure (parasite pressure) favored the same solution (high biliverdin) independently four times. Similar environments produce similar adaptations.
- **Common-design interpretation:** A Designer can repeat the same engineering solution in unrelated groups when similar challenges require it — just as human engineers independently invent similar solutions to the same problem. Convergence may reflect design wisdom rather than (or in addition to) selection's action.

Ask:
> "Which interpretation do you find more parsimonious — that natural selection independently arrived at this exact physiological solution four separate times, or that a Creator produced the same solution in four separate groups facing similar challenges? What additional evidence would change your mind either direction?"

Discussion goal: Students recognize that convergence is genuinely surprising under a pure common-ancestry model (it requires the exact same solution to arise independently multiple times) and is a commonly cited challenge to that model. Common design handles convergence more easily. Neither interpretation is ruling out by the data — the data is the explanandum, not the explanation.

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** From one of today's case studies — name the strongest piece of evidence, give it a quality score, and explain what would change your confidence.

**Model answers:**

*Zebra:* "Strongest evidence: Caro et al. horse coat experiment — stripes significantly reduced fly landings and bites. Quality score: 5. Controlled experiment, direct measurement, isolated stripe variable. What would change my confidence: a replication in wild conditions with actual zebras, or evidence that the effect disappears when horsefly populations are absent."

*Skink:* "Strongest evidence: in vitro biliverdin assay showing *Plasmodium* inhibition. Quality score: 4. Direct mechanistic test; needs replication. What would change my confidence: repeated experiments from multiple laboratories, plus an in vivo study showing reduced parasite loads in green-blooded vs. non-green-blooded skinks in the same environment."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for: one named piece of evidence + quality score with justification + one specific additional test that would change confidence. Deduct 2 pts for vague "better evidence" without specifics.

## Homework

Homework is practice only. Check for completion and give feedback, but do not record points.

| Assignment | Suggested completion look-fors |
|-----------|-------------------------------|
| One-page comparison (evidence tables for both cases) | Both hypothesis matrices complete; quality scores present with justifications; comparison paragraph identifies which evidence type was stronger and why |
| 5 short responses on hypothesis quality | Each response connects evidence quality to specific methodological feature (control, correlation, direct vs. indirect measurement) |

**Suggested feedback focus:**
- Data use: Specific data points from case summaries cited; quality scores justified with methodological reasoning.
- Comparison depth: Explicitly identifies what distinguishes high-quality evidence from low-quality; names at least one additional test that would strengthen a weaker hypothesis.

**Strong comparison paragraph example:**
> "Both case studies illustrated the difference between correlational and experimental evidence. In the zebra case, the Larison geographic correlation had a quality of 3 because confounding variables (latitude, temperature) also correlate with both stripe density and fly pressure. The Caro experiment scored 5 because it isolated stripes as the single variable. In the skink case, the in vitro parasite assay scored 4 because it directly tested a proposed mechanism — but needs replication, and an in vivo test would be needed to confirm the effect in living animals. In both cases, the strongest evidence was the result that most directly tested the specific claim with isolated variables and direct measurement."

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Convergence proves evolution" | "Convergence is actually one of the more interesting challenges for a pure common-ancestry model — because it requires the same complex solution to arise independently multiple times. Common design also predicts convergence. What the data shows is that the same trait appears in separate lineages; what caused that is interpretation." |
| "A correlation study is worthless" | "Correlations are genuine evidence — they identify patterns worth investigating further. The key is understanding what they can and cannot show. A correlation can rule out interpretations that predict no correlation. It can suggest a mechanism. But establishing causation requires an experiment that isolates the variable." |
| "If we don't know the function, there is none" | "Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. The skink biliverdin case shows that a trait can look like a quirk before the function is identified. Science requires humility about unknown functions — which is also why 'vestigial = useless' is an overclaim." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Find the Caro et al. (2019) paper (or a published summary) on the zebra stripe experiment. What were the specific controls? What confounds remain? What is your confidence score for the conclusion that stripes deter flies? Could stripes serve multiple functions simultaneously? Write a one-page evaluation.
- **Struggling learners:** Pre-filled hypothesis matrix with the Claim column already completed for both cases. Students fill in Test Design and Evidence Outcome from the case data summary; teacher walks through quality scoring together as a class.
- **ELL support:** Vocabulary card: hypothesis, convergence, correlation, controlled experiment, falsifiable, evidence quality. Case data summary available with key sentences highlighted. Bilingual quality scale (1–5) with one-sentence descriptions of each level.
