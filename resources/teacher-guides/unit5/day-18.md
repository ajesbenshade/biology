# Teacher Guide: Unit 5 Day 18 – Human Impacts on Biodiversity + Conservation Strategies

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains teacher background, ranking activity supports, and grading criteria.

## Scripture Focus

**Genesis 1:28 + Psalm 24:1**

Tie in:
> “Dominion is responsible care. The earth is the Lord’s, so conservation is not ownership—it is stewardship.”

## Teacher Objectives

- Explain the HIPPO threats to biodiversity.
- Evaluate major conservation tools.
- Analyze a recovery case study such as the California condor.
- Connect conservation work to biblical stewardship.

## Background for the Teacher

A useful summary of major biodiversity threats is **HIPPO**:
- **H**abitat loss
- **I**nvasive species
- **P**ollution
- **P**opulation growth / human pressure
- **O**verexploitation

Conservation tools may include:
- protected areas
- captive breeding
- reintroduction programs
- habitat restoration
- sustainable harvesting and land-use practices
- policy and international cooperation

The **California condor** case is often used because it shows both damage and recovery. The species declined dramatically due to human causes including lead poisoning, habitat disruption, and pesticide-related issues. Recovery efforts included captive breeding and reintroduction.

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- “Conservation is faithful stewardship.”
- “Using knowledge to preserve what God declared good is an act of obedience.”
- “Human harm can be real, but restoration efforts can also reflect faithful care.”

## Materials & Preparation

- HIPPO handout or poster.
- Condor case summary.
- Strategy-ranking cards.
- Optional recovery graph.

## Bell Work (8 min)

**Prompt:** List one human impact on biodiversity.

Examples:
- habitat destruction
- pollution
- overhunting / overfishing
- introducing invasive species

**Scoring:** 5 pts for effort.

## Lesson Delivery (70 min)

### 1. HIPPO Overview (15 min)

Define each threat clearly with one example.

Suggested script:
> “Most biodiversity threats are not mysterious. They are tied to human choices, which means prevention and repair are possible.”

### 2. Conservation Tools Sort and Rank (25 min)

Groups rank strategies by likely effectiveness.

Possible considerations:
- cost
- speed
- long-term effect
- scope
- dependence on cooperation

Ask:
- Which tool addresses the cause instead of only the symptom?
- Which works best for already critically endangered species?
- Which requires long-term policy change?

### 3. Condor Case Study (20 min)

Students annotate the condor case:
- what caused decline?
- what actions were taken?
- what evidence shows recovery?

Teacher line:
> “Human action helped cause the decline. Human stewardship helped support recovery. That is a morally important pattern.”

### 4. Debrief (10 min)

Ask:
> “What makes conservation biblical?”

Expected points:
- God owns creation
- humans are caretakers
- wise dominion protects life rather than exploiting it carelessly

## Homework Grading

- **HIPPO and strategy questions** — 10 pts
- **Condor reflection** — 5 pts

**Rubric:**
- Accuracy: 60%
- Application: 40%

## Common Confusions and Fixes

- **“Protected areas solve everything.”**  
  Fix: “They help greatly but do not remove all threats.”
- **“Conservation is only for scientists.”**  
  Fix: “Stewardship belongs to everyone at different scales.”
- **“If humans caused harm, nothing can be reversed.”**  
  Fix: “Some damage is lasting, but many cases show meaningful recovery efforts.”

## Differentiation

- **Struggling learners:** give a HIPPO chart with examples partially filled in.
- **Advanced learners:** require a written defense of the top-ranked conservation strategy.
- **ELL support:** pre-teach conserve, restore, invasive, reintroduce, exploitation.

## Prayer Close

> “Lord, guide us to protect Your creation with wisdom, humility, and faithful stewardship.”
