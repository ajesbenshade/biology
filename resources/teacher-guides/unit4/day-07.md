# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 7 – Chapter 12 Review and Biotechnology Bridge

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Genesis 1:28**
> "God blessed them and said to them, 'Be fruitful and increase in number; fill the earth and subdue it. Rule over the fish in the sea and the birds in the sky and over every living creature that moves on the ground.'"

Frame "dominion" as responsible stewardship: biotechnology tools are extensions of our capacity to understand and care for creation. The call is to use them with humility, evidence-based reasoning, and ethical reflection—not uncritical enthusiasm or fearful rejection.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Consolidate Chapter 12 inheritance content through active review stations.
- Introduce PCR, gel electrophoresis, and CRISPR at a foundational level.
- Evaluate one biotechnology scenario using scientific evidence.

**Background for you:** Today is a transition day. The review stations reinforce Mendelian and complex inheritance before students move into biotechnology work. The biotech intro is brief and conceptual. Focus on what each tool does, what kind of data it produces, and how today's heredity review prepares students for that data work.

---

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Biotechnology tools are in a long tradition of human stewardship—using knowledge to serve. The question is not whether to use them, but how to use them responsibly."
- "Good stewardship requires evidence. We don't endorse or reject a technology based on emotion—we ask: what does the data show, and what does wisdom require?"
- When discussing CRISPR or gene editing: "We approach powerful tools with humility. God knows what He designed; we are discoverers, not creators."

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Question:** Diagnose this heredity error: "A carrier mother and unaffected father can never have a son with an X-linked disorder because the father is unaffected." What is wrong, and how would you correct it?

**Good answers:**
- "The statement ignores that sons inherit their X chromosome from the mother. If the mother is a carrier, she can pass the affected allele to a son even if the father is unaffected."
- "An unaffected father does not give an X chromosome to his sons, so his phenotype does not prevent an X-linked recessive disorder in a male child."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Award full credit for identifying the inheritance mistake and correcting the parent-of-origin logic. Deduct 2 pts for vague statements without the X-chromosome explanation.

## Review Station Answer Keys (24 min total + transitions)

**Station 1 – Punnett Square Practice**

| Cross | Expected Phenotype Ratio |
|-------|------------------------|
| Tt × Tt | 3 Tall : 1 Short |
| I^A i × I^B i | 1 AB : 1 A : 1 B : 1 O |
| X^H X^h × X^H Y | 1 normal female : 1 carrier female : 1 normal male : 1 affected male |

**Station 2 – Chi-Square Interpretation**

Present a data set (e.g., observed: 28 dominant, 12 recessive in 40 offspring; expected: 30, 10).

$\chi^2 = \frac{(28-30)^2}{30} + \frac{(12-10)^2}{10} = \frac{4}{30} + \frac{4}{10} = 0.13 + 0.40 = 0.53$

Interpretation: Small value → results are consistent with the 3:1 model; chance variation is sufficient explanation.

**Station 3 – Pedigree Clues**

| Clue | Likely Interpretation |
|------|-----------------------|
| Trait appears in every generation | Often dominant |
| Unaffected parents have an affected child | Often recessive |
| Mostly males affected | Consider X-linked recessive |
| Father passes trait to every daughter but no sons | Consider X-linked dominant |

## Lesson Delivery Script (85 min)

### 1. Bell Work and Launch (8 min)

Students diagnose the heredity error independently, then compare with a shoulder partner only after the silent work window.

Transition cue:
> "When the timer hits zero, take your paper and stand behind your station number."

### 2. Review Stations (24 min + transitions)

Use the answer keys below while circulating. Aim for three stations at about 7 minutes each.

### 3. Mini-Lesson: Biotechnology Introduction (15 min)

### PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

**What it does:** Amplifies (copies) a specific segment of DNA millions of times.
**When it's used:** Forensics, disease diagnosis, paternity testing, research.

Say:
> "PCR doesn't make new DNA from scratch—it copies existing DNA. You need a template to start. Think of it like a biological photocopier that can make millions of copies in hours."

**Three cycles:**
1. **Denaturation** (heat ~95°C) — strands separate.
2. **Annealing** (cool ~55°C) — primers attach to target sequence.
3. **Extension** (72°C) — DNA polymerase fills in the new strand.

Each cycle doubles the number of copies: 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → … → millions.

### Gel Electrophoresis

**What it does:** Separates DNA fragments by size using an electric current through a gel.

Say:
> "DNA is negatively charged, so it moves toward the positive end. Smaller fragments move farther; larger fragments move less. The result is a pattern of bands—like a barcode for DNA."

**How to read a gel:**
- Bands lower on the gel = smaller fragments
- Band position compared to a "ladder" (known size markers) tells you fragment size
- Matching band patterns = same DNA source

### CRISPR-Cas9

**What it does:** Acts as molecular scissors guided to a precise DNA target. The guide RNA leads the Cas9 protein to the correct location; Cas9 cuts both strands.

Say:
> "CRISPR can disable a gene, correct a mutation, or insert new sequences. For today, keep the big idea simple: guide RNA is the GPS and Cas9 is the scissors. We will sort through more complex ethical distinctions tomorrow once the data reading is steadier."

Transition cue:
> "Back to your seats, scenario card face down, and wait until everyone is ready before you begin the discussion."

## Guided Discussion and Checkpoint (31 min total)

**Sample prompt:** "A child is born with a rare genetic disorder affecting one gene. Scientists have developed a CRISPR treatment that could correct the mutation in body cells (somatic). What data would you want to see before recommending this treatment?"

**Evidence-based responses to guide toward:**
- Safety data: off-target edit rate in cell models and animal trials
- Efficacy data: does the correction persist? Does it reach enough cells?
- Comparison: risk of the disease vs. risk of the treatment
- Long-term follow-up data

Say: "Good decision-making about technology requires asking 'what is the evidence?' not just 'what do I feel about this?'"

Block logistics:
- 8 minutes for partner discussion.
- 5 minutes for whole-class share.
- 8 minutes for individual written reflection on what evidence would matter most.
- 10 minutes for the short checkpoint and one written reflection on a biotechnology term.

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Name one biotechnology tool and one type of data needed to evaluate its effectiveness.

**Good answers:**
- "PCR — data showing whether the target DNA segment was actually amplified (gel bands at expected size)"
- "Gel electrophoresis — band position data compared to a size ladder to confirm fragment identity"
- "CRISPR — sequencing data confirming the intended edit was made and no major off-target cuts occurred"

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for correct tool + specific data type. Deduct 2 pts for vague "data" without specifying what kind.

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Review corrections from station work | 10 pts | Errors corrected accurately; reasoning added |
| Claim-evidence-reasoning paragraph (biotech use case) | 10 pts | Claim: clear statement about a technology. Evidence: specific data type cited. Reasoning: explains why evidence supports claim. |

**Strong CER example:**
> "Claim: PCR is a reliable tool for diagnosing viral infections. Evidence: PCR tests for COVID-19 detected viral RNA in patient samples with sensitivity above 95% in clinical studies. Reasoning: Because PCR amplifies even tiny amounts of a target sequence, it can detect infection before symptoms appear and before antibody tests become positive, making it effective for early diagnosis."

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "PCR makes new DNA sequences" | "PCR copies existing sequences. You must have a template—it can't create information that isn't there." |
| "Gel electrophoresis identifies who committed a crime directly" | "Gels show DNA fragment patterns. The interpretation—whether fragments match a suspect's sample—is a probabilistic comparison, not a direct ID." |
| "CRISPR always works perfectly" | "CRISPR is precise but not infallible. Off-target edits are a real concern and are actively studied. No human technology is error-free." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Research one current CRISPR clinical trial (e.g., sickle cell treatment). What phase is it in? What efficacy data has been published? Evaluate the strength of that evidence.
- **Struggling learners:** Provide a one-page biotech reference with diagrams: PCR cycle, gel diagram with labels, CRISPR guide RNA + Cas9 schematic.
- **ELL support:** Vocabulary anchor chart with: PCR, gel electrophoresis, CRISPR, gene editing—each with a one-sentence definition and a visual.
