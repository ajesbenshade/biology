# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 15 – Full Unit Synthesis Workshop

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains cumulative review prompts and facilitation notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Romans 1:20**
> "For since the creation of the world God's invisible qualities... have been clearly seen, being understood from what has been made."

Today asks students to see genetics as one connected system rather than a pile of isolated facts. That coherence can deepen wonder at the order God built into living things.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Help students connect DNA information flow, inheritance, and biotechnology into one coherent sequence.
- Move students beyond isolated facts into explanation and synthesis.
- Surface any final conceptual disconnects before Day 16.
- Save one or two strong models that can steady anxious students before the practice assessment.

**Background for you:** Keep the synthesis work written and concrete. Students will sound more confident if they can point to a clear sequence, a labeled diagram, and one specific piece of evidence than if they are pushed into polished performance too early.

---

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "The coherence of genetics is not random; it reflects the order God built into creation."
- "Students should move beyond memorizing parts and begin seeing how those parts fit together into one truthful explanation."
- "Clarity matters more than performance today. We want accurate connections, not rushed sounding-smart answers."

---

## Materials & Preparation

- Cumulative synthesis packet moving from DNA information flow to heredity to biotechnology evidence.
- Teacher exemplar showing one complete sequence answer.
- Readiness checklist for the end of class.
- Colored pens or highlighters for revision marks during the workshop.

---

## Routine and Logistics Notes

- Put packets on desks before class so the bellwork starts immediately.
- Keep Block 2 independent for the first half of the time so every student produces a real written response.
- Use partner check only after students have written enough to share, not as a substitute for writing.
- Save one or two strong anonymous responses to reuse during Day 16 confidence-setting.

---

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Prompt:** Sequence DNA to mRNA to protein for one short example. Then name where a mutation would change the final protein.

**Strong answer:** "If the DNA template produces mRNA AUG-CCU-UAA, the protein is Met-Pro-Stop. A mutation that changes the coding sequence before the stop codon can change the amino acid sequence and the final protein."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for the correct sequence and a clear note about where the mutation affects the protein outcome.

---

## Lesson Delivery Script (85 min)

### 1. Bell Work and Launch (8 min)

Students complete the sequence task independently while the teacher checks that packets and checklists are ready.

Transition cue:
> "Finish the final codon, star the place a mutation would matter most, and keep your packet open for the model."

### 2. Mini-Lesson (15 min)

Model one full-sequence explanation that connects:
- DNA change
- protein change
- trait or inheritance consequence
- biotechnology evidence or application

Say:
> "Today we are not hopping from chapter to chapter. We are tracing one connected story."

### 3. Guided Practice Block 1 – Structured Synthesis Packet (25 min)

Students complete the packet in order, moving from molecular genetics to heredity to biotechnology.

Teacher moves:
- Stop students who skip sequence words like first, next, because, and therefore.
- Require at least one diagram or labeled representation in the packet.
- Mark one place where each student's explanation becomes vague so they can fix it in Block 2.

### 4. Guided Practice Block 2 – Written Synthesis and Readiness Check (25 min)

Students write one full synthesis response independently for 15 minutes, then swap with one partner for a 5-minute checklist read, then revise for the final 5 minutes.

Checklist focus:
- Are the ideas connected in the right order?
- Is at least one piece of evidence or vocabulary used precisely?
- Is one weak area marked for Day 16 review?

### 5. Debrief and Exit Ticket (10 min)

Students complete the readiness checklist and name the strand they will review tonight.

---

## Workshop Focus

Use prompts that force connection across the unit, for example:

- How does a DNA change become a trait difference?
- How can inheritance patterns and biotechnology both depend on accurate DNA information?
- Why do controls, ratios, and diagrams all matter when explaining genetics?

---

## Key Teaching Moves

- Ask follow-up questions that connect chapters rather than staying inside one skill bucket.
- Put strong student explanations on the board as models.
- Redirect vague answers toward sequence words such as first, next, because, therefore.

---

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Prompt:** What is one connection across Chapters 11–13 that is clearer to you now than it was last week, and what evidence helped you see it?

**Strong answer:** "It is clearer that a DNA mutation can change a protein, which can change a trait and then be tracked with biotechnology tools. The sequence model and the synthesis packet helped because I had to connect each step instead of treating them as separate topics."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for naming one real connection and one concrete support from class.

---

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Require one extra comparison between two biotechnology tools or two inheritance models inside the synthesis response.
- **Support:** Provide a sentence-frame ladder using first, next, because, therefore, and finally.
- **ELL support:** Give a visual sequence strip with icons for DNA, RNA, protein, trait, and biotechnology evidence.

---

## Planning Note

Save two or three strong cumulative responses to model before the Day 16 practice assessment. Look for responses that show sequence words, specific evidence, and clear transitions between chapters.
