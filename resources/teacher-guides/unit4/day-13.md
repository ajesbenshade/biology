# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 13 – Data and Reasoning Studio

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains feedback moves and sample emphasis points not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Proverbs 18:13**
> "To answer before listening, that is folly and shame."

Data reasoning requires students to listen to the evidence before they answer. That habit matters in science and in discipleship: careful attention comes before confident conclusions.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Help students cite precise data in biotechnology explanations.
- Improve the difference between evidence statements and reasoning statements.
- Reinforce correct use of positive and negative controls.
- Connect today's writing habits to the same explanation moves used earlier in Unit 4.

**Background for you:** Treat this as a precision day, not a high-volume day. Students do not need more datasets than they can process carefully. They need repeated practice naming exact evidence, checking controls, and revising weak reasoning into trustworthy explanation.

---

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Good scientists and faithful learners both slow down long enough to hear what the evidence is actually saying."
- "Humility shows up when students revise weak wording instead of defending it just because they wrote it first."
- "Precision in language is part of truthfulness. Vague scientific writing usually hides vague thinking."

---

## Materials & Preparation

- PCR and gel-analysis practice sets
- One weak CER exemplar and one strong CER exemplar
- Highlighters for claim, evidence, and reasoning annotation

---

## Routine and Logistics Notes

- Keep all datasets face down until the whole class labels the controls together.
- During annotation, require the same colors everywhere so peer feedback stays fast and consistent.
- Use the cue "claim first, evidence second, reasoning third" whenever students start writing in the wrong order.
- Pull students who still omit controls into one short reteach cluster before independent writing begins.

---

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Prompt:** Read this sentence: "The experiment worked because there was a band in the sample lane." What is too weak about that statement?

**Strong answers:**
- "It does not say what size the band was."
- "It does not compare the sample to the positive control."
- "It ignores the negative control, so it does not rule out contamination."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for identifying at least one missing data detail and one missing control comparison.

---

## Lesson Delivery Script (85 min)

### 1. Bell Work and Launch (8 min)

Students diagnose the weak evidence sentence independently, then compare answers with one partner.

Transition cue:
> "Highlighter down, datasets closed, and eyes up while we build the board model together."

### 2. Mini-Lesson (15 min)

Model one full dataset and annotate claim, evidence, and reasoning separately.

Use both a weak and strong exemplar:
- Weak evidence: vague band language, no control reference
- Strong evidence: exact size, control comparison, narrow conclusion

Say:
> "If students skip from claim to conclusion without naming the data, the explanation sounds scientific but it is not yet trustworthy."

### 3. Guided Practice Block 1 – Data Annotation (27 min)

Students annotate two short datasets. First dataset is scaffolded, second is more independent.

Teacher moves:
- Ask "Which data point?" every time students say "the data shows."
- Require students to underline the control comparison before they can write a claim.

### 4. Guided Practice Block 2 – CER Draft and Peer Revision (25 min)

Students write one full CER independently, then use peer review to strengthen wording.

Peer protocol:
- Partner A checks whether the evidence sentence names a specific data point.
- Partner B checks whether the reasoning explains why that data supports the claim.
- Writer revises one sentence before turning in the final draft.

### 5. Debrief and Exit Ticket (10 min)

Project one anonymous weak sentence and revise it together so the class sees the final polish move.

---

## Key Teaching Moves

- When students say "the data shows," ask "which data point?"
- Require mention of controls before accepting a conclusion about a gel or PCR result.
- Use sentence stems if needed: "The evidence supports the claim because..."

---

## Watch Fors

- Students naming a band without comparing it to a control
- Reasoning that simply repeats the evidence
- Conclusions that overreach beyond the data provided
- Students acting as if data writing is "just biotech" instead of another version of Unit 4 reasoning

---

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Prompt:** Rewrite this weak sentence so it becomes strong evidence: "The sample looks similar to the control."

**Strong answer:** "The sample lane contains a band at 400 bp that matches the positive control band at 400 bp, while the negative control remains blank."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for a sentence with a specific data value and at least one control reference.

---

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Add a second dataset and ask students to compare reliability.
- **Support:** Provide a CER frame with blanks for data references and reasoning connectors.

---

## Planning Note

Flag students who still omit controls or use vague evidence wording. Those students should be seen again quickly during Day 16 conferences even if their overall score looks acceptable.
