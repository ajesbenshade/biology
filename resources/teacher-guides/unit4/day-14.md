# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 14 – Problem-Solving Clinic and Review Workshop

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains grouping suggestions and correction priorities not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Colossians 3:23**
> "Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart, as working for the Lord, not for human masters."

Clinic days can feel repetitive, but careful revision still matters. Students honor God when they show their work honestly, correct it patiently, and keep practicing what is not yet automatic.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Repair setup errors in Punnett squares, pedigrees, probability, and chi-square.
- Help students locate where their reasoning broke down.
- Keep practice specific and efficient rather than repetitive.
- End with a second clinic rotation that rehearses cumulative review without adding a new game structure.

**Background for you:** Students do not need more novelty today. They need one predictable clinic routine that lets them fix setup errors, vocabulary confusion, and shaky justification. Keep the block calm and procedural so students can focus on the math and biology rather than the format.

---

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Faithful work often looks like careful revision, not exciting novelty."
- "Showing each step honestly is an act of integrity because it makes our reasoning visible and correctable."
- "Precision with genotype, phenotype, expected, and observed is part of truthful scientific speech."

---

## Materials & Preparation

- Three clinic sets: Punnett squares, probability plus chi-square, and pedigree interpretation.
- One extension set for students already near mastery.
- Group list based on dominant error patterns from Days 11–13.
- One short answer-check sheet for the teacher table.

---

## Routine and Logistics Notes

- Assign students to their first clinic before class starts so no time is lost sorting after bellwork.
- Use one consistent rule in both rotations: students must show one fully worked solution before moving to the next problem.
- Keep the same paper flow in both blocks so the second rotation feels familiar, not like a new game.
- Pull quick teacher conferences during the second rotation only after students have attempted one problem independently.

---

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Prompt:** A student writes this conclusion: "The phenotype ratio is Tt, Tt, tt, and tt, so the cross is half dominant and half recessive." What is wrong with the setup?

**Strong answers:**
- "The student mixed genotype and phenotype language. Tt and tt are genotypes, not phenotypes."
- "The dominant phenotype includes both Tt outcomes, so the phenotype ratio would be 2 dominant to 2 recessive, or 1:1."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for identifying the vocabulary mistake and correcting the ratio language.

---

## Lesson Delivery Script (85 min)

### 1. Bell Work and Launch (8 min)

Students diagnose the setup mistake independently, then compare with their first clinic partner.

Transition cue:
> "Circle the first line where the error begins, then move to your clinic with only your pencil and packet."

### 2. Mini-Lesson (15 min)

Walk through one mixed inheritance problem from setup to written conclusion.

Model these moves explicitly:
- identify what kind of problem it is
- write the setup cleanly
- solve each step before naming the conclusion
- use vocabulary precisely in the final sentence

### 3. Guided Practice Block 1 – First Clinic Rotation (25 min)

Students complete their first skill-strand set.

Teacher moves:
- Ask students to circle the first line where the error appears.
- Require one fully shown answer before approving the next problem.
- Redirect quick guessers back to the setup line.

### 4. Guided Practice Block 2 – Second Clinic Rotation (25 min)

Students switch to a second skill strand and follow the same routine.

Use the same approval rule in the second clinic rotation that you used in the first: no student advances until one fully shown solution has been checked for setup, vocabulary, and conclusion.

### 5. Debrief and Exit Ticket (10 min)

Students write one process change that made a later problem easier than the first one.

## Suggested Grouping

- Group 1: Punnett square and genotype-phenotype confusion
- Group 2: Probability and chi-square computation
- Group 3: Pedigree interpretation and inheritance-pattern claims
- Group 4: Mixed-review extension for students already near mastery

---

## Key Teaching Moves

- Ask students to circle the first line where an error appears.
- Require all work shown before discussing the final answer.
- Push vocabulary precision: genotype is not phenotype, expected is not observed.

---

## Common Fixes

| Error | Correction |
|------|------------|
| Phenotype labeled as genotype | Re-state the allele combination separately from the visible trait |
| Chi-square setup incomplete | Compute each category separately before summing |
| Pedigree claim too fast | Check all generations before naming the pattern |

---

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Prompt:** What did you change in your process today that made your second clinic problem stronger?

**Strong answer:** "I stopped jumping to the final answer and checked whether I had written the genotype setup correctly first. That kept my phenotype ratio from being wrong later."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for naming one real process change and linking it to improved accuracy.

---

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Use the mixed-review extension set and require a written comparison of two different problem types.
- **Support:** Narrow each clinic page to two must-finish problems plus one model answer to compare against.
- **ELL support:** Provide sentence starters such as "The first error appears when ___" and "The corrected conclusion should say ___."

---

## Planning Note

Students who still cannot identify where an error begins should be prioritized for Day 16 conferences even if they eventually reach correct answers with help.
