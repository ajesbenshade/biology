# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 11 – Review and Error Analysis

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

## Materials & Preparation

- Anonymized incorrect sample responses (from Day 10 station work or previous homework): 4–6 examples covering most common error types.
- Review worksheets for workshop (pre-printed: one per student).
- Error log template (optional): each student records their own error patterns across the unit.
- Readiness check printed (short mixed-format: 8–10 items).

---

## Most Common Unit 4 Errors — Master List (for your reference)

Use this to select which anonymized examples to display:

| Error Type | Common Mistake | Correct Understanding |
|-----------|--------------|----------------------|
| Codon reading | Wrong orientation on codon table | First base = left column, second = top row, third = right |
| Punnett square | Forgetting to list all genotype outcomes before converting to phenotype | Always list 4 boxes, then group by phenotype |
| Chi-square | Adding (O-E) before dividing, or dividing total by one number | Compute (O-E)²/E per category, then sum |
| Gel interpretation | Saying "any band = success" | Must compare size to positive control AND check negative control |
| CER reasoning | Reasoning just restates evidence | Reasoning explains the *why*—logical connection from evidence to claim |
| Replication: leading vs. lagging | Labeling both strands the same | Leading = continuous, toward fork; lagging = fragments (Okazaki), away from fork |
| Mutation: frameshift scope | Thinking only the inserted/deleted bases are affected | Every codon after the mutation point is shifted |
| Genotype vs. phenotype | Treating them interchangeably | Genotype = allele combination; phenotype = observable trait |

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Prompt:** Display one anonymized incorrect gel interpretation. Example:

> "Student response: 'The sample worked because there is a band in the experimental lane.'"

**Ask students to diagnose the error and write the corrected response.**

**Corrected model:** "This response ignored the controls. To conclude the sample has the target DNA, we need to confirm: (1) the positive control shows a band at the expected size, and (2) the negative control shows no band. Only then can we interpret the experimental band as meaningful."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for identifying the specific error (no control check) and writing the correction. Deduct 2 pts for vague "they didn't explain enough."

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Misconception Review (20 min)

Display 4–6 anonymized errors on the board or projector. For each:
1. Ask: "What's wrong here? What clue tells you?"
2. Take 1–2 student responses.
3. Explain the correct approach clearly.
4. Write the corrected version on the board.

**Suggested sequence (adjust to your class's actual errors):**

**Error 1 — Codon table misread:**
Wrong: "AGA = Met"
Correct: "AUG = Met (start codon). AGA = Arg. The first base of the codon is the row, not the column."

**Error 2 — Chi-square computation:**
Wrong: χ² = (58+22−60−20) / (60+20) = 0
Correct: Compute per category first: (58-60)²/60 + (22-20)²/20 = 0.067 + 0.20 = 0.267

**Error 3 — CER reasoning restates evidence:**
Wrong reasoning: "The evidence shows a band at 400 bp in the sample lane."
Correct reasoning: "The band at 400 bp in the sample matches the positive control at 400 bp, confirming the expected fragment is present; the clean negative control rules out contamination, so the evidence supports that the target gene was amplified."

**Error 4 — Frameshift scope:**
Wrong: "Only the inserted base changes."
Correct: "A frameshift shifts the reading frame from the insertion/deletion point forward. Every codon after that position is read incorrectly—not just the affected base pair."

**Error 5 — Genotype vs. phenotype:**
Wrong: "The phenotype of this plant is Tt."
Correct: "Tt is the genotype (the allele combination). The phenotype is 'tall' (the observable trait resulting from the dominant T allele)."

### 2. Workshop: Small-Group Error Analysis and Revision (25 min)

Distribute the review worksheet and anonymized response sets. Groups:
1. Identify the error(s) in each sample.
2. Write a corrected response.
3. Explain in one sentence what the error was and what the correct understanding requires.

Circulate. For struggling groups, prompt: "Which term did the student use incorrectly?" or "What information is missing that would make this answer complete?"

For advanced groups: "Could you design a follow-up question that would reveal whether the student who wrote this actually understands the correction?"

### 3. Readiness Check (12 min)

Administer as low-stakes in-class practice (not graded for a major score—use for your diagnostic purposes).

**Sample items and answers for the readiness check:**

1. "Translate: mRNA = AUGCCGUAA → Met-Pro-Stop"
2. "Cross: I^A i × I^B I^B → 50% AB (I^A I^B), 50% B (I^B i). Phenotypes: 50% Type AB, 50% Type B."
3. "Name the enzyme that joins Okazaki fragments → Ligase"
4. "A band appears at 300 bp in both positive control and sample. Negative control is clean. Conclusion → Sample contains target fragment of ~300 bp."
5. "Observed: 45 dominant, 15 recessive (60 total). Expected: 45, 15. χ² = 0. Interpretation → Perfect match to 3:1 model; could be rounding. Very small χ², no reason to doubt the model."
6. "A point mutation changes GAG to GUG. Glu → Val. This is a missense mutation—different amino acid. Real example: sickle cell disease."

### 4. Debrief (3 min)

Ask each group to share one error they corrected and what changed in their reasoning. Keep brief—just one sentence per group.

Say:
> "Every error you corrected today is a concept you now understand better than you did this morning. That's what Day 11 is for."

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** What is one error pattern you corrected today, and what changed in your reasoning?

**Strong answer:** "I was misreading the codon table—I was using rows for the third base instead of the first base. Now I know: first base is the left row, second is the top column, third is the right column. I retranslated three sequences correctly after the correction."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for naming a specific error and describing the corrected understanding. Deduct 3 pts for vague answers.

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Final Unit 4 review sheet | 10 pts (completion) | All sections complete; vocabulary accurate |
| Revise one readiness-check response | 5 pts | Clear improvement visible; reasoning added where missing |

**Strong revision example:**
Before: "The band shows the experiment worked."
After: "The band at 300 bp in the experimental lane matches the positive control band at 300 bp. The negative control shows no band, ruling out contamination. Therefore the experiment produced the target DNA fragment successfully."

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Chi-square being too high means we were wrong" | "A high chi-square means the observed results differ significantly from what the model predicts. It doesn't automatically mean the data is wrong—it means the model may not be the best fit, or there's an additional factor to investigate." |
| "Correction means I failed" | "Error analysis is part of science. Scientists revise interpretations when new evidence appears. Correcting your own work is the same skill." |
| "If I improve my readiness check, it replaces my previous score" | Clarify your grading policy—typically the revision demonstrates learning, not replaces; framing this as growth-oriented reduces test anxiety before Day 12. |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Given a correct and incorrect CER pair for the same gel image, write a comparison: what specific vocabulary or evidence made one CER stronger? Could this be an Assessment question?
- **Struggling learners:** Error log template pre-filled with the four correction categories (codon, chi-square, CER reasoning, gel interpretation). Students mark which category their Day 10 errors fell into and focus workshop time on those specifically.
- **ELL support:** Error correction sentence frames: "The original response said ___, but this is incorrect because ___. The correct answer is ___, because ___."

## Planning Note for Day 12

After reviewing the readiness check results, group students mentally by readiness level. Strong students can be given the extension option on the assessment. Students with persistent errors in one category may benefit from a modified starting point or a targeted verbal check-in before the test begins.
