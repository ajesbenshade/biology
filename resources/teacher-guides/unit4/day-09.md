# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 9 – Biotechnology Data Analysis

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Proverbs 3:5-6**
> "Trust in the Lord with all your heart and lean not on your own understanding; in all your ways submit to him, and he will make your paths straight."

In data analysis, we resist the temptation to overstate conclusions or see what we want to see. Submitting our interpretations to evidence—and ultimately to God's truth—is the mark of honest science. Read aloud; tie to ethics of biotech decisions where overconfident conclusions have real consequences for real people.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Teach accurate interpretation of PCR gel results: bands indicate fragment presence, size, and compare to controls.
- Guide students to write strong claim-evidence-reasoning (CER) responses from data.
- Connect biotech ethics to biblical stewardship: healing applications are good; overreach and misuse are warnings.

**Background for you:** PCR amplifies a specific DNA segment, producing many copies. Gel electrophoresis then separates those copies by size—smaller fragments travel farther through the gel toward the positive electrode. A "band" is where fragments of the same size collect. Controls are essential: a **positive control** (known sample that should produce a band) confirms the method worked; a **negative control** (water or blank sample) confirms no contamination. Results are only interpretable if controls behave correctly.

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Precise data reading honors the order God built into creation. Overstating claims—saying more than the evidence warrants—lacks intellectual humility."

## Materials & Preparation

- Printed sample PCR/gel images (or projector slides): include a lane ladder, positive control lane, negative control lane, and 2–3 experimental sample lanes.
- CER template handouts (three-column: Claim | Evidence | Reasoning).
- Biotech scenario packets: two scenarios per group (e.g., GMO crop resistance verification; CRISPR edit confirmation in cell culture).
- Whiteboard for modeling band-reading during the guided demo.

---

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Prompt:** Interpret this gel description:
- Lane 1: Ladder (size markers at 100 bp, 200 bp, 400 bp, 600 bp, 800 bp)
- Lane 2: Positive control — band at 500 bp
- Lane 3: Experimental sample — band at 500 bp

**Model answer:** "The experimental sample contains the target DNA fragment. The band at 500 bp matches the positive control, confirming the fragment is the correct size. The method is valid because the positive control produced the expected band."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Award full credit for: correct fragment identification + reference to control as validation. Deduct 2 pts for reading the band without mentioning the control.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Guided Analysis Demo (20 min)

Project a labeled gel image on the board (or draw one). Walk through each element:

**How to read a gel — board framework:**

| Lane | Contents | What to look for |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| 1 | DNA Ladder | Known size markers; use to estimate experimental band sizes |
| 2 | Positive control | Should show a band at expected size; confirms method works |
| 3 | Negative control | Should show NO band; confirms no contamination |
| 4–6 | Experimental samples | Compare band position/size to controls |

Say:
> "Every gel is only as trustworthy as its controls. If your positive control failed—no band—you can't trust your experimental results at all. If your negative control has a band—contamination—you can't trust a positive result either."

**Model CER on the board with a sample gel:**

- **Claim:** "Sample 4 contains the target gene insertion."
- **Evidence:** "A band appears in lane 4 at approximately 600 bp, matching the positive control band in lane 2 at 600 bp. No band appears in the negative control (lane 3)."
- **Reasoning:** "The size match to the positive control indicates the correct fragment is present. The clean negative control rules out contamination. Together, these data points support the claim that the gene insertion was successful."

Say:
> "Notice what strong evidence looks like: it's specific (600 bp, not just 'a band'), it references controls, and the reasoning explains why the data supports the claim—not just restates it."

**Common confusion to address pre-emptively:**

> "Does any band mean success? No. A band in the wrong lane (negative control) means contamination. A band at the wrong size means a different fragment is present. Size and controls both matter."

### 2. Small-Group Data Sets (25 min)

Groups work through two scenarios. Circulate and prompt with: "What specific data point supports your claim?" and "What would the negative control look like if your method had contamination?"

**Scenario A — GMO crop resistance test (sample answer key):**

Gel description: Ladder, positive control (band at 400 bp), negative control (no band), Sample 1 (band at 400 bp), Sample 2 (no band), Sample 3 (band at 400 bp).

- CER: "Samples 1 and 3 contain the herbicide-resistance gene insert (claim). Both show bands at 400 bp matching the positive control; Sample 2 shows no band (evidence). The absence of a band in the negative control confirms the positive results are not due to contamination. Sample 2 did not receive or retain the insert (reasoning)."

**Scenario B — CRISPR edit verification (sample answer key):**

Gel description: Ladder, positive control at 300 bp, negative control (no band), Edited cell line (band at 300 bp), Unedited cell line (band at 500 bp).

- CER: "The CRISPR edit was successful in the edited cell line (claim). The edited cell line shows a band at 300 bp matching the positive control; the unedited line shows a band at 500 bp, indicating the original, uncut sequence (evidence). The size difference confirms that CRISPR cut the target site, producing a shorter detectable fragment (reasoning)."

**Differentiation:**
- Struggling learners: Provide pre-highlighted gel images with bands already circled; students identify size and compare to controls only.
- Advanced: Ask students to propose a question this gel cannot answer (e.g., "Does the gene insert produce a functional protein?") and what additional experiment would be needed.

### 3. CER Draft + Peer Check (12 min)

Students write a full CER for their strongest scenario. Partners swap and annotate:
- Is the claim falsifiable? (Could you design an experiment to disprove it?)
- Is the evidence specific? (Does it include actual data values—bp sizes, lane numbers?)
- Does the reasoning connect the evidence to the claim—or just restate the evidence?

Say: "Strong reasoning explains *why* the evidence means what you claim. 'The band at 400 bp matches the control' is evidence. 'This match shows the same-size fragment is present, making contamination unlikely' is reasoning."

### 4. Debrief + Ethics Tie (5–10 min)

Share one or two strong evidence statements from student work. Then:

Say:
> "When PCR data is misread—intentionally or carelessly—the consequences can be real. A false positive in a disease diagnostic sends a healthy person through unnecessary treatment. A falsely confirmed crop modification gets released into an ecosystem. Honest data interpretation is not just academic—it is a form of care for people and creation."

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** What was your strongest evidence for your CER claim today?

**Strong answer example:** "My strongest evidence was that a band appeared at 400 bp in my experimental sample, matching the positive control at 400 bp, while the negative control lane was blank—ruling out contamination."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for a specific data reference (size, lane, control comparison). Deduct 3 pts for vague answers ("I saw a band").

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Corrected missed data-interpretation items | 10 pts | Corrections include reasoning, not just new answers |
| Extension analysis — new scenario CER | 5 pts | All three CER components present; evidence is specific |
| Unit 4 review packet (start/completion) | Completion | All sections attempted |

**Rubric for extension CER:**
- Evidence specificity: 50%
- Reasoning logic (connects evidence to claim): 30%
- Accuracy of interpretation: 20%

**Strong extension CER example:**
> "Claim: The patient sample does not contain the viral DNA. Evidence: No band appears in the patient sample lane; the positive control shows a clear band at 350 bp and the negative control shows no band, confirming the method functioned correctly. Reasoning: Because the positive control confirms amplification would have occurred if the target DNA were present, and no band appeared in the patient sample, we can conclude the viral sequence was absent in this sample."

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Any band means success" | "Band size and position matter. A band at the wrong size means a different fragment is present—not necessarily the target. Always compare to the positive control." |
| "Why do we need a negative control?" | "The negative control rules out contamination. If the blank sample produces a band, the reagents or equipment have been contaminated—and your positive results can't be trusted." |
| "If the positive control fails, can we still use the data?" | "No. A failed positive control means the method didn't work in this run—PCR didn't amplify, or the gel didn't separate properly. The experiment must be repeated." |
| "CER reasoning is just restating the evidence" | "Reasoning explains *why* the evidence supports the claim—the logical connection. 'The band is at 400 bp' is evidence. 'A 400 bp band matches the expected fragment size for the gene insert, confirming its presence' is reasoning." |

---

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Compare two gels from the same experiment run at different conditions (e.g., different annealing temperatures). Which produces cleaner bands? What does this show about PCR optimization? Write a CER comparing reliability.
- **Struggling learners:** Pre-highlighted gel images with circled bands and labeled sizes. CER scaffold with sentence starters: "My claim is ___ because the evidence shows ___. This matters because ___."
- **ELL support:** Gel diagram with labeled vocabulary (ladder, band, lane, control) in a visual glossary format.
