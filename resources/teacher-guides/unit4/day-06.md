# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 6 – Human Genetics and Pedigrees

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Acts 17:26 / Romans 8:22** (Colossians 1:16-17 continued as unit anchor)
> "From one man he made all the nations…" (Acts 17:26)
> "We know that the whole creation has been groaning…" (Romans 8:22)

Pedigrees reveal inheritance patterns in real families. God's sustaining order allows us to trace these patterns carefully. Acts 17:26 reminds us of shared human dignity; Romans 8:22 frames discussion of genetic disease with compassion—creation groans under brokenness, and we respond with care and truth-seeking.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Teach pedigree symbols and interpretation with correct conventions.
- Distinguish autosomal vs. sex-linked inheritance patterns using clue sets.
- Calculate probabilities from pedigree data.

**Background for you:** A pedigree is simply a family tree that tracks which people have a trait (shaded) and which don't (unshaded). Lines show marriages and parent-child relationships. Figuring out the inheritance mode requires careful reasoning from the pattern—no single rule works for every pedigree.

- Pedigree symbol reference card for each student or posted on board.

## Pedigree Symbol Reference (for your use / board display)

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| Square (□) | Male |
| Circle (○) | Female |
| Filled square/circle | Affected individual |
| Half-filled square/circle | Known carrier (often for sex-linked) |
| Horizontal line | Mating/marriage |
| Vertical + horizontal below | Offspring |
| Roman numerals (I, II, III…) | Generation numbers |

---

## Bell Work Answer Key (10 min)

**Prompt:** Provide a simple pedigree (two unaffected parents in Generation I; one affected son, two unaffected daughters in Generation II). Ask students to identify likely carriers.

**Answer:**
- Both unaffected parents in Gen I are carriers (since they produced an affected child without being affected themselves).
- The affected son is homozygous recessive (aa).
- The unaffected daughters each have a 2/3 chance of being carriers (Aa), since we know parent genotypes are Aa × Aa.

**Grading:** 5 pts for effort and identifying parents as carriers. Award full credit if reasoning is evident.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Symbol Review (10 min)

Draw on board and annotate each symbol. Common errors to preemptively address:

| Common Error | Clarification |
|-------------|--------------|
| Confusing the parent-connection line with the offspring line | "The horizontal line between two people is a mating pair. The vertical line going down leads to offspring." |
| Thinking half-filled always means carrier | "Half-filled is only used when we've determined someone carries a recessive allele but isn't affected. Not all carriers are marked." |
| Listing generation numbers wrong | "Generation I is always at the top (the grandparents or oldest known generation)." |

### 2. Group Pedigree Analysis (25 min)

Assign 3–4 sample pedigrees with different inheritance modes. Groups determine: mode of inheritance, likely genotypes for key individuals, and rationale.

**Clue set for determining inheritance mode:**

| Clue | Likely Interpretation |
|------|----------------------|
| More males affected than females | Sex-linked recessive (males have one X) |
| Trait appears in every generation | Autosomal dominant |
| Trait skips generations | Autosomal recessive (or sex-linked recessive via female carriers) |
| Affected female present | Cannot be sex-linked recessive (would need two copies) — unless father is affected |
| Two unaffected parents → affected child | Autosomal recessive (both parents are carriers) |

Say: "These clues guide our reasoning from evidence—but a small pedigree can still be ambiguous. More family data would improve confidence."

**Sample answer key for four pedigrees (adjust to match your printed sheets):**

1. Both sexes equally affected, trait skips a generation → **Autosomal recessive**
2. Only males affected, mothers of affected males unaffected → **Sex-linked recessive**
3. Every affected individual has an affected parent → **Autosomal dominant**
4. All carriers are female, only males show trait → **Sex-linked recessive** (X-linked)

### 3. Probability Practice (20 min)

Walk through at least two probability calculations from pedigree data.

**Example 1 — Autosomal recessive:**
- Both parents are confirmed carriers (Aa × Aa).
- Probability next child is affected: 25% (1 in 4)
- Probability next child is a carrier: 50% (2 in 4)

**Example 2 — Sex-linked recessive:**
- Mother is a carrier (X^a X), father is unaffected (X Y).
- Probability son is affected: 50%
- Probability daughter is affected: 0% (can be carrier, not affected)
- Probability daughter is a carrier: 50%

Say: "These are probabilities, not guarantees. Each pregnancy is an independent event. A couple who has had three affected children still has the same 25% chance for the next."

### 4. Debrief (5 min)

Ask: "What additional data would increase your confidence in a pedigree conclusion?"

Expected answer: "More generations, larger family sizes, testing DNA of specific individuals to confirm carrier status."

Say: "Good science is honest about its limits. This is a model based on available evidence."

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** What additional data would strengthen your confidence in a pedigree conclusion?

**Strong answer:** "Testing DNA of specific individuals to confirm carrier status; or tracing more generations to see if the pattern holds consistently."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for any evidence-based answer that shows understanding of why small pedigrees have limits.

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Pedigree analysis worksheet | 10 pts | Correct inheritance mode identified for each pedigree + carriers correctly labeled |
| Short explanation of one pattern with evidence | 5 pts | Named mode + cited pedigree clue as evidence |
| Research summary on one genetic disorder (optional depth) | Bonus | Accurate condition name, inheritance mode, brief description |

**Strong explanation example:**
> "The pedigree for Pedigree B shows X-linked recessive inheritance. Only males are affected, and their mothers—who are unaffected—must be carriers, since each affected male has an unaffected mother. No affected female appears, consistent with the requirement of two X-linked recessive alleles for female expression."

**Rubric:**
- Symbols/modes accurate: 70%
- Probability calculation correct: 20%
- Compassion note / ethical framing (bonus): Up to 5 pts extra

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Why are more males affected in X-linked recessive?" | "Males have only one X chromosome. If that one X carries the recessive allele, it's expressed—there's no second X to mask it. Females need two copies of the recessive allele to be affected." |
| "If a parent is a carrier, will their children always be carriers too?" | "No. Each child independently inherits one allele from each parent. Even a carrier parent has a 50% chance of passing on the normal allele." |
| "Autosomal means it affects only the body, not sex?" | "Autosomal means the gene is on one of the 22 non-sex chromosomes (autosomes). It can affect either sex equally." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Mixed-mode challenge pedigree combining autosomal and sex-linked traits in one family tree; students must argue which mode fits better and what additional test would resolve ambiguity.
- **Struggling learners:** Provide guided pedigree annotation prompts—step-by-step questions that walk them through identifying carriers before asking for the inheritance mode.
- **ELL support:** Pedigree symbol reference card with translated labels; visual "clue list" for inheritance mode identification.
