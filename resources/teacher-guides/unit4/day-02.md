# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 2 – DNA Structure and Replication

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Colossians 1:16-17**
> "All things were created through him and for him. He is before all things, and in him all things hold together."

Emphasize: DNA replication shows precise, reliable order across billions of cell divisions—God sustains this fidelity so life continues faithfully from generation to generation.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Students model DNA double helix structure with correct base pairing (A-T, C-G).
- Explain semi-conservative replication: each new DNA molecule has one original strand and one new strand.
- Understand why high fidelity matters for stable inheritance across generations.

**Background for you:** The "semi-conservative" name comes from the fact that each copy *conserves* half of the original. This was confirmed experimentally (Meselson-Stahl experiment, 1958)—worth mentioning as an example of testable science.

---

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "The amazing accuracy of DNA copying—one error per billion base pairs—reflects the sustaining power of Christ described in Colossians 1:17. Creation holds together because He holds it together."
- "We don't appeal to chance to explain this precision; we recognize design that calls for careful stewardship."

---

## Materials & Preparation

- DNA model kits, or paper strips + tape for base pairing simulation.
- Projector for replication animation (optional but helpful).
- **Strawberry DNA extraction (optional mini-lab):** strawberries, dish detergent, isopropyl alcohol (chilled), ziplock bags, strainer or cheesecloth, clear cups.
- Printed replication flowchart template for homework reference.

---

## Bell Work Answer Key (10 min)

**Question:** Sketch and label one DNA segment from memory (from Day 1).

**Full credit model:**
- Double helix shape (two strands twisting).
- At least two base pairs labeled: A-T and C-G.
- Strands running in opposite directions (antiparallel) — accept if attempted.

**Grading:** Effort + attempt at structure — 5 pts. Award full credit if all labeled components present; 3 pts for partial attempt.

---

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Vocabulary Review (10 min)

Quick oral quiz on Day 1 terms. Suggested call-and-response:

| Teacher Ask | Expected Response |
|-------------|------------------|
| "What molecule carries the genetic code?" | DNA |
| "What is a gene?" | A section of DNA with instructions for one trait |
| "How many chromosomes in a human body cell?" | 46 |

### 2. Model Build (25 min)

Students construct a DNA segment with labeled components.

Say:
> "A always pairs with T—adenine and thymine. C always pairs with G—cytosine and guanine. This is called complementary base pairing. Like a perfect lock and key God designed into the molecule itself."

**Check for understanding:** Ask students to swap one base pair and explain why that would be a mutation.

### 3. Replication Walk-Through (20 min)

Walk through each step clearly:

| Step | Enzyme | Action |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Helicase | Unzips the double helix, separating the two strands |
| 2 | DNA Polymerase | Reads the template strand and adds matching nucleotides |
| 3 | Ligase | Joins Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand |
| Result | — | Two identical DNA molecules, each with one old + one new strand |

Say:
> "Each new DNA molecule keeps one original strand—that's what semi-conservative means. Half the old information is preserved. This ensures traits pass reliably from cell to cell and generation to generation."

**Key analogy:** "It's like photocopying a page but using half the original ink in each copy—you always retain half the original."

### 4. Optional Mini-Lab: Strawberry DNA Extraction (15 min)

Steps:
1. Mash strawberry in bag with a small amount of detergent + water.
2. Strain liquid through cheesecloth into a clear cup.
3. Slowly pour cold alcohol on top—DNA precipitates at the interface as a stringy white mass.

Say:
> "That stringy material you see is actual DNA—real genetic material, visible to the naked eye. This is the same kind of molecule that carries every instruction for building a strawberry."

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Why does replication fidelity matter for inheritance across generations?

**Strong answer:** "If DNA is copied with errors, the instructions for building traits change. Over time, errors can accumulate and cause disease or death. Accurate copying ensures children reliably inherit the traits encoded in parental DNA."

**Grading:** 5 pts for connecting accuracy to reliable inheritance; deduct 2 pts if explanation stays surface-level (e.g., "errors are bad").

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Replication flowchart | 10 pts | Correct order: helicase → polymerase → ligase; semi-conservative result labeled |
| 5 practice questions | 10 pts | 2 pts each; accept reasonable scientific language |
| Reflection paragraph | 5 pts | Must mention: inaccurate replication → mutations → changed traits or disorders |

**Strong reflection example:**
> "If DNA replication were not accurate, the genetic code would change each time a cell divides. These changes—mutations—can alter the instructions for proteins, leading to traits that differ from parents or, in severe cases, to cell dysfunction and disorders. The precision of replication is what makes heredity reliable."

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Semi-conservative means half the DNA is destroyed" | "No—semi means each new molecule keeps one original strand and builds one new strand. Both copies survive." |
| "Why is one strand called the lagging strand?" | "DNA polymerase can only add in one direction, so one strand must be built in short segments. Ligase joins those segments." |
| "Mutations always cause disease" | "No—most mutations are silent (no protein change) or happen in non-coding regions. Significant disease requires mutations in specific genes and often multiple events." |

---

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Compare proofreading mechanisms across organisms. Research the fidelity rate (error rate) in bacteria vs. human cells. What role does proofreading play?
- **Struggling learners:** Provide a labeled enzyme sequence reference card to use during the walk-through. Use color-coding for original vs. new strands.
- **ELL support:** Visual glossary with enzyme names, pronunciations, and simple function descriptions.
