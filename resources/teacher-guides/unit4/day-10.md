## Station Answer Keys (40 min, ~10 min per station)

### Station 1 – Codon Decoding

Students translate mRNA sequences using a codon table.

**Sample problems and answers:**

| mRNA Sequence | Codons | Amino Acids |
|--------------|--------|------------|
| `AUGUUUCGA` | AUG / UUU / CGA | Met / Phe / Arg |
| `AUGCCAUAA` | AUG / CCA / UAA | Met / Pro / Stop |
| `AUGCAGCUUGGU` | AUG / CAG / CUU / GGU | Met / Gln / Leu / Gly |
| `AUGAGCUUCUGA` | AUG / AGC / UUC / UGA | Met / Ser / Phe / Stop |

**Justification requirement:** Students must write which row/column of the codon table they used for at least one amino acid.

**Common error here:** Misreading the codon table orientation (rows vs. columns). Remind students: first base of codon = left column, second base = top row, third base = right column.

### Station 2 – Inheritance (Punnett + Chi-Square)

**Punnett problems and answers:**

| Cross | Genotype Ratio | Phenotype Ratio |
|-------|--------------|----------------|
| Aa × Aa | 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa | 3 dominant : 1 recessive |
| AaBb × AaBb | 9 A_B_ : 3 A_bb : 3 aaB_ : 1 aabb | 9:3:3:1 |
| I^A i × I^B i | 1 I^A I^B : 1 I^A i : 1 I^B i : 1 ii | 1 AB : 1 A : 1 B : 1 O |

**Chi-square problem and answer:**

Observed from a Tt × Tt cross of 80 pea plants: 58 tall, 22 short. Expected: 60 tall, 20 short (3:1 ratio).

$$\chi^2 = \frac{(58-60)^2}{60} + \frac{(22-20)^2}{20} = \frac{4}{60} + \frac{4}{20} = 0.067 + 0.20 = 0.267$$

Interpretation: Very small χ² → results are consistent with the 3:1 model; chance variation explains the discrepancy.

### Station 3 – Replication Labeling

Students receive a replication fork diagram and label each component.

**Answer key:**

| Structure/Label | Identification |
|----------------|--------------|
| Helicase | At the replication fork; unzips the double helix |
| Leading strand | Continuously synthesized toward the fork |
| Lagging strand | Synthesized in fragments (Okazaki fragments) away from the fork |
| DNA Polymerase | Adds new nucleotides to the growing strand |
| Ligase | Joins Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand |
| Template strand | Old strand being read by DNA Polymerase |
| New strand | Newly synthesized complementary strand |

**Common confusion:** Students often label both new strands the same. Remind them: the leading strand is synthesized continuously in one direction; the lagging strand is synthesized in short fragments in the opposite direction relative to fork movement.

### Station 4 – Mutation Classification

Students classify examples as point mutation or frameshift, and evaluate likely effect.

**Answer key:**

| Example | Type | Likely Effect |
|---------|------|--------------|
| Single base substitution: GAG → GUG (sickle cell) | Point — missense | Changed amino acid (Glu → Val); altered protein shape |
| Single base deletion early in gene | Frameshift | All downstream codons shifted; likely nonfunctional protein |
| Silent substitution: CAA → CAG (both = Gln) | Point — silent | No amino acid change; protein unchanged |
| Large insertion (50 bp) in coding region | Frameshift | Reading frame disrupted; likely truncated/nonfunctional protein |
| Substitution introducing a stop codon (UAA) | Point — nonsense | Protein synthesis stops early; truncated protein |

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Problem:** Decode codon AUG and explain what it signals.

**Answer:** AUG codes for methionine (Met). It is also the universal start codon—every protein synthesis begins here, signaling the ribosome to begin translation.

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for amino acid name + start codon function.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Frame the Day (3 min before stations)

Say:
> "Today is not about new information—it's about fluency. The same way a musician practices scales before playing a piece, we practice the skills before we demonstrate mastery on the assessment. Work carefully, not just quickly. Write justifications—not just answers."

### 2. Practice Stations (40 min — 4 rotations of ~10 min)

Rotate groups on a signal. As you circulate:
- Station 1: Watch for codon table navigation errors.
- Station 2: Check that chi-square students are computing (O-E)²/E per category before summing.
- Station 3: Confirm students distinguish leading vs. lagging strand direction.
- Station 4: Confirm students explain *why* a frameshift disrupts downstream codons, not just that it does.

Prompt for any station: "Can you show me which part of your evidence supports that answer?"

### 3. Partner Error-Check (15 min)

Students trade papers with a partner. Annotate corrections using: ✓ (correct), ? (unclear), ✗ + correction needed.

Say:
> "Kind, specific feedback builds mastery. 'This is wrong' is not feedback. 'The codons should be read AUG/CCU, not AU/GCC—start with the A' is feedback."

### 4. Wrap-Up Synthesis Challenge (7 min)

**Challenge problem:** "A mutation in the helicase gene prevents helicase from unzipping DNA. What happens to DNA replication? What happens to the organism's cells?"

**Answer:** Without helicase, the double helix cannot be separated, so DNA polymerase cannot access the template strand. Replication cannot proceed. Cells that need to divide (e.g., skin, blood) cannot replicate their genetic material—leading to cell death or failure to reproduce.

Say:
> "Synthesis problems like this connect the molecular level to the cellular level. These are the kinds of questions the assessment will ask. If you can trace from one step to the next, you're ready."

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Which skill area improved most for you today, and what evidence shows that?

**Strong answers:**
- "Codon decoding improved—I was making orientation errors with the codon table, but after the correction I decoded all four sequences correctly."
- "Chi-square calculation: I learned I was forgetting to divide each (O-E)² by E before summing. After the partner check I redid it and got 0.267."

**Grading:** 5 pts for a specific skill + specific evidence of improvement. Deduct 3 pts for vague answers.

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Leading vs. lagging strand — which is which?" | "The leading strand is synthesized continuously toward the replication fork (in the direction the fork moves). The lagging strand moves away from the fork in short Okazaki fragments, because DNA Polymerase can only add in one direction." |
| "Why does a frameshift affect ALL downstream codons?" | "The reading frame is set from the start codon. One insertion or deletion shifts every codon after it—like removing one letter from a sentence: every 'word' (codon) from that point forward is garbled." |
| "Chi-square: do I add before dividing or after?" | "Compute (O-E)²/E for EACH category separately, then sum all the results. Never add the Os and Es first." |
| "Silent mutations don't matter at all?" | "Silent mutations don't change the amino acid, so they usually don't affect protein function. However, they can still affect gene regulation or RNA splicing in some cases—science is learning more about this." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Write their own mixed-problem scenario: a mutation in a specific enzyme from today's lab, with predicted molecular and cellular consequences. Exchange with another advanced student for peer evaluation.
- **Struggling learners:** Provide reference card with: codon table, Punnett square setup template, replication fork with blank labels pre-positioned, mutation type definitions with examples.
- **ELL support:** Bilingual vocabulary anchor for station key terms; visual codon table with color-coded first-base rows.
