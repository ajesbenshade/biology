# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 5 – Complex Inheritance Patterns

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Colossians 1:16-17**
> "All things were created through him and for him. He is before all things, and in him all things hold together."

Human genetic diversity—varied blood types, blended flower colors, roan cattle coats—reflects God's creative intentionality, not random accidents. We study these patterns rigorously to appreciate His design and use knowledge with compassion.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Explain incomplete dominance, codominance, and multiple alleles clearly.
- Model blood-type inheritance and interpret genotypes and phenotypes.
- Compare Mendelian and non-Mendelian outcomes using data and evidence.

**Background for you:** Mendel's simple dominant/recessive model is actually the exception rather than the rule. Most traits involve more nuance. This day builds critical thinking by showing that we always test models against evidence—and that God built flexibility and variety into the inheritance system He designed.

---

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "God designed inheritance with beautiful variety. Blood types show us how one gene locus can produce multiple outcomes, all under His sustaining order."
- During blood-type discussion: "Genetic diversity in humans glorifies the Creator. We treat genetic differences with respect and compassion—never as a basis for ranking or devaluing people."

## Materials & Preparation

- Projector/whiteboard for notes and examples (snapdragon flower colors, ABO blood type table).
- Printed blood-type simulation worksheet with blank Punnett squares for ABO crosses.
- Optional: color-coded genotype cards (red = I^A, blue = I^B, white = i) for struggling learners.
- Bibles or printed Colossians 1:16-17.

## Bell Work Answer Key (10 min)

**Question:** Differentiate genotype and phenotype with one example.

| Level | Example Answer |
|-------|---------------|
| Basic | "Genotype is the genes (BB); phenotype is the trait (brown eyes)." |
| Good | "Genotype = alleles (Bb); phenotype = what we see (brown eyes, since B is dominant)." |
| Strong | "Genotype is the genetic code in the DNA; phenotype is the observable result. In incomplete dominance, genotype Rr gives a pink phenotype—neither the red of RR nor the white of rr." |

**Follow-up:** Ask 3–4 students to share; correct gently if genotype and phenotype are conflated.
**Grading:** 5 pts for effort and accuracy.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Direct Instruction: Three Complex Patterns (20 min)

Write on board as you cover each:

**Incomplete Dominance**
- Neither allele is fully dominant; the heterozygote shows a blended phenotype.
- Example: Red (RR) × White (rr) snapdragons → Pink (Rr) offspring.
- Say: "The blending is in the phenotype, not the alleles. Alleles are still inherited separately—Mendel's law still holds. We just get a mixed-expression result."
- Biblical tie: "God's subtle design in incomplete dominance shows variety doesn't require abandoning order."

**Codominance**
- Both alleles are fully expressed; the heterozygote shows both traits simultaneously.
- Example: AB blood type—both A and B antigens are present on red blood cells.
- Say: "This is not blending. If you have AB blood, your cells have both A-type and B-type proteins on them. Both alleles are fully present and working."

**Multiple Alleles**
- More than two allele options exist at one gene locus in the population (though any individual still only carries two).
- Example: ABO blood type has three alleles: I^A, I^B, and i.

**ABO Blood Type Reference Table:**

| Phenotype (Blood Type) | Possible Genotypes |
|----------------------|-------------------|
| A | I^A I^A or I^A i |
| B | I^B I^B or I^B i |
| AB | I^A I^B |
| O | ii |

Say: "These patterns don't contradict Mendel—they extend his work. God built flexibility into creation."

### 2. Simulation: Blood-Type Inheritance Modeling (25 min)

Students complete Punnett squares for sample crosses. Walk them through the first one together:

**Worked example:**
Cross: I^A i × I^B i

|   | I^B | i |
|---|-----|---|
| **I^A** | I^A I^B (AB) | I^A i (A) |
| **i** | I^B i (B) | ii (O) |

Expected offspring: 25% AB, 25% A, 25% B, 25% O

Say: "All four blood types are possible from two parents who are type A and type B. Without knowing their full genotypes, we couldn't predict this—that's why genotype matters."

**Additional crosses for student practice:**
1. I^A I^A × I^B i → outcomes: 50% Type A (I^A I^B → AB; wait, let students find this)
2. I^A i × I^A i → outcomes: 25% AA, 50% Ai (Type A), 25% ii (Type O) → 75% A, 25% O
3. ii × I^B I^B → outcomes: 100% I^B i (Type B)

Circulate and help with square setup. For struggling students: provide pre-filled squares with just the phenotype column blank.

### 3. Comparison Task (10 min)

Students classify given examples as Mendelian, incomplete dominance, codominance, or multiple alleles.

**Answer key:**
- Tall/short pea plants → Mendelian (complete dominance)
- Pink snapdragons → Incomplete dominance
- Roan cattle (red + white hairs mixed) → Codominance
- AB blood type → Codominance + Multiple alleles
- Blood type O from two type A parents → Multiple alleles (both parents I^A i)

Say: "We choose the best model based on evidence—always test your assumptions about which pattern fits."

### 4. Debrief (5 min)

Ask: "How can similar-looking phenotypes come from completely different mechanisms?"

Expected answer: "Incomplete dominance and codominance can both produce an 'in-between' appearance, but the mechanism is different—blending in output vs. full dual expression. Evidence from protein testing or offspring ratios distinguishes them."

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Which type of complex inheritance was easiest to identify, and why?

**Good answers:**
- "Codominance was easiest because both traits are visibly present at the same time, like in AB blood type."
- "Incomplete dominance was easy with flower color since the phenotype is clearly between the two parents."

**Grading:** 5 pts for a thoughtful reason tied to observable evidence, not just a definition of the pattern.

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Complex inheritance analysis questions from simulation | 10 pts | Correct crosses + phenotype/genotype ratios; explanations present |
| Evidence-based claim comparing two patterns | 5 pts | Named patterns, observable distinction cited as evidence |

**Strong claim example:**
> "Codominance differs from incomplete dominance because both alleles are fully expressed simultaneously, as seen in AB blood type where both A and B antigens are detectable. In incomplete dominance, neither allele is fully expressed—the phenotype is intermediate, as in pink snapdragons from red and white parents."

**Rubric:**
- Accuracy: 60%
- Evidence use: 30%
- Clarity: 10%

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "Is codominance the same as blending?" | "No. In codominance, both full traits appear. Roan cattle have distinct red hairs AND distinct white hairs on the same animal—not pink hairs. AB blood has both A and B proteins fully present." |
| "Can one person have three blood type alleles?" | "No—you have two copies of each gene (one from each parent). The three alleles (I^A, I^B, i) exist in the population, but any one person carries only two." |
| "Does complex inheritance break Mendel's laws?" | "No. Alleles still segregate and are inherited independently. What changes is how they're expressed in the phenotype." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Multi-generation blood-type family puzzle—given grandparents' phenotypes, infer possible genotypes for parents and probability ranges for grandchildren's blood types.
- **Struggling learners:** Use color-coded genotype cards to physically place alleles into Punnett squares. Pre-fill the square framework and have students complete only the interior boxes.
- **ELL support:** Visual reference card with phenotype images next to inheritance pattern names.
