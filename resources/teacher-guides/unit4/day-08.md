# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 8 – Genetic Engineering Applications and Ethics

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Proverbs 3:5-6**
> "Trust in the Lord with all your heart and lean not on your own understanding; in all your ways submit to him, and he will make your paths straight."

Genetic engineering represents one of the most powerful capabilities humans have developed. This verse calls students—and us—to submit even our most impressive knowledge to God's wisdom. Affirm legitimate, life-serving applications (e.g., insulin production, disease treatment) while honestly discussing risks and the need for humility before the complexity God designed.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Describe recombinant DNA, GMO applications, and CRISPR basics with accurate vocabulary.
- Evaluate biotechnology scenarios using evidence and scientific reasoning.
- Communicate claims with clear vocabulary and data support.

**Background for you:** Recombinant DNA simply means combining DNA from different sources. The basic method: cut DNA with restriction enzymes, insert a gene of interest into a vector (like a plasmid), and introduce the recombinant plasmid into a host cell (like bacteria) that then produces the desired protein. Insulin for diabetics has been made this way since the early 1980s—a clear example of life-serving application.

---

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Recombinant DNA technology allowed scientists to produce human insulin in bacteria—a direct use of genetic knowledge to help people with a disease. This is good stewardship."
- "CRISPR offers potential cures for genetic diseases that have caused tremendous suffering. Using it carefully, with strong evidence, reflects care for human life."
- "Germline editing—changes that would be inherited by future generations—raises questions we must approach with particular humility. We are making decisions for people who cannot yet consent."
- "GMO crops that resist drought and provide nutrition in regions facing food insecurity can be genuine acts of stewardship of creation. Evaluate them by evidence, not fear."

## Bell Work Answer Key (8 min)

**Question:** Name one claimed benefit and one potential limitation of gene editing (from prior days).

**Good answers:**

| Benefit | Limitation |
|---------|-----------|
| Correcting disease-causing mutations (e.g., sickle cell) | Off-target edits; unintended changes elsewhere in the genome |
| Producing therapeutic proteins in bacteria (e.g., insulin) | Regulatory and access challenges; who has access to treatments? |
| Pest-resistant crops reducing pesticide use | Potential ecological effects; unknown long-term consequences |
| Gene therapy for inherited disorders | Delivery challenges; immune response risks |

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for one specific benefit and one specific limitation with a supporting reason.

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Key Terms and Applications (20 min)

**Recombinant DNA**
- Definition: DNA that has been formed by combining segments from different organisms or sources.
- Classic example: The human insulin gene is inserted into a bacterium's plasmid. The bacterium then expresses the gene and produces human insulin, which is harvested and purified.

Say:
> "Before recombinant DNA, diabetics required insulin extracted from pig or cow pancreases—limited supply, some allergic reactions. Recombinant DNA gave us an unlimited supply of human insulin. This is molecular biology in service of human health."

**GMOs (Genetically Modified Organisms)**
- Definition: Organisms whose genome has been altered to include genes from another organism or to modify gene expression.
- Examples:
  - Bt corn: contains a bacterial gene for a protein toxic to certain insects—reduces pesticide use.
  - Golden Rice: engineered to produce beta-carotene (Vitamin A precursor); addresses malnutrition.
  - Disease-resistant papaya: saved the Hawaiian papaya industry from a devastating virus.

Say:
> "Some GMOs have strong safety records based on decades of data. Others are newer with less long-term study. The question isn't 'GMO or not'—it's 'what does the evidence show for this specific application?'"

**CRISPR (review/expand from Day 7)**
- New framing: distinguish somatic (body cell) editing vs. germline (reproductive cell) editing.
- Somatic: affects only that patient; cannot be inherited by children.
- Germline: affects the patient and all biological descendants; much higher ethical caution required.

Say:
> "In 2023, the FDA approved the first CRISPR-based treatment for sickle cell disease—editing patients' own blood stem cells. That's somatic editing. Germline editing of human embryos is a different category, with much less consensus and much higher stakes."

### 2. Pros/Cons Debate (25 min)

Divide class into groups. Assign each group one scenario:

**Scenario A:** CRISPR to treat sickle cell disease in a 16-year-old patient (somatic therapy).
**Scenario B:** Engineering bacteria to produce a malaria vaccine protein at low cost for sub-Saharan Africa.
**Scenario C:** Modifying wheat to be drought-resistant for food security in arid regions.
**Scenario D:** Using CRISPR to edit an embryo to remove a gene associated with an inherited disease.

Groups complete debate outline (Scientific support column / Ethical concern column) and select one spokesperson.

**Debrief:** Ask each group for one scientific point and one ethical point. Tie together:

> "Notice that the evidence question and the ethics question are both real—and they're separate. We need both honest data and wise values to make good decisions."

### 3. Virtual Lab: Gene Insertion Simulation (20 min)

Walk through (or have students work through) a gene insertion simulation:

**Steps to identify:**
1. Restriction enzyme cuts the plasmid and the target DNA at matching recognition sequences.
2. Sticky ends on both fragments allow them to join.
3. Ligase seals the combined plasmid (now recombinant).
4. Recombinant plasmid is introduced into a bacterial host cell.
5. Bacteria reproduce; each offspring carries the plasmid and expresses the inserted gene.

**Student recording:** Have students draw and label each step and predict: "If this step fails, what happens?"

**Answer key for failure points:**
- Wrong restriction enzyme → no complementary sticky ends → fragments won't join
- Ligase absent → strands join temporarily but break easily; recombinant DNA unstable
- Plasmid not taken up by bacteria → no production of desired protein
- Insert is backward → protein is mistranslated

### 4. Debrief / Evidence Synthesis (5 min)

Ask: "Which biotechnology application had the strongest data support in today's examples?"

Expected answer: Human insulin production via recombinant DNA—decades of safety data, proven efficacy, global use. Contrast with newer applications still in clinical trial phases.

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** Which biotechnology application had the strongest data support, and why?

**Strong answer:** "Recombinant insulin production has the strongest support—it has been in clinical use since 1982, with extensive safety and efficacy data across millions of patients worldwide."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for naming a specific application with a specific data-based rationale (time in use, trial data, outcome data). Deduct 2 pts for opinion without data tie.

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Debate outline | 10 pts | At least 3 scientific points + 3 ethical points; evidence-based (not just opinion) |
| Review packet – biotechnology section | Completion | All questions answered; vocabulary used correctly |
| Evaluation paragraph (GMO, CRISPR, or recombinant DNA) | 5 pts | Technology identified; benefit and risk cited with evidence; ethical framing appropriate |

**Strong paragraph example:**
> "CRISPR-based therapy for sickle cell disease represents a promising application of genetic engineering. Clinical trials published in 2023 showed that a majority of patients treated with CRISPR-edited stem cells had significant or complete relief from pain crises. However, long-term follow-up data beyond five years is still limited, and the high cost of the treatment raises questions about equitable access. Approaching this technology with both hope and humility—affirming its potential while insisting on continued data collection—reflects responsible stewardship of a powerful tool."

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "GMOs are inherently unsafe" | "Safety varies by application. Bt corn and recombinant insulin have long safety records. Newer applications require more data. 'GMO' is a category, not a verdict." |
| "CRISPR can fix anything" | "CRISPR is precise but limited. It works best on single-gene disorders. Complex diseases involving many genes (like most heart disease or cancer) are far harder targets. Also, delivery to the right cells is still a major challenge." |
| "Recombinant DNA creates a new organism" | "Recombinant DNA modifies a host organism to carry and express a foreign gene. The host organism (e.g., bacteria) is not a new species—it's the same bacteria, now producing a new protein." |
| "Germline editing is the same as somatic editing" | "No. Somatic edits affect only the treated individual. Germline edits affect that person's biological descendants too—which is why they raise much higher ethical concern." |

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Research the He Jiankui case (2018, China)—the first known case of human germline editing. What was done? What was the scientific community's response? What ethical standards were violated? Write a brief evidence-based evaluation.
- **Struggling learners:** Provide a pre-filled debate outline with sentence frames: "One scientific benefit of [technology] is ___, supported by ___. One limitation is ___, because ___."
- **ELL support:** Technology comparison chart with visuals: recombinant DNA process diagram, GMO example photo, CRISPR scissors-and-guide metaphor image.
