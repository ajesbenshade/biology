# Teacher Guide: Unit 4 Day 4 – Mendelian Inheritance

> **For teacher use only.** This guide contains answer keys, lesson scripts, and grading notes not intended for student view.

---

## Scripture Focus

**Genesis 1:21**
> "So God created the great creatures of the sea and every living thing with which the water teems and that moves about in it, according to their kinds, and every winged bird according to its kind."

God's orderly patterns in inheritance reflect His logical, predictable creation. Mendel discovered laws embedded in living things—not invented by him, but uncovered through careful observation and testing.

---

## Teacher Objectives

- Solve monohybrid Punnett square problems accurately.
- Distinguish genotype (allele combination) from phenotype (observable trait).
- Introduce chi-square reasoning for comparing expected vs. observed ratios.

**Background for you:** Gregor Mendel was an Augustinian friar who ran careful, repeated experiments with pea plants. His work was ignored for decades until rediscovered in 1900. His approach—controlled crosses, large sample sizes, ratio analysis—is a model of rigorous scientific observation. The 3:1 phenotype ratio from a monohybrid cross is a mathematical prediction, not a guarantee in every small sample.

---

## Key Biblical Integration Points

- "Mendel's patterns show God built heredity with logic we can discover, test, and steward. The ratios aren't magic—they're mathematical consequences of how alleles segregate."
- "Mendel's success came from precise observation and honest counting. Good science requires the same integrity."

## Bell Work Answer Key (10 min)

**Question:** Predict the outcome from a simple dominant/recessive cross: Tt × Tt

**Full answer:**

|  | T | t |
|--|---|---|
| **T** | TT | Tt |
| **t** | Tt | tt |

- Genotype ratio: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt
- Phenotype ratio: 3 Tall : 1 Short (if T = tall is dominant)

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for completed square + correct ratios. 3 pts if square is correct but ratios are missing.

---

## Lesson Delivery Script (70 min)

### 1. Vocabulary Review (15 min)

| Term | Definition | Quick Example |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| Genotype | The actual allele combination | Tt, TT, tt |
| Phenotype | The visible trait produced | Tall or short |
| Dominant | Allele expressed when present | T (tall) |
| Recessive | Allele expressed only when homozygous recessive | t (short, only if tt) |
| Homozygous | Two identical alleles | TT or tt |
| Heterozygous | Two different alleles | Tt |

Say:
> "Genotype is what's in the DNA. Phenotype is what you see. A tall plant could be TT or Tt—same phenotype, different genotype. That's why these words are not interchangeable."

### 2. Guided Practice: Punnett Square Problems (25 min)

Work through these in order, increasing complexity:

**Problem 1 – Monohybrid, basic:**
- Cross: TT × tt
- Expected: All Tt (100% tall phenotype)

**Problem 2 – Monohybrid, standard:**
- Cross: Tt × Tt
- Expected: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt → 3:1 phenotype ratio

**Problem 3 – Monohybrid, all genotypes named:**
- Cross: Tt × tt
- Expected: 1 Tt : 1 tt → 1:1 phenotype ratio (testcross)

Say before each:
> "Set up the square first. Fill in the parent alleles on top and side. Fill in each box. Then count—genotype ratio first, then convert to phenotype ratio."

### 3. Chi-Square Introduction (15 min)

**Formula:** $\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}$

- O = observed count
- E = expected count based on Mendelian ratios

**Worked example:**
A student flips coins 40 times to simulate Tt × Tt. Results: 32 tails-tails (recessive), 8 heads (dominant). Expected: 30 dominant, 10 recessive (3:1).

| Phenotype | Observed (O) | Expected (E) | (O-E)² / E |
|-----------|------------|------------|------------|
| Dominant | 32 | 30 | (2)²/30 = 0.13 |
| Recessive | 8 | 10 | (2)²/10 = 0.40 |
| **Total χ²** | | | **0.53** |

Say:
> "A chi-square value of 0.53 is small. That means the observed results are close enough to expected that we don't have strong reason to doubt the 3:1 model. A large chi-square means something unexpected is happening."

**Key concept:** Chi-square doesn't prove—it evaluates whether chance variation could explain the discrepancy.

### 4. Chapter 11 Quiz (last 20 min)

Administer the quiz. Collect before students leave.

## Exit Ticket Answer Key

**Question:** When observed ratios differ from expected ratios, what is your next scientific step?

**Strong answer:** "Calculate chi-square to see if the difference is within normal chance variation. If chi-square is large, investigate other factors—maybe the sample was too small, or another genetic factor is involved."

**Grading:** 5 pts. Full credit for chi-square reference. 3 pts for mentioning sample size without chi-square.

## Homework Grading Key

| Assignment | Points | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| 10 monohybrid problems | 10 pts | 1 pt each; Punnett filled correctly + ratios stated |
| Paragraph: genotype → phenotype | 5 pts | Must note that same phenotype can have different genotypes; 3:1 ratio logic explained |

**Strong paragraph example:**
> "Punnett squares predict the possible genotype combinations when two parents cross. For a Tt × Tt cross, the expected genotype ratio is 1:2:1 (TT:Tt:tt). Because T is dominant, both TT and Tt produce the dominant phenotype, giving a 3:1 phenotype ratio. This means that by observing only the phenotype, we cannot always determine the genotype—a tall plant could be TT or Tt."

## Common Confusions & Fixes

| Confusion | Fix / What to Say |
|-----------|------------------|
| "The ratio must be exactly 3:1" | "The ratio is a mathematical prediction for large samples. In small samples, chance variation is normal. That's what chi-square helps us evaluate." |
| "Dominant means more common" | "No—dominant means expressed when one copy is present. A dominant allele can be rare in a population." |
| "Genotype and phenotype mean the same thing" | "Genotype is the allele combination in the DNA. Phenotype is the observable trait. One genotype difference (TT vs. Tt) can produce the same phenotype." |

---

## Differentiation

- **Advanced:** Add incomplete dominance problems where the heterozygote shows a blended phenotype (e.g., red × white = pink). Explain why this doesn't "disprove" Mendel—the alleles are still separate, just both partially expressed.
- **Struggling learners:** Provide a Punnett square setup template with labeled rows/columns and step-by-step prompts. Allow reference to vocabulary chart during problems.
- **ELL support:** Translate key terms (genotype, phenotype, dominant, recessive) and provide visual examples next to each.
