# Unit 5 Study Guide

Use this guide to review Unit 5 by chapter. Focus on evidence, mechanisms, population change, and stewardship. Strong answers in this unit clearly separate direct evidence from broader interpretation.

## Big Ideas to Master

### Chapter 14 — Evidence for Evolution
- Scientists use fossil, anatomical, embryological, molecular, and biogeographical evidence to study historical questions.
- Evidence must be described carefully before it is interpreted.
- Similarity data may support different frameworks depending on the assumptions used.

### Chapter 15 — The Theory of Evolution
- Natural selection changes populations when heritable variation affects survival and reproduction.
- Mutation, gene flow, genetic drift, and sexual selection also affect allele frequencies.
- Population genetics provides measurable evidence of microevolutionary change.
- Hardy-Weinberg is a null model used to compare expected and observed frequencies.

### Chapter 16 — Survival in Changing Environments
- Speciation involves reduced gene flow and reproductive isolation.
- Extinction risk rises when populations are small, isolated, or under strong pressure.
- Human activity affects biodiversity through habitat loss, pollution, overuse, and other pressures.
- Conservation and sustainability can be discussed as forms of stewardship.

## Skills Checklist

Make sure you can:

- distinguish observation from interpretation in a biological case study
- explain natural selection using a real example such as bedbugs, deer mice, or tuskless elephants
- identify whether a claim is strongest as direct evidence, supported inference, or broad extrapolation
- calculate or interpret allele-frequency changes in a simple population dataset
- explain why Hardy-Weinberg is a comparison model rather than a description of every population
- compare allopatric and sympatric speciation clearly
- explain one cause of biodiversity loss and one evidence-based conservation response
- connect scientific reasoning with biblical stewardship without weakening either

## Quick Self-Check Questions
1. What is the difference between microevolution and macroevolution as used in this course?
2. Why is artificial selection useful when explaining natural selection?
3. What is one difference between gene flow and genetic drift?
4. What does a Hardy-Weinberg deviation tell you?
5. Why does reproductive isolation matter in speciation?
6. What is one major human cause of biodiversity loss?

## Practice Prompts
1. Describe one fossil, anatomical, or molecular line of evidence and explain what it directly shows.
2. Explain natural selection using one Unit 5 case study.
3. Define genetic drift and give one example such as bottleneck or founder effect.
4. Compare allopatric and sympatric speciation in 2–3 sentences.
5. Explain why one extinction or conservation claim needs both data and careful interpretation.
6. Discuss the role of stewardship in biodiversity conservation using one scientific example.
