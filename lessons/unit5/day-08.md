# Day 8: Early Ideas, Darwin, and the Modern Synthesis

## Objectives

- Summarize key developments from early evolutionary ideas to modern synthesis.
- Compare artificial selection and natural selection using finch examples.
- Explain what makes natural selection a scientific theory.

## Key Terms

- natural selection
- artificial selection
- modern synthesis
- adaptation
- scientific theory

## Textbook Content

- Chapter 15.1 traces how observations, inheritance, and population genetics formed modern evolutionary theory.
- Darwin’s finches are used to connect environmental pressures with trait frequency changes.
- Artificial selection provides observable analogies for selection mechanisms.

## In-Class Activities / Minilabs

1. **Timeline Build (20 min):** Place major milestones from pre-Darwin to modern synthesis.
2. **Finch Data Lab (30 min):** Analyze beak-depth data before and after environmental change.
3. **Selection Compare (15 min):** Chart differences between artificial and natural selection.

**Sample answers:**
- Artificial selection is intentional human choice; natural selection is differential reproductive success in a given environment.
- A scientific theory explains broad evidence and generates testable predictions.

## Critical Perspective

- Which finch conclusions are strongest because they are based on measurable data?
- How far can beak-change data be extended before claims become speculative?
- What philosophical assumptions appear when discussing long-term origin narratives?
- How should students separate mechanism evidence from worldview interpretation?

## Biblical Integration

Mechanisms observed in populations can be studied with full rigor while Christians affirm that creation is purposeful. Scientific models describe patterns; Scripture anchors identity, purpose, and stewardship.

## Homework / Assignment

- Read Chapter 15.1 and complete guided notes.
- Answer short-response prompt comparing theory, law, and hypothesis.

## Exit Ticket / Reflection

In two sentences, explain why natural selection is a theory and give one data-based example.

## Teacher Notes Only (not for students)

- Keep emphasis on evidence quality and vocabulary precision.
- Encourage students to use “supports” language instead of absolute claims when warranted.
