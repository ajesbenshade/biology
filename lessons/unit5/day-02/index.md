# Day 2: Microevolution, Macroevolution, and Cladistics

## Objectives

- Distinguish observable microevolutionary change from macroevolutionary historical claims.
- Explain the five major lines of evidence introduced in Chapter 14.
- Build and interpret a simple cladogram from trait data.

## Key Terms

- microevolution
- macroevolution
- cladistics
- cladogram
- derived trait

## Textbook Content

- Chapter 14.1 introduces evidence categories used in evolutionary biology: fossil, anatomical, embryological, molecular, and biogeographical.
- Owl and other population examples illustrate short-term observable shifts in trait and allele frequencies.
- Cladistics organizes organisms by shared derived characteristics to model possible relationships.

## In-Class Activities / Minilabs

1. **Evidence Sort (15 min):** Sort 20 evidence cards into the five evidence categories.
2. **Cladogram Build (30 min):** Use a six-organism trait matrix to construct a branching cladogram.
3. **Interpretation Check (15 min):** Mark which conclusions are direct observations and which are inferred history.

**Sample answers:**
- Microevolution: measurable change in trait/allele frequencies inside populations over generations.
- Macroevolution claim: broad historical narratives about deep ancestry patterns.
- Cladogram branch points indicate shared derived traits, not direct proof of ancestry by themselves.

## Critical Perspective

- Which parts of a cladogram are directly data-driven, and which parts are model assumptions?
- How can similarities be interpreted as either common ancestry or common design?
- What evidence would strengthen one interpretation over the other?
- How should scientists communicate uncertainty when presenting historical reconstructions?

## Biblical Integration

Science benefits from clear definitions and honest distinctions between what is measured and what is inferred. Christians can engage evolutionary content rigorously while affirming that creation is purposeful and intelligible.

## Teacher Notes Only (not for students)

- Require students to cite specific trait data in claims.
- Correct imprecise wording that treats all similarity as automatic proof of one historical explanation.
