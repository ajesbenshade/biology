# Day 8: Chapter Opener + Case Study — Don’t Let the Bedbugs Bite!

## Objectives
- Describe how a beneficial mutation can increase in frequency through natural selection.
- Identify evidence in the bedbug case that distinguishes observation from interpretation.
- Explain why pesticide resistance is a strong example of measurable microevolution.

## Key Terms
- mutation
- natural selection
- adaptation
- resistance
- allele frequency

## Textbook Content
- Chapter 15 opener focuses on bedbugs as an example of rapid adaptation under strong selection pressure.
- Case study frames pesticide resistance as a population-level change in trait frequency over time.

**Sample answers (placeholder):**
- A mutation that increases survival under pesticide pressure becomes more common over generations.
- The case demonstrates measurable change within a population, not automatic proof of large-scale origin claims.

## Critical Perspective
- How does this observed resistance example support microevolution, and what would be required to justify macroevolution extrapolation?
- Which conclusions are directly measured in the bedbug data, and which depend on broader assumptions?
- When a mutation is called “positive,” positive relative to what environment and purpose?
- How can we evaluate adaptation evidence rigorously while still considering purposeful design?

## Biblical Integration
Psalm 104:24 reminds us that God made creation with wisdom and order. As students analyze adaptation in populations, they can practice careful science while recognizing God’s sustaining design.
