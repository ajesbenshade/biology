# Day 8: Chapter Opener + Case Study — Don’t Let the Bedbugs Bite!

## Objectives
- Describe how a resistance trait can become more common in a population under strong selection pressure.
- Distinguish directly observed evidence in the bedbug case from broader interpretation.
- Explain why pesticide resistance is a strong example of measurable microevolution.
- Use population-level reasoning rather than individual “trying to adapt” language.

## Key Terms
- mutation
- natural selection
- adaptation
- resistance
- allele frequency

## Textbook Content
- Chapter 15 opens with bedbugs as a clear modern example of rapid adaptation under selection pressure.
- The case study shows how a resistance-related trait can increase in frequency when pesticides consistently remove non-resistant individuals.
- Students should focus on what is directly supported by the case: measurable population change across generations.

![Bedbug close-up](../../../images/unit5/bedbug-opener.jpg)
"Chapter 15 opener: bedbugs as a case study in resistance and population change."

## In-Class Activities / Minilabs
1. **Phenomenon launch (10 min):** Study the opener image and predict why one treatment may stop working over time.
2. **Case-study annotation (20 min):** Mark observations, mechanism claims, and broader extrapolations in the bedbug narrative.
3. **Population simulation (25 min):** Model how a resistance allele can rise in frequency across repeated treatment rounds.
4. **Debrief and evidence check (10 min):** Explain what the simulation demonstrates directly and what it does not prove by itself.

**Sample answers:**
- A resistance-related mutation already present in a population can become much more common when pesticide exposure consistently removes non-resistant individuals.
- The bedbug case strongly supports population-level change under selection pressure, but broader claims about large-scale history require additional evidence beyond this one example.

## Critical Perspective
- What evidence in the bedbug case is directly measured rather than inferred?
- Why is pesticide resistance such a strong example of microevolutionary change?
- What assumptions would be required to move from this case to larger macro-historical claims?
- How does precise scientific language improve both understanding and fairness?

## Biblical Integration
Creation is orderly and knowable, so careful scientific analysis is a form of stewardship. Students can study measurable adaptation in populations while recognizing that God sustains the living world with wisdom.

## Homework / Assignment
- Complete the Chapter 15 opener questions.
- Write two short responses: one observed fact from the bedbug case and one broader claim that would require additional evidence.
- Finish the resistance-frequency graph from class if needed.

## Exit Ticket / Reflection
What specific evidence from today most strongly supports the claim that natural selection can rapidly change allele frequencies in a population?

## Teacher Notes Only (not for students)
- Keep student language population-based: avoid “the bugs got used to it.”
- Reinforce the distinction between observed frequency change and larger historical extrapolation.
