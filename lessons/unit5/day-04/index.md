# Day 4: Radiometric Dating and Forensic Isotope Data

## Objectives

- Explain the basic logic of radioactive decay used in radiometric dating.
- Interpret a 14C/12C graph and identify what the graph can and cannot establish.
- Analyze a nuclear-test-era isotope graph and connect it to dating calibration.

## Key Terms

- isotope
- radioactive decay
- half-life
- radiometric dating
- calibration

## Textbook Content

- Chapter 14.2 introduces radiometric dating as a method based on predictable isotope decay rates.
- Carbon-14 dating is useful for relatively recent once-living material and has practical range limits.
- Forensic-style graphs (14C/12C trends, nuclear testing effects) show why calibration is necessary.

## In-Class Activities / Minilabs

1. **Graph Read (15 min):** Annotate key features on a 14C/12C ratio graph.
2. **Decay Modeling (20 min):** Use manipulatives to simulate half-life reduction.
3. **Forensic Data Questions (25 min):** Use two isotopic graphs to infer likely date ranges.

**Sample answers:**
- If modern atmospheric 14C shifts due to nuclear testing, calibration curves are needed for accurate estimates.
- Carbon-14 is not appropriate for very old rock dating; other isotope systems are used.

## Critical Perspective

- Which parts of radiometric dating are direct measurement, and which require assumptions?
- How does calibration strengthen reliability, and where do uncertainties remain?
- Why is method selection (e.g., carbon-14 vs. other isotopes) crucial for valid conclusions?
- How should students evaluate claims that go beyond what the specific isotope data can support?

## Biblical Integration
