# Day 5: Embryological, Anatomical, and Genetic Evidence

## Objectives

- Compare embryological patterns (including pharyngeal-arch stages) across vertebrates.
- Classify structures as homologous, analogous, or vestigial using evidence.
- Interpret molecular similarity data and identify limits of inference.

## Key Terms

- embryology
- homologous structure
- analogous structure
- vestigial structure
- molecular evidence

## Textbook Content

- Chapter 14.3 discusses developmental and anatomical comparisons across organisms.
- Embryological similarities can indicate shared developmental pathways.
- Molecular comparisons (DNA/protein) provide quantifiable similarity data.
- Tying-It-All-Together includes paleoproteomic approaches for comparing ancient proteins.

## In-Class Activities / Minilabs

1. **Structure Sort (20 min):** Classify ten structure cards as homologous, analogous, or vestigial.
2. **Embryology Comparison (20 min):** Analyze staged embryo diagrams and annotate shared and diverging features.
3. **Molecular Table Analysis (20 min):** Calculate percent similarity from a short protein dataset.

**Sample answers:**
- Bird wing and human arm are homologous (shared structural framework).
- Bird wing and insect wing are analogous in function but structurally distinct.
- Similarity percentages show measurable closeness but do not alone determine complete historical pathway.

## Critical Perspective

- What is the strongest evidence for structural comparison claims in today’s data?
- Which conclusions require extrapolation beyond the observed embryology images?
- How can molecular similarity support both ancestry and design-based interpretations?
- Why is careful classification important before drawing broad conclusions?
- How can common-design and common-descent interpretations be compared fairly using the same molecular dataset?

## Biblical Integration

The repeated patterns in life can be studied scientifically and also appreciated as signs of order and wisdom in creation. Faith invites gratitude, while science trains precision and careful reasoning.

## Exit Ticket / Reflection

Choose one dataset from today and identify one observation, one inference, and one unresolved question.

## Teacher Notes Only (not for students)

- Enforce evidence-first writing: every claim needs cited data.
- Distinguish function similarity from structural similarity explicitly.
