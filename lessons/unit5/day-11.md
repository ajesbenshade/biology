# Day 11: Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

## Objectives

- State all five Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium conditions.
- Use $p + q = 1$ and $p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$ to solve population problems.
- Analyze Nerita versicolor sea-snail data for equilibrium vs. disruption.

## Key Terms

- Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium
- random mating
- large population
- no migration
- no selection

## Textbook Content

- Chapter 15.2 defines genetic equilibrium as a model with strict assumptions.
- Deviations from assumptions indicate forces driving population change.
- Sea-snail examples provide practical interpretation of equilibrium conditions.

## In-Class Activities / Minilabs

1. **Condition Sort (10 min):** Identify valid Hardy-Weinberg assumptions.
2. **Math Practice (30 min):** Solve six allele/genotype frequency problems.
3. **Snail Case Analysis (25 min):** Determine whether sample populations meet equilibrium expectations.

**Sample answers:**
- If $q = 0.3$, then $p = 0.7$, and expected heterozygote frequency is $2pq = 0.42$.
- Evidence of migration or selection violates equilibrium assumptions and can shift frequencies.

## Critical Perspective

- Why is Hardy-Weinberg best understood as a baseline model rather than a full historical explanation?
- Which assumptions are most often violated in real populations?
- How does using a model with explicit assumptions improve scientific honesty?
- What interpretation errors occur when students forget model limits?

## Biblical Integration

Models are useful tools for understanding order in creation, but they are not reality itself. Faith and science both benefit when assumptions are made explicit and conclusions are proportionate to evidence.
